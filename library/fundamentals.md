## 1.1 Chemical and physical properties

### 1.1.1 Introduction to the urea process

#### Chemical reactions

The production of urea proceeds by a two-stage reaction. Ammonia and carbon dioxide react to form ammonium carbamate:

$$2 \text{NH}_3 + \text{CO}_2 \rightleftharpoons \text{NH}_4^+\bar{\text{O}}\text{C}(=\text{O})\text{NH}_2 \quad \Delta H = -117 \text{ kJ/mol}$$

This strongly exothermic reaction reaches an equilibrium very rapidly. The reaction system shown above will hereinafter be referred to the carbamate equilibrium.

In the liquid phase, ammonium carbamate is next dehydrated to urea and water:

$$\text{NH}_4^+\bar{\text{O}}\text{C}(=\text{O})\text{NH}_2 \rightleftharpoons \text{NH}_2\text{C}(=\text{O})\text{NH}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \quad \Delta H = +15.5 \text{ kJ/mol}$$

This endothermic equilibrium reaction is rather slow compared with the first one; the system will hereinafter be called the urea equilibrium.

The above summary data on equilibria and kinetics enable the essence of the lay-out of the urea plant to be derived. Ammonia and carbon dioxide have to be contacted in a device capable of removing large quantities of reaction heat: the HP carbamate condenser.

From this HP carbamate condenser the mixture is sent to the reactor, where the second equilibrium is established.

From the fact that the dehydration reaction does not proceed to completion it follows that the unconverted reactants must be removed from the reactor solution. The way in which this is done characterises most urea processes.

The process pressures and temperatures are dictated, as well as the compositions, by the phase behaviour of the four-component mixture (ammonia, carbon dioxide, water and urea), by the inert percentage, and by the desired utility consumption or the steam production of the plant.

#### The Stamicarbon total recycle $\text{CO}_2$ stripping process

In the Stamicarbon total recycle $\text{CO}_2$ stripping process almost the whole quantity of unconverted reactants is returned to the reactor. A large proportion of the reactants is removed from the reactor solution at the synthesis pressure by contacting this liquid countercurrently with carbon dioxide.

By stripping ammonia from the liquid, the carbamate equilibrium is forced to the left, which results in dissociation of the carbamate not converted into urea. The reaction heat needed is supplied by external heating of the stripper (HP heat exchanger) tubes.

Owing to the short residence time in the stripper (HP heat exchanger) and the relatively low temperature, the urea equilibrium is prevented from establishing, so that the hydrolysis of urea does not get out of bounds.

The stripped reactor solution is flashed off to a much lower pressure (approx. 4.0 bar) and then subjected to distillative separation to remove residual ammonia and carbon dioxide. After this, these reactants are pumped back, dissolved in water, to the synthesis section.

### 1.1.1.1 Phase behaviour in the urea process system

To obtain a good understanding of the process, it is essential to know the phase behaviour of mixture containing ammonia, carbon dioxide, urea and water. The phase behaviour of such a quaternary system is far from ideal and rather difficult to understand.

We shall here try to explain the behaviour of mixtures from the properties of their components by application of basic principles from phase theory.

You can classify the four components mentioned above into three groups:

1. the light components, ammonia and carbon dioxide, which in mixtures occur in the liquid phase only at high pressures and/or low temperatures;
2. the medium-weight component, water, which occurs both in the liquid and in the gas phase;
3. the heavy component, urea, which occurs in the gas phase only at very low pressures and/or high temperatures.

The phase transitions are schematically represented in equation 1.

| $\text{NH}_3 + \text{CO}_2$ | $\text{H}_2\text{O}$ | gas phase |
|---|---|---|
| $\text{NH}_3 + \text{CO}_2$ | $\text{NH}_4\text{OCONH}_2$ | $\text{NH}_2\text{CONH}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O}$ | liquid phase |

*equation 1: Representation of the reaction system and the phase transitions*

The phase behaviour of this four-component system is largely determined by the behaviour of the binary system ammonia-carbon dioxide.

#### Ammonia and carbon dioxide

Therefore, the simplest description of the behaviour of the four-component mixture is obtained by considering the behaviour of this binary system and then to add the properties of water and urea.

According to the first reaction equation, ammonia and carbon dioxide react to form the salt, ammonium carbamate. For the ammonia-carbon dioxide system this has two important consequences:

1. The reaction of two light components results in the formation of the heavy component ammonium carbamate. The pressure of the mixture will therefore be lower than the pressure of the individual components.
2. The strong interaction between ammonia and carbon dioxide results in azeotropy.

The fact that the dissociation pressure of the ammonium carbamate remains relatively low, also at higher temperatures, has the surprising effect that ammonium carbamate may be present in liquid form while the components constituting it are supercritical. In combination with the azeotropy, this leads to the situation illustrated in fig. 1.

This figure describes the gas-liquid equilibrium of the system at a constant pressure and temperatures at which ammonium carbamate does not occur in the solid state. When a gas mixture having the composition $X_{\text{az}}$ is cooled, it will condense at temperature $T_{\text{az}}$, the azeotropic condensation temperature. During the condensation the temperature remains constant: the mixture behaves like an unary system.

When a gas mixture with a different composition is cooled, it will behave as a binary mixture and show a condensation range, not a point. The beginning and the end of this range will lie at temperatures lower than the azeotropic condensation temperature. The boiling range starts at the lowest temperature at which gas bubbles are formed. Therefore, this temperature is called the bubble point. The start of the condensation range is the highest temperature at which liquid is still present, and is therefore called the dew point.

#### Ammonia, carbon dioxide and water

As said before, water is a medium-weight component compared with ammonia and carbon dioxide. However, these two components differ widely in their behaviour with respect to water.

Ammonia is very readily soluble in water, carbon dioxide only very poorly. This means that a liquid phase can only contain carbon dioxide in the bound form, e.g. as ammonium carbamate or, possibly, as ammonium carbonate.

The presence of water makes the azeotropy disappear, but a maximum dew point and a bubble point remain. The location of these points is determined by the water fraction: as water is a medium-weight component, the maxima will be at a higher temperature according to the greater water fraction.

The relation between the composition and the maximum temperatures of dew and bubble points is given by the so-called top-ridge lines. The phase diagram for a pressure, which occurs in the synthesis section, is shown schematically in fig. 2.

This figure also shows the projections of the top-ridge liquid line and of a number of liquid isotherms. A liquid isotherm represents the compositions of mixtures which at the isotherm temperatures are exactly at their bubble points. The isotherms are shown in more detail in fig. 3.

It is seen that on the carbon-dioxide side of the top-ridge liquid line the liquid isotherms are much closer together than on the ammonia side. If there is an excess of ammonia or carbon dioxide with respect to the top-ridge composition, the pressure in the mixture can be maintained by applying, for equal disturbances, a lower temperature if the excess is on the carbon-dioxide side. In other words, carbon dioxide is less soluble in the mixture than ammonia.

#### Ammonia, carbon dioxide, water and urea

Urea is the heaviest of the four components. This means that, like water, in a liquid phase urea can act as solvent. As the urea fraction becomes greater, the vapour pressure of the mixture will become lower, assuming the temperature to remain constant.

The affinity between ammonia and urea is lower than that existing between ammonia and water, however.

The graphical representation of the phase behaviour of a quaternary mixture is very difficult. Therefore, use is mostly made of a quasi-ternary representation: urea and water, occurring in a given ratio, are taken to constitute one component. The result is that one can consider a system having only three components: ammonia, carbon dioxide and water/urea.

If no extra water is pumped to the synthesis section, the water/urea ratio would, in accordance with the urea equilibrium equation, be equal to unity. Fig. 3 gives the projection of the quasi-ternary equilibrium at constant pressure of this situation.

The figure further shows the course of the isobar at chemical equilibrium, representing the compositions and temperatures at a given pressure, with the mixture being in chemical equilibrium. On the intersection of this isobar and the top-ridge liquid line lies the equilibrium composition at which the temperature of the liquid mixture is maximum at this pressure. Because the formation reaction of urea is endothermic, the conversion into urea will under these conditions be maximum, too.

For this equilibrium composition to be reached, a mixture has to be used that has a composition lying on the line representing the reaction path to the point of intersection mentioned. From the gross reaction equation of the formation of urea from ammonia and carbon dioxide and the molar weight of these substances it follows that the use of a mixture containing 56.4% by wt. carbon dioxide and 43.6% by wt. ammonia will lead exactly to the water/urea corner point. All reaction paths are parallel to this line.

### 1.1.1.2 The chemical-technological realization of the synthesis process

The chemical-technological realization of the synthesis is determined by the physical and chemical equilibria in the system constituted by ammonia, carbon dioxide, water and urea.

The Stamicarbon total-recycle-carbon-dioxide-stripping-process opts for a synthesis section consisting of four items, viz.

1. HP CARBAMATE CONDENSER,
2. REACTOR,
3. STRIPPER (HP HEAT EXCHANGER), AND
4. HP SCRUBBER.

The first three items together form what is called the synthesis loop. Throughout the synthesis section the same pressure prevails.

Ammonia, carbon dioxide containing stripped reactants, and carbamate recycled from the HP stage are supplied to the HP carbamate condenser. The composition of the mixture at the prevailing pressure is not far from the top-ridge line. The heat of condensation released in the reaction is used for the generation of LP steam. The degree of condensation in the HP carbamate condenser can be controlled by means of the steam pressure.

From the HP carbamate condenser the mixture flows to the reactor. The residence time in the reactor is such that there is enough time to reach the urea equilibrium almost completely.

The composition in the synthesis loop is such that the product leaving the reactor has the highest temperature possible at the system pressure. This is achieved if the end of the reaction path is on the top-ridge liquid line. At constant pressure, the temperature can then increase across the reactor.

In order to raise the temperature of the mixture and to provide the heat of reaction for the urea formation, heat has to be supplied to the reactor. This heat is produced by condensation of ammonia and carbon dioxide.

In order that the reaction path may be followed at the higher possible temperature throughout, it is necessary to establish a plug flow. In addition, there should be intensive contact between the gas and the liquid phase. It is necessary that there constantly be physical equilibrium between the phases to keep the temperature as high as possible.

The stripper (HP heat exchanger) is designed as a counter-current film evaporator: the liquid flows down along the tube wall in a film, and carbon dioxide introduced at the bottom entrains carbamate that has dissociated into ammonia and carbon dioxide.

The heat required for the decomposition of carbamate is supplied by condensation of steam.

The raw materials for the synthesis, carbon dioxide and ammonia, contain inerts. Besides, air is added to the carbon dioxide, with a view to protection against corrosion and combustion of hydrogen. This means that there will have to be a constant vent from the synthesis loop to prevent inert accumulating.

In order to recover ammonia and carbon dioxide from this vent flow, the gas is at first washed in the high-pressure scrubber with the carbamate recycle from the low-pressure stage and secondly washed with process condensate in the LP absorber. The solution formed in the HP scrubber is pumped to the high-pressure carbamate condenser by means of a liquid-liquid ejector.

#### THE HP CARBAMATE CONDENSER

In the HP carbamate condenser ammonia and carbon dioxide are condensed and ammonium carbamate is formed:

$$2 \text{NH}_3 + \text{CO}_2 \rightleftharpoons \text{NH}_4\text{OCONH}_2 \quad \Delta H = -117 \text{ kJ/mol}$$

The molar ratio between ammonia and carbon dioxide corresponding to the maximum dew point at the system pressure is about 2.5. However, in the HP carbamate condenser this molar ratio is about 3.0, which implies that at this point in the process (see fig. 2) there is a slight excess of $\text{NH}_3$ present relevant to the top ridge composition.

Incomplete condensation is required for the HP carbamate condenser because a low temperature at the reactor inlet is undesirable. The partial condensation in the HP carbamate condenser has the following consequences:

* the temperature of the mixture at the reactor inlet is relatively high;
* the non-condensed gases ensure the supply of sufficient heat in the reactor to make the urea-formation reaction proceed and in addition raise the temperature;
* the fraction of the total gas supply that does not condense in the HP carbamate condenser nor in the reactor ensures that the inert percentage in the top of the reactor will be such that the partial vapour pressures of ammonia and carbon dioxide are sufficiently high.

This quantity is sufficient to bring the reactor outlet temperature to about $183^{\circ}\text{C}$. The heat is released at such a temperature level that it is suitable for generation of 4.4 bar steam without an outsize HP carbamate condenser having to be employed.

#### THE OVERALL PROCESS. PHYSICAL AND CHEMICAL EQUILIBRIA.

In the reactor, liquid-phase ammonium carbamate is converted into urea and water. This conversion is an endothermic equilibrium reaction:

$$2 \text{NH}_3 + \text{CO}_2 \rightleftharpoons \text{NH}_4^+\bar{\text{O}}\text{C}(=\text{O})\text{NH}_2 \quad \Delta H = -117 \text{ kJ/mol}$$
$$\text{NH}_4^+\bar{\text{O}}\text{C}(=\text{O})\text{NH}_2 \rightleftharpoons \text{NH}_2\text{C}(=\text{O})\text{NH}_2 + \text{H}_2\text{O} \quad \Delta H = +15.5 \text{ kJ/mol}$$

In the first reaction, carbon dioxide and ammonia are converted to ammonium carbamate; the reaction is fast and exothermic. In the second reaction, which is slow and endothermic, ammonium carbamate dehydrates to produce urea and water. Since more heat is produced in the first reaction than consumed in the second, the overall reaction is exothermic.

Processes differ mainly in conditions (composition, temperatures and pressure) at which these reactions are carried out. Traditionally, the composition of the liquid phase in the reaction zone is expressed by two molar ratios:

* $\text{NH}_3/\text{CO}_2$ and
* $\text{H}_2\text{O}/\text{CO}_2$

Both reflect the composition of the so-called initial mixture, (e.g. the hypothetical mixture consisting only of $\text{NH}_3$, $\text{CO}_2$ and $\text{H}_2\text{O}$, if both reactions are shifted completely to the left).

The achievable conversion per pass, dictated by the chemical equilibrium, as a function of temperature goes through a maximum (see figures 4 and 5). This effect is usually attributed to the fact that the ammonium carbamate concentration as a function of temperature goes through a maximum. This maximum in the ammonium carbamate concentration can be explained, at least qualitatively, by the respective heat effects of the carbamate and urea reactions. This mechanism cannot however explain the observed conversion maximum fully and quantitatively; other contributing mechanisms have been suggested.

The influence of the composition of the initial mixture on the chemical equilibrium can be explained qualitatively by the carbamate and urea reactions and the law of mass action:

* Increasing the $\text{NH}_3/\text{CO}_2$ ratio (increasing the $\text{NH}_3$ concentration) increases $\text{CO}_2$ conversion, but reduces $\text{NH}_3$ conversion. See figures 6 and 7.
* Increasing the amount of water in the initial mixture (increasing the $\text{H}_2\text{O}/\text{CO}_2$ ratio) results in a decrease in both $\text{CO}_2$ and $\text{NH}_3$ conversion (see figures 8 and 9).

Also in these cases a full quantitative description cannot be derived simply from the law of mass action and the carbamate and urea reactions. Other, not yet fully understood reaction mechanisms probably contribute to the chemical equilibria to a minor extent.

In figures 5, 6 and 7, the conversion at chemical equilibrium is expressed as $\text{CO}_2$ conversion, that is, the amount of $\text{CO}_2$ in the initial mixture converted into urea (plus biuret), if no changes occur in the overall $\text{NH}_3$, $\text{CO}_2$ and $\text{H}_2\text{O}$ concentrations in the liquid phase.

This way of representing the chemical equilibrium is consistent with the presentation usually found in the traditional urea literature. However, it is based on the arbitrary choice of $\text{CO}_2$ as the key component. Historically, this may be justified by the fact that early in urea processes, $\text{CO}_2$ conversion was more important than $\text{NH}_3$ conversion. For the present generation of stripping processes, however, giving a higher weight to $\text{CO}_2$ conversion is not justified.

Comparing, e.g. figures 7 and 8, shows that an arbitrary choice of one of the two feed stock components as yardsticks to evaluate optimum reaction conversion can easily lead to faulty conclusions.

Ultimately, project economics (investment and consumptions) will dictate the choice of process parameters in the reaction section. Without going into such time and place-dependent economic considerations, one can argue that the urea yield (i.e. the concentration of urea in the liquid phase) is a better tool for judging optimum process parameters than $\text{CO}_2$ or $\text{NH}_3$ conversion.

Figure 10 illustrates that urea yield as function of temperature also goes through a maximum: the location of this maximum is of course composition dependent. Figure 11 again shows the detrimental effect of excess water on urea yield; thus, one of the targets in designing a recycle system must be to minimize water recycle.

Figure 12 shows that urea yield as function of $\text{NH}_3/\text{CO}_2$ ratio reaches a maximum somewhat above the stoichiometric ratio ($2/1$). This is one of the reasons that all commercial processes operate at $\text{NH}_3/\text{CO}_2$ ratios above the stoichiometric ratio. Another important reason for this can be found from the physical phase equilibria in the $\text{NH}_3$-$\text{CO}_2$-$\text{H}_2\text{O}$-urea system.

The equation for the net of the urea formation shows that this net rate decreases as the carbamate concentration becomes lower and the urea and water concentrations become higher. The closer the chemical equilibrium is approached, the lower the reaction rate will be.

Therefore, the reactor installed is given such a volume (residence time) as ensures that equilibrium conversion will be attained to 95% by wt. It is then said that the path covered is 95% by wt. Residence time in the reactor is about one hour. A greater path covered than this 95% by wt. lowers the cost of separation, but calls for a disproportional large reactor volume.

The reaction heat required for the endothermic urea formation is produced by condensation of ammonia and carbon dioxide. In the HP carbamate condenser about 72 wt.% of the HP stripper gas supplied is condensed. The percentage of condensed reactants in the reactor outlet is as high as about 90% by wt. Condensation of ammonia and carbon dioxide causes the volumetric percentage of inert to rise.

A higher inert pressure at constant overall pressure means a lower system pressure, so temperature will be higher if less inert is present. A rise in temperature will cause an increase in pressure. A temperature increase has a favourable influence on the position of the urea equilibrium and the rates of reaction. However, a rise in pressure results in a reduced stripping efficiency.

Thus the choice of reactor temperature (or the corresponding reactor pressure) is an optimization between high reactor conversion on the one hand and high stripping efficiency on the other.

#### THE STRIPPER (HP HEAT EXCHANGER)

As the urea formation reaction does not proceed to completion, non-converted ammonia, carbon dioxide and ammonium carbamate must be removed from the reactor solution. These reactants are for the major part recovered at synthesis pressure in the HP stripper and recycled to the HP carbamate condenser.

The removal of ammonium carbamate should be rapid, and be effected at not too high a temperature. The urea equilibrium will then not get time to establish, which would result in hydrolysis of urea.

In the method developed by Stamicarbon the reactor solution is countercurrently stripped with carbon dioxide. This lowers the ammonia partial pressure in the gas phase and will cause ammonia to evaporate from the liquid phase.

Owing to the lower ammonia concentration in the liquid phase, ammonium carbamate dissociates to ammonia and carbon dioxide, as appears from the carbamate equilibrium equation. The heat of dissociation is supplied by steam condensing on the outside of the stripper tubes. This keeps the liquid phase at boiling temperature. The stripping mechanism can qualitatively explained as follows.

See figures 13 and 14.

Heating of the tubes ensures that the stripping in a first approximation is an isothermic process (constant temperature). The composition of the liquid feed to the HP stripper is represented by point $L_1$. This is a composition close to the chemical equilibrium isobar.

The feed gas to the HP stripper consists of pure $\text{CO}_2$ plus some inert, represented by point $G_1$. The gross composition of gas plus liquid feed to the HP stripper is now represented by C on the line connecting $G_1$ and $L_1$.

From the law of conservation of matter, it follows that the composition of the liquid + gas leaving the HP stripper now also must be on a line through point C. Assuming, the process to be isothermal (which, for a first approximation is true), a possible composition for the liquid leaving the HP stripper would be $L_2$. A gas phase in equilibrium with $L_2$ is represented by point $G_2$.

As the actual gas phase in the HP stripper bottom is represented by point $G_1$ it follows that there is a driving force for removal of $\text{NH}_3$ from the liquid phase $L_2$.

As this reasoning is true for all liquid compositions on the isotherms, it follows that in theory any liquid composition on this isotherm could be reached as solution outlet HP stripper. From figure 14, it therefore follows that very low $\text{NH}_3$ and $\text{CO}_2$ compositions can be reached in the HP stripper.

In practice, the $\text{NH}_3$ and $\text{CO}_2$ concentration in the HP stripper are limited by the size of the HP stripper. Moreover, especially in the bottom part of the tubes, the process is not isotherm anymore. Instead the relative cool $\text{CO}_2$ introduced in the bottom takes care for some cooling of the liquid falling down along the tube walls. This cooling effect in the bottom predominates the heating by steam on the shell side. As net result, the liquid leaving the HP stripper is appreciable cooler as compared with the liquid introduced despite the heating by steam.

Further increase of the size of the HP stripper to realize even higher stripping efficiency would not only economically not be justified but also the occurrence of two side reactions prohibits a lower stripping efficiency.

In the bottom part of the HP stripper tubes conditions are highly favourable for hydrolysis and biuret formation. The temperature is high and carbamate and ammonia concentrations are low. To reduce these reactions, carbon dioxide enters the bottom part of the HP stripper with a maximum temperature of $160^{\circ}\text{C}$. By maintaining a stationary condensate level in the HP stripper bottom, the transfer of heat to the liquid is blocked partially. In this part of the HP stripper, the stripping process is almost adiabatic. Finally, the level of the stripped reactor solution is controlled so that the residence time is minimum here.
