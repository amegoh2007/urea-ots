# **Comprehensive Engineering Analysis of the Atmospheric Absorber (323C005) and Vent Stack (328V001) in Stamicarbon Urea Synthesis**

## **Executive Overview and Process Context**

In the modern Stamicarbon total-recycle carbon dioxide stripping urea production process, maximizing the conversion of ammonia and carbon dioxide into urea while stringently minimizing environmental emissions constitutes a primary engineering objective. The synthesis loop, operating at elevated pressures of approximately 140 bar and temperatures exceeding 180 °C, inherently generates non-condensable inert gases and trace off-gases. These gases must be continuously purged from the system to maintain thermodynamic equilibrium and prevent the accumulation of inerts, which would otherwise lower the partial pressures of the primary reactants and severely degrade the overall conversion efficiency. The atmospheric absorption and venting system, principally comprising the Atmospheric Absorber (323C005) and the Vent Stack (328V001), serves as the ultimate mass transfer and fluid dynamic barrier before process gases are safely discharged into the environment.  
This comprehensive research report provides an exhaustive, expert-level process and instrumentation analysis of the 323C005 and 328V001 units. By meticulously synthesizing original equipment manufacturer (OEM) datasheets, Process Flow Diagrams (PFDs), Process and Instrumentation Diagrams (P\&IDs), and operational manuals, this document maps the complex process flow streams entering and exiting these vessels. Furthermore, it delineates the mechanical and thermodynamic design parameters, dissects the associated instrumentation and control loops, and establishes a rigorous physical chemistry framework essential for the high-fidelity modeling of the multiphase transport phenomena occurring within these critical assets. The insights generated herein are designed to facilitate advanced process simulation, debottlenecking, and operational optimization.

## **Process Flow Streams: Routing, Mapping, and Characterization**

The fluid dynamics surrounding the Atmospheric Absorber and the Vent Stack are defined by the convergence of vapor reliefs and condensate drains originating from the low-pressure (LP), evaporation, and desorption sections of the urea plant. Tracking these streams requires a holistic understanding of the plant's pressure cascading strategy.

### **OEM Stream Numbers and System Routing**

Based on the Process Flow Diagrams for the Evaporation (PFD No. 21\) and Desorption (PFD No. 22\) sections of the 1750/2000 Metric Tons Per Day (MTPD) capacity plant, a highly integrated network of streams surrounds these units. The specific hydrodynamic flow path interfacing with the 323C005 and 328V001 units is structured through a precise sequence of unit operations:

1. **Vapor Feed Sourcing:** Non-condensable gases, unreacted ammonia ($NH\_3$), and carbon dioxide ($CO\_2$) are collected from multiple low-pressure and vacuum nodes. Specifically, vapors evaporated from the pre-evaporator and the first-stage evaporator (324E001) that are not condensed in the primary condensers (324E002) are withdrawn by ejectors (e.g., 324F002) and discharged into the Atmospheric Absorber. Furthermore, non-condensed gases from the flash tank condenser (323E011) and the level tank (323D011) are routed to the Atmospheric Absorber under the strict regulation of pressure controller PIC323203, which maintains the upstream flash tank system at approximately 1.13 bar.  
2. **Liquid Feed Sourcing:** The primary absorbing medium utilized in the 323C005 vessel is process water originating from the LP Absorber (322C001). This process water, which has already been utilized to scrub high-pressure inert vents, retains a significant residual capacity to absorb ammonia and carbon dioxide. It is pumped or gravity-fed to the spray nozzles at the top of the Atmospheric Absorber.  
3. **Absorber Discharge and Transfer:** Within the Atmospheric Absorber (323C005), the upward-flowing vapor and downward-flowing liquid engage in counter-current mass transfer. The resulting rich absorption solution (ammonia water) exits the bottom of the absorber at a temperature of approximately 55 °C. This liquid is discharged directly to the Vent Stack (328V001). Simultaneously, the scrubbed off-gases exit the top of the absorber and are also routed into the Vent Stack.  
4. **Vent Stack Operations:** The Vent Stack (328V001) functions concurrently as a final knockout drum and an environmental discharge conduit. The liquid phase pools at the base of the stack and overflows to the Ammonia Water Tank (328D003), forming a vital hydrostatic seal. The cleaned, inert gases ascend the 914 mm diameter stack and are discharged directly to the atmosphere.

### **Internal Configuration and Mass Transfer Mechanisms**

The internal architecture of the 323C005 Absorber is intricately engineered to facilitate highly efficient gas-liquid contact across a minimized vertical footprint.  
The vessel achieves its separation duty through one theoretical tray, which is physically realized by a 1500 mm deep packed bed of metallic Pall Rings. The selection of 25 mm x 25 mm x 1 mm Pall rings is a sophisticated chemical engineering decision. Pall rings, which feature slotted walls and internal stamped tongues, offer a drastically reduced pressure drop and a higher flooding limit compared to traditional Raschig rings, while maintaining a remarkably high specific surface area. The 1 mm material thickness provides the necessary structural rigidity against the hydrodynamic load of the down-flowing liquid while maximizing the interstitial void fraction, thereby reducing the resistance to vapor flow.  
Liquid distribution is equally critical to the column's efficiency. The process water is introduced via a high-efficiency full-cone spray nozzle situated in the vapor space above the packed bed. This spray nozzle is rated for a hydraulic capacity of 45 m³/h and requires a pressure drop of 1 bar to achieve proper atomization. This significant atomization energy breaks the bulk liquid into a fine mist of droplets, drastically increasing the initial interfacial surface area for mass transfer before the fluid even contacts the Pall ring bed.  
The packing material is structurally supported by a perforated grid that boasts a free area equal to 95% of the column's cross-sectional area. This exceptionally high open area prevents premature liquid hold-up and localized flooding at the support plate. The grid is engineered to withstand the static weight of the stainless steel packing plus the dynamic mass of the liquid hold-up, which the OEM calculates at approximately 20% of the packing's total volume during steady-state operation.

### **Material Science and Fabrication Codes**

Corrosion is an ever-present and critical threat in urea production, particularly in zones where ammonium carbamate can form, condense, or concentrate. The Stamicarbon Material Specification 18005 dictates strict metallurgical requirements to ensure the operational longevity of the equipment.  
The wetted process-side components of the Atmospheric Absorber, including the upper and lower shell sections, the dished heads, internal clips, the spray nozzle, the support grid, and the nozzle pipes, are exclusively fabricated from material grade BB.01. In the Stamicarbon nomenclature, BB.01 corresponds to DIN 1.4306 or 1.4541, which are the European equivalents to AISI 304L and AISI 321 austenitic stainless steels, respectively. The low carbon content (typically \< 0.03%) of the 304L grade mitigates the precipitation of chromium carbides at the grain boundaries during welding. This is a crucial defense mechanism against intergranular corrosion, which rapidly degrades standard stainless steels in ammonium carbamate service.  
Furthermore, fabrication protocols dictate that all welds in contact with the process fluid must exhibit full penetration. This eliminates micro-crevices and incomplete fusion zones where stagnant corrosive fluids could accumulate and initiate pitting or crevice corrosion. The vessel is designed according to the rigorous AD-Merkblätter pressure vessel code, utilizing a joint efficiency of 0.85. To optimize capital expenditure without sacrificing safety, non-wetted structural components such as the lower skirt and lifting lugs are fabricated from carbon steel (CS). A 3 mm corrosion allowance is applied to all carbon steel parts to account for atmospheric degradation over the plant's lifecycle. Where carbon steel must interface with the process, such as the main shell flange, a minimum 4 mm lining of BB.01 material is applied post-machining to protect the base metal.

### **Fluid Dynamics and Internal Configuration**

The 328V001 is a vertically oriented cylindrical vessel conspicuously devoid of complex mass transfer internals, trays, or packing. Its operational efficacy is driven by fundamental fluid mechanics and gravitational phase separation.  
As the scrubbed gases enter the stack from the Atmospheric Absorber via the N3 nozzle, they experience an immediate and massive expansion in the cross-sectional flow area as they transition from standard process piping into the 914 mm OD stack shell. This sudden area expansion causes a proportional plunge in the superficial vapor velocity. According to Stokes' Law and the Souders-Brown equation, when the upward vapor velocity drops below the terminal settling velocity of the entrained liquid droplets, gravity overcomes the aerodynamic drag forces. Consequently, the liquid droplets fall out of suspension, allowing the stack to function as a highly effective knockout drum. The demisted gas then proceeds upward to the massive 900 mm connection (N5) for unrestricted discharge to the atmosphere.  
Simultaneously, the liquid phase—originating from the 323C005 N4 outlet—enters the stack via the N2 nozzle and pools in the base of the vessel. As the liquid level rises, it eventually reaches the elevation of the N1 overflow nozzle. At this precise geometric point, the fluid gravity-drains continuously into the Ammonia Water Tank (328D003). This overflow mechanism acts as a permanent, fail-safe hydrostatic liquid seal. The weight of the liquid column perfectly counterbalances the slight positive pressure of the upstream processes, preventing process gases from bypassing the stack and dangerously pressurizing the downstream liquid storage tanks.

### **Material Science and Fabrication Codes**

Given its role in handling aqueous ammonia, process condensate, and trace urea at elevated temperatures (up to 100 °C design), the 328V001 is subject to the same rigorous metallurgical standards as the upstream absorber.  
The entire shell, the end heads, the nozzle pipes, and any minor wetted internal baffles are fabricated from 1.4306 / TP304L austenitic stainless steel, strictly conforming to the Stamicarbon Material Specification 18005 (BB.01). To ensure traceability and metallurgical integrity, material certificates compliant with the EN10204 3.1B standard are mandatory for all pressurized components.  
Structural supports, exterior clips, and the lifting lugs utilized for the erection of the 4-meter tall stack are fabricated from Carbon Steel (CS). However, welding carbon steel directly to a thin-walled stainless steel pressure vessel risks carbon migration, galvanic corrosion, and the introduction of severe thermal stresses due to differing coefficients of thermal expansion. Therefore, the engineering specification mandates that these carbon steel appurtenances must either be welded to intermediate reinforcing pads made of 1.4306/TP304L stainless steel, or the appurtenances themselves must be fabricated entirely from the upgraded stainless steel alloy.

## **Instrumentation, Piping, and Process Control Strategy**

Stable, autonomous operation of the atmospheric absorption and venting system relies on a resilient instrumentation architecture designed to manage fluctuating liquid inventories, respond to upstream process upsets, and prevent environmental excursions.

### **Level Indication and Control Architecture (323C005)**

Analysis of the Process and Instrumentation Diagrams (P\&IDs), specifically Document PID\_No\_\_103\_2\_Recirculation\_2.pdf, reveals the presence of critical level monitoring instrumentation physically associated with the Atmospheric Absorber.  
The primary instrument tag **3230011 LEVEL T** is explicitly linked to the 323C005 vessel. In the context of urea and ammonia water services, level measurement presents unique challenges due to the propensity of ammonium carbamate to crystallize rapidly at lower temperatures or in stagnant zones. Therefore, traditional differential pressure (DP) transmitters utilizing standard impulse tubing are prone to catastrophic plugging. To circumvent this, the level is measured using DP transmitters equipped with flush-mounted, flanged diaphragm seals. These seals sit perfectly flush with the inner wall of the vessel, eliminating dead legs. Furthermore, to provide an additional layer of security against crystallization, the instrument flanges and the immediate surrounding vessel wall are often steam-traced.  
The measurement taps for LT 3230011 are located at the bottom sump of the 323C005 vessel, physically situated below the gas inlet nozzles (N1 and N3). The lower tap is positioned near the bottom tangent line, while the upper reference tap is located in the vapor space just below the packing support grid.  
This Level Transmitter (LT) generates a continuous 4-20 mA signal corresponding to the hydrostatic head of the liquid in the sump, which is relayed to the plant's Distributed Control System (DCS). While this signal triggers vital high-level and low-level control room alarms, it does not typically actuate a direct flow control valve on the water outlet line (N4). Because the fluid gravity-drains directly into the Vent Stack (328V001), which utilizes a passive gravity overflow (N1) to establish the master hydraulic baseline, the LT on 323C005 is primarily utilized as a monitoring safeguard. It ensures the static liquid head is maintained, confirming that the liquid seal is intact without rising to a level that would flood the gas inlets and choke the column.

### **Condensate Routing and Upstream Integration**

The P\&ID explicitly groups the tag **3236011 CONDEIDO** directly with the level transmitter. This designation tracks the routing of the process condensate—specifically the feed water originating from the LP Absorber—that enters the 323C005 via the N2 nozzle and is forced through the 45 m³/h full-cone spray nozzle. The stability of this condensate flow is the primary independent variable that operators can manipulate to dictate the mass transfer efficiency within the column.  
Upstream integration is further governed by the pressure controller **PIC323203**. As detailed in the operational manual, the non-condensed gases from the flash tank condenser (323E011) and its associated level tank (323D011) are vented to the Atmospheric Absorber. PIC323203 actuates a control valve in this vent line to maintain the flash tank system at a strict operating pressure of 1.13 bar. Because the expansion of these gases across the control valve causes a sharp temperature drop due to the Joule-Thomson effect, solid carbamate deposits can rapidly form, choking the valve. To neutralize this threat, a continuous flow of 9 bar live steam is injected directly downstream of the PV323203 valve, ensuring the piping to the 323C005 remains clear of obstructions.

### **Environmental Monitoring of the Vent Stack**

While continuous emission monitoring systems (CEMS) are not explicitly detailed in the provided structural datasheets, the Vent Stack (328V001) represents the plant's final, heavily scrutinized environmental emission point. Modern Stamicarbon plant design principles dictate that the ammonia concentration in the off-gas discharged from the 900 mm N5 nozzle must remain exceptionally low, often guaranteed by the licensor to be less than 1 ppm $NH\_3$. Achieving this requires flawless operation of the upstream Atmospheric Absorber to strip the ammonia before the gas reaches the stack.

## **Thermodynamic Modeling and Kinetic Framework**

For process engineers tasked with replicating the behavior of the 323C005 and 328V001 units within steady-state or dynamic chemical flowsheeting software (e.g., Aspen Plus, Pro/II, or proprietary Stamicarbon models), rigorous thermodynamic and kinetic frameworks must be established. Simplistic ideal-gas or Raoult's law assumptions will yield highly erroneous results.

### **Equations of State and Activity Coefficient Models**

The multiphase, multicomponent system passing through these vessels—comprising $NH\_3$, $CO\_2$, $H\_2O$, and trace urea—exhibits severe non-ideal thermodynamic behavior. This non-ideality is driven by extensive molecular speciation, strong ionic interactions, and the chemical formation of ammonium carbamate in the liquid phase.  
To accurately model the liquid phase, property packages such as UNIQUAC (Universal QuasiChemical) or the Electrolyte-NRTL model are absolutely essential. These advanced models calculate the activity coefficients of the complex ionic species ($NH\_4^+$, $HCO\_3^-$, $NH\_2COO^-$) that spontaneously form when the ammonia and carbon dioxide dissolve into the process water. For the vapor phase, empirical equations of state such as the Nakamura equation or advanced variations of the Soave-Redlich-Kwong (SRK) EOS are frequently employed. These models accurately predict the vapor fugacity coefficients of the highly non-ideal $NH\_3$ and $CO\_2$ gas mixtures at the relatively low operating pressure of 1 bar a and temperature of 67 °C.

### **Rate-Based Mass Transfer Modeling (323C005)**

Traditional equilibrium-stage models (e.g., theoretical plates) fall drastically short in accurately predicting the performance of the Atmospheric Absorber. The 1500 mm depth of the Pall ring bed provides a relatively short vapor residence time, meaning the system rarely approaches true thermodynamic equilibrium.  
Therefore, a non-ideal, rate-based distillation model must be utilized. In a rate-based framework, actual mass transfer coefficients for the liquid and gas phases ($k\_L$, $k\_G$) and the effective interfacial area ($a\_e$) are calculated dynamically for every differential height segment of the column. These parameters should be derived using empirical correlations specific to 25 mm metallic Pall rings, such as the widely accepted Billet-Schultes or Bravo-Fair pressure drop and mass transfer models.  
Furthermore, the model must rigorously account for the heat of absorption. The dissolution of ammonia into water is a highly exothermic process. As the feed water (introduced via N2) absorbs the upward-flowing $NH\_3$, the massive release of latent heat and heat of solution causes the fluid temperature to rise significantly, resulting in the measured exit operating point of 55 °C to 67 °C. If this heat is not adequately dissipated by the bulk mass of the water, the localized temperature spike will sharply decrease the solubility limit of the $NH\_3$, driving the equilibrium in reverse and potentially causing unacceptable ammonia slip out of the N5 gas outlet.

### **Hydrodynamic Modeling of the Vent Stack (328V001)**

The Vent Stack should be modeled computationally as a rigorous phase separator (flash drum) combined with a hydrostatic routing module.  
To accurately predict the knockout efficiency, the vertical velocity of the gas moving through the 914 mm ID shell must be calculated across all operating cases to ensure it remains strictly below the terminal settling velocity of the smallest anticipated liquid droplets. Applying the Souders-Brown equation, incorporating the actual densities of the gas mixture and the ammonia water, allows engineers to verify that the N5 gas outlet remains entirely free of entrained liquid carryover.  
Additionally, the hydrostatic head generated by the liquid pool below the N1 overflow nozzle dictates the physical backpressure exerted on the upstream 323C005 unit. The simulation must equate the volumetric liquid holdup to the cylindrical geometry of the stack base, allowing the model to accurately simulate transient liquid surge volumes and pressure fluctuations during severe plant upsets.

## **System Integration and Operational Dynamics**

The 323C005 and 328V001 units do not operate as isolated islands; they are deeply and symbiotically integrated into the plant's broader fluid recovery, economics, and environmental protection strategy.

### **Integration with Desorption and Hydrolysis (System 328\)**

The ultimate destination for all the ammonia-rich liquid captured by the Atmospheric Absorber and collected in the Vent Stack is the Ammonia Water Tank (328D003).  
The physical process path flows sequentially: Liquid exits 323C005 (N4) $\\rightarrow$ enters 328V001 (N2) $\\rightarrow$ pools and overflows 328V001 (N1) $\\rightarrow$ drains to 328D003.  
Once residing in the Ammonia Water Tank, this fluid becomes the primary feedstock for the Desorption and Hydrolysis section (System 328). The collected fluid is pumped (via the desorber feed pumps 328P003A/B) through a series of heat exchangers into the desorption columns. Here, the bound ammonia and urea are subjected to intense thermal stripping using medium- and low-pressure steam. This breaks the chemical bonds, recovering the highly valuable $NH\_3$ and $CO\_2$ reactants as vapors, which are then condensed and returned to the high-pressure recirculation section. Simultaneously, the process yields a purified process condensate stream that is clean enough to be reused as cooling tower makeup water or utilized in the granulation plant scrubbers.  
This creates a highly efficient, symbiotic loop. The continuous purging of inerts through the Atmospheric Absorber prevents the debilitating buildup of non-condensables in the main synthesis loop, which would otherwise stifle the entire total-recycle $CO\_2$ stripping efficiency. Conversely, the continuous removal of the resultant ammonia water via the Vent Stack to the Desorption section recovers the economic value of the purged reactants and prevents the liquid inventory from backing up and catastrophically flooding the absorber.

### **Startup, Shutdown, and Transient Upset Conditions**

During transient operations, the thermodynamic and hydraulic loads exerted on the 323C005 and 328V001 systems fluctuate wildly, requiring specific operator interventions.  
The operating manual dictates stringent procedures during a plant "Water Run" or initial startup sequence. Prior to introducing raw ammonia and carbon dioxide feedstocks into the high-pressure synthesis loop, the atmospheric systems must be fully primed. The LP Absorber feed pump (322P002) is engaged to circulate process condensate through the LP absorber, which subsequently drains down into the Atmospheric Absorber (323C005). This establishes the necessary liquid seal in the Vent Stack (328V001) and ensures that the 45 m³/h spray nozzle is fully active, providing the immediate scrubbing capability required to capture any sudden bursts of unreacted ammonia that frequently occur during the chaotic pressurization of the synthesis loop.  
During emergency upset scenarios, such as a complete cooling water failure in the upstream evaporation condensers (e.g., 324E002, 324E005), a massive surge of high-temperature, uncondensed ammonia and water vapor will bypass the primary liquid recovery phases and hit the 323C005 directly. The oversized capacity of the N2 water inlet and the robust 25 mm Pall ring bed are specifically engineered to handle such surge conditions. By maintaining a high continuous flow of subcooled process water, the absorber can instantaneously quench the temperature spike and absorb the massive ammonia load before it can break through the 328V001 stack and violate atmospheric emission limits.

## **Conclusion**

The Atmospheric Absorber (323C005) and Vent Stack (328V001) constitute the foundational pillars of environmental compliance and reactant recovery within the Stamicarbon urea production process. Serving as the ultimate safeguard against atmospheric ammonia emissions, these units ensure the closed-loop efficiency of the plant by capturing purged gases and returning them to the desorption infrastructure.  
The comprehensive analysis of their OEM datasheets reveals a highly robust mechanical design. The strategic utilization of BB.01 (1.4306/1.4541) low-carbon stainless steel effectively neutralizes the severe threat of intergranular corrosion posed by ammonium carbamate solutions.
