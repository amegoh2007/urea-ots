# **Comprehensive Process and Instrumentation Analysis of the Pre-Evaporation Section in Stamicarbon Urea Production**

## **1\. Thermodynamic and Process Context of the Pre-Evaporation Architecture**

In modern industrial fertilizer complexes, the Stamicarbon carbon dioxide ($CO\_2$) stripping process represents the benchmark for urea production. The synthesis of urea is governed by two sequential reactions: the highly exothermic formation of ammonium carbamate from ammonia and carbon dioxide, followed by the slow, endothermic dehydration of ammonium carbamate into urea and water. Because the dehydration reaction is equilibrium-limited, the reactor effluent contains a substantial fraction of unconverted reactants that must be separated and recycled. The evolution of the Stamicarbon process—from the traditional "N=1" once-through plants to the highly heat-integrated "N=2" and modern Flash Design concepts—has increasingly focused on optimizing the thermal efficiency of this separation and recovery phase.  
Within this framework, the pre-evaporation section bridges the gap between the low-pressure recirculation stage and the final high-vacuum evaporation or granulation stages. The specific equipment block comprising the Flash Tank (323F004), the Pre-evaporator (323E010), and the Separator Pre-evaporator (323F010) is tasked with the critical function of pre-concentrating the urea solution from approximately 68.7 wt.% to 80.0 wt.%. This concentration step is thermodynamically delicate. It must successfully drive off excess water and free ammonia to meet the stringent feed requirements of downstream granulation units while simultaneously suppressing the endothermic degradation of urea into biuret. Biuret is a phytotoxic byproduct whose formation kinetics are severely accelerated by elevated temperatures and high urea concentrations.  
By executing this intermediate concentration under a controlled vacuum of 0.46 bar, the pre-evaporation block artificially depresses the boiling point of the mixture. This allows the system to achieve the target 80.0 wt.% urea concentration at a strictly regulated temperature of $99^\\circ\\text{C}$, thereby avoiding the thermal thresholds that trigger rapid biuret formation. Furthermore, this intermediate step allows the plant to utilize low-grade heat sources—such as low-pressure (LP) steam or condensing medium-pressure carbamate gases—minimizing the extraction of high-value, high-pressure steam and optimizing the overall energy balance of the complex.

## **2\. Process Flow Tracking and Stream Characterization**

The fluid routing through the 323F004, 323E010, and 323F010 equipment block follows a sequential logic of adiabatic expansion followed by heat-integrated vaporization and mechanical phase disengagement. The Evaporation Process Flow Diagram (PFD) defines the holistic mass balance for this section using a series of specific original equipment manufacturer (OEM) stream identifiers, including streams 314, 315, 317, 319, 331, 3310, 341, 343, 401, 402, 4020, 600, 607, 701, 702, 703, 715, 718, 719, 720, 721, 722, and 756\. While the exact tabular mapping of these numbered streams to individual nozzles is abstracted in the overarching PFD, the rigorous thermodynamic parameters, mass flows, and chemical compositions characterizing the boundary conditions of this equipment block are explicitly defined by the operational parameters of the plant.  
The feed to the Flash Tank (323F004) originates from the low-pressure rectifying column (323C003). This incoming urea-carbamate solution is delivered at an operating pressure of 4.12 bar and a temperature of $135^\\circ\\text{C}$. Compositionally, this feed stream consists of 68.7 wt.% urea, 27.7 wt.% water, 2.1 wt.% ammonia, 1.1 wt.% carbon dioxide, and 0.4 wt.% biuret. Upon entering the Flash Tank, the solution passes through a letdown control valve and experiences a rapid, isenthalpic pressure drop from 4.12 bar down to 1.13 bar. This adiabatic expansion forces the highly endothermic dissociation of residual ammonium carbamate into ammonia and carbon dioxide gas, while simultaneously vaporizing a fraction of the free water. The extraction of latent heat during this flashing process drives the bulk liquid temperature down dramatically, cooling the solution from $135^\\circ\\text{C}$ to an equilibrium temperature of $106^\\circ\\text{C}$ to $107^\\circ\\text{C}$. The evolved vapor stream—primarily steam and ammonia—is discharged from the top of the Flash Tank to the Flash Tank Condenser (323E011), while the partially concentrated liquid is routed forward.  
The cooled, flashed liquid subsequently enters the Pre-evaporator (323E010). At this junction, the primary feed is joined by a secondary recycle stream. This recycle is a weak urea solution, featuring a concentration of approximately 45.0 wt.%, returned from the granulation section's scrubbing system via the urea solution recycle pumps (335P004A/B) at a volumetric flow rate of $3.4\\text{ m}^3\\text{/h}$. Inside the Pre-evaporator, the combined solution is subjected to a vacuum of 0.46 bar. Low-pressure steam supplied to the shell side of the heat exchanger provides the sensible and latent heat required to drive off the remaining volatile components, heating the two-phase mixture to a tightly controlled $99^\\circ\\text{C}$.  
The two-phase effluent from the Pre-evaporator is discharged directly into the Separator Pre-evaporator (323F010) for mechanical phase disengagement. Operating isobarically at 0.46 bar and isothermally at $99^\\circ\\text{C}$, the Separator isolates the vapor phase, routing it overhead to the First Stage Evaporator Condenser (324E002). The liquid phase, now fully concentrated to the target specifications, exits the bottom of the Separator. This final discharge stream consists of 80.0 wt.% urea, 19.4 wt.% water, 0.4 wt.% biuret, 800 ppm ammonia, 200 ppm carbon dioxide, and 194 ppm formaldehyde.

## **3\. Equipment Mechanical Design and Nozzle Architecture**

The physical containment of the pre-evaporation process demands robust mechanical engineering. The fluids involved are highly corrosive, particularly in regions where carbamate may condense or pool, necessitating the use of specialized metallurgy and precise fabrication tolerances. Both the Flash Tank and the Separator Pre-evaporator are designed in strict accordance with the German AD-Merkblätter pressure vessel codes and Stamicarbon's proprietary material specification 18005\. The process-wetted components of both vessels are fabricated from 1.4306 (BB.01) stainless steel, an ultra-low carbon austenitic alloy specifically selected for its resistance to intergranular corrosion in urea-carbamate environments. Furthermore, all internal and external nozzle edges subjected to fluid flow must be radiused and rounded to eliminate sharp corners that could induce localized stress concentrations or serve as initiation sites for crevice corrosion.

### **3.1 Flash Tank (323F004) Structural and Nozzle Details**

The Flash Tank is a vertically oriented cylindrical vessel designed to absorb the kinetic energy and volumetric expansion associated with the adiabatic flashing of the urea solution. The vessel features an inside diameter of 1384 mm and a cylindrical shell height of 1800 mm. It is engineered to endure a design pressure ranging from full vacuum (-1.0 bar g) up to 5.0 bar g at a design temperature of $165^\\circ\\text{C}$. The structural mass of the vessel is 1,235 kg upon delivery, which increases to an operating weight of 1,753 kg with process fluid holdup, and must support a massive hydro-test weight of 4,685 kg when fully flooded with water.  
To handle the violent multi-phase fluid dynamics, the primary process nozzles are equipped with specialized internals and reinforced to withstand immense external piping loads. The main gas/liquid inlet (Nozzle N1, DN 400\) and the overhead gas outlet (Nozzle N2, DN 400\) are both equipped with internal deflector disks. These disks serve to rapidly dissipate the momentum of the incoming flashed fluid, spreading it into a thin film to maximize the interfacial area for phase equilibrium, while the outlet disk acts as an impingement separator to strip entrained liquid droplets from the exiting vapor stream. The external piping loads on these nozzles are extreme; Nozzle N2 is designed to accommodate axial forces ($F\_Z$) up to 8,000 N and bending moments ($M\_X$) up to 9,500 Nm, reflecting the thermal expansion stresses and two-phase flow vibrations inherent to the system. The instrument connections, designated as pad-type flanges, are intentionally machined with 20 mm of excess bolt length to ensure secure mating with the heavy-duty instrument transmitters required for high-pressure urea service.

### **3.2 Separator Pre-evaporator (323F010) Structural and Nozzle Details**

The Separator Pre-evaporator is a substantially larger vessel, engineered to provide the necessary residence time and cross-sectional area for the terminal gravity-driven disengagement of vapors from the concentrated 80.0 wt.% urea melt. The vessel has an outside diameter of 3,500 mm, an inside diameter of 3,478 mm, and a cylindrical shell height of 2,437 mm. The empty delivery weight of the separator is 10,500 kg, scaling to an operating weight of 11,700 kg, and a hydro-test weight of 42,500 kg. Like the Flash Tank, it is rated for full vacuum (-1.0 bar g) up to a positive design pressure of 2.0 bar g at $130^\\circ\\text{C}$.  
Because 80.0 wt.% urea has a significantly elevated crystallization temperature, the lower section of the Separator is highly susceptible to freezing during startup, shutdown, or low-load operations. To mitigate this risk, the vessel is equipped with an integrated steam tracing coil (DN 15), constructed from 1.4306 stainless steel. This internal heating device is rated for 6/1 bar g at $165^\\circ\\text{C}$ and provides critical auxiliary heat to maintain the melt in a fluid state.  
Internally, the Separator features a cylindrical top baffle positioned near the massive DN 600 vapor outlet (Nozzle N3). This baffle forces a directional change in the ascending vapor, coalescing entrained liquid droplets and directing them downwards. Conversely, the liquid outlet (Nozzle N2, DN 400\) is protected by a vortex breaker. This component prevents the formation of a vapor-entraining funnel as the dense urea melt ($1173.94\\text{ kg/m}^3$) drains from the vessel, ensuring that vapor is not pulled down into the liquid piping where it could cause cavitation in the downstream pumps.

## **4\. Instrumentation, Control Logic, and Level Measurement Architecture**

The stability of the pre-evaporation block depends on a network of tightly coupled cascade and split-range control loops. The highly dynamic nature of flashing two-phase flow requires control systems that prioritize thermodynamic inference and remote hydraulic balancing over simple localized throttling. The Process and Instrumentation Diagrams (PID\_No.\_103\_1\_Recirculation\_1 and PID\_No.\_105\_1\_Evaporation) dictate a control philosophy that seamlessly integrates the mechanical vessels with the distributed control system (DCS).  
The Flash Tank (323F004) operates as a stabilized expansion buffer, requiring strict pressure and inventory control. The pressure within the vessel envelope is maintained at $1.13\\text{ bar}$ by the pressure indicating controller PIC323203. Rather than directly throttling the immediate vapor outlet of the Flash Tank, this control loop actuates the pneumatic control valve PV323203, which is located further downstream in the vapor discharge line connecting the Flash Tank Condenser (323E011) to the atmospheric absorber. By restricting the evacuation of non-condensable gases at the condenser outlet, PIC323203 exerts an artificial backpressure across the entire flash system, precisely governing the thermodynamics of the initial adiabatic expansion.  
The liquid inventory within the Flash Tank is managed by the level indicating controller LIC323505. The physical level is measured directly within the vertical cylindrical shell of 323F004. This is achieved using differential pressure (DP) cells mounted on the pad-type instrument nozzles N7 and N8, which transmit a continuous 4-20mA signal to the DCS. A local sight glass (Level Indicator, LI) is also installed on nozzle N9 to provide operators with a direct visual verification of the electronic readings. Based on the DP cell inputs, LIC323505 modulates the liquid discharge control valve LV323505, metering the flashed solution into the downstream Pre-evaporator.  
The Pre-evaporator (323E010) functions as a continuous-flow tubular heater and relies entirely on a thermodynamic cascade loop rather than direct liquid level control. The primary objective of this unit is to establish a strict $99^\\circ\\text{C}$ output temperature to suppress biuret formation while driving off water. This parameter is governed by the temperature indicating controller TIC323012. Operating as the master controller in a cascade configuration, TIC323012 calculates the required thermal input and dynamically adjusts the setpoint of the slave pressure controller PIC329208. PIC329208 subsequently modulates the admission of low-pressure steam to the shell side of the Pre-evaporator. By throttling the steam pressure, the system precisely controls the temperature differential ($\\Delta T$) across the tube bundle, governing the exact rate of latent heat transfer to the urea solution. The Pre-evaporator contains no internal level transmitters; its fluid inventory is determined purely by the hydraulic push from LV323505 and the pull of the downstream vacuum.  
The Separator Pre-evaporator (323F010) completes the separation process under high vacuum. The operational pressure is continuously monitored by the pressure indicator PI323204, which targets $0.46\\text{ bar}$. To maintain this deep vacuum against the continuous influx of vaporized water and ammonia, the manual/hand-indicating control valve HIC/HV323605 is utilized. This valve throttles the massive DN 600 vapor discharge line connecting the top dome of the Separator to the First Stage Evaporation Condenser (324E002), balancing the evacuation rate against the vapor generation rate.  
Remarkably, the Separator Pre-evaporator lacks any active level control instrumentation or modulating discharge valves on its liquid outlet. To minimize the residence time of the highly concentrated 80.0 wt.% urea melt—and thereby drastically reduce the kinetic window for biuret formation—the vessel utilizes a continuous gravity-driven overflow mechanism. The concentrated liquid drains freely from the vortex-breaker-equipped Nozzle N2, dropping through a sealed barometric leg piping arrangement directly into Compartment I of the downstream Urea Solution Tank (323D002). Consequently, the burden of level control is shifted entirely to the storage tank, where the level indicating controller LIC323507 monitors the accumulated inventory and subsequently commands the frequency and output of the urea solution forward pumps (323P003A/B).

## **5\. Operational Dynamics: Startup, Shutdown, and Emergency Scenarios**

The pre-evaporation block undergoes severe thermal and hydraulic transients during operational shifts. The standard operating manual defines strict procedural sequences for preheating, vacuum testing, normal startup, capacity turn-down, and emergency isolation to protect both the metallurgy of the vessels and the chemical integrity of the urea melt.  
During the initial plant preheating and water-run phases, the pre-evaporation block is prepared to accept high-temperature flows. Low-pressure steam is introduced to the shell side of the Pre-evaporator, and the tracing coils of the Separator are energized. The vents on the shell side are kept open to expel non-condensable gases and ensure uniform heat distribution, maintaining a pre-heating pressure of approximately 2 bar controlled by PIC329208. Simultaneously, the vacuum integrity of the entire evaporation train is verified. With the liquid inlet to the Pre-evaporator blinded and the discharge lines flooded with steam condensate to create hydraulic seals in the barometric legs, the steam ejectors (324F002, 324F004, 324F005) are activated. The pressure in the pre-evaporator system must successfully drop to the target 0.46 bar within approximately 15 minutes, confirming that no atmospheric ingress will compromise the process thermodynamics.  
As the actual plant startup commences and the upstream urea synthesis and recirculation sections begin generating product, the pre-evaporation block is brought online. The vacuum controllers are set to automatic, pulling the system down to 0.46 bar. As the 4.12 bar solution from the rectifying column breaches LV323501, it flashes violently into the Flash Tank. Once a stable liquid level is established in 323F004, LIC323505 is engaged, opening LV323505 to admit the flashed fluid into the Pre-evaporator. The temperature controller TIC323012 is placed in cascade mode to actively govern the steam input, locking the effluent temperature at $99^\\circ\\text{C}$. The separated liquid overflows from the Separator into the Urea Solution Tank. Because downstream granulation units require highly specific concentrations, the initial dilute urea solution is not forwarded to production. Instead, the downstream urea feed pumps (335P001A/B) are utilized to recycle the off-spec melt back into the Urea Solution Tank via a dedicated recycle valve (LV324501B), keeping the evaporation train in continuous circulation until the 80.0 wt.% and subsequent 98.6 wt.% thresholds are consistently achieved. During this circulation phase, steam condensate must be carefully injected into the recycle line to dilute the melt, preventing the circulating inventory from over-concentrating and triggering catastrophic crystallization within the piping.  
When a planned plant shutdown is executed, the mass flow into the pre-evaporation section is gradually curtailed by closing the upstream synthesis letdown valves. As the level in the Flash Tank drops, LV323505 throttles closed. To maintain thermal equilibrium and prevent the stagnant urea melt from freezing inside the Pre-evaporator and Separator, the entire evaporation train is once again placed on full circulation. The forward feed valve to the granulator (LV324501A) is driven closed, and the split-range recycle valve (LV324501B) is opened. The vacuum is then carefully broken by admitting false atmospheric air into the condenser overhead lines via PV324202 and PV324203, raising the system back to atmospheric pressure. As the vacuum decays, the steam pressure to the Pre-evaporator must be aggressively reduced to approximately 2 bar to prevent the circulating urea solution from boiling dry or degrading into biuret at atmospheric pressure. The circulating fluid is gradually displaced by injecting steam condensate into the suction lines, flushing the residual urea out of the heat exchanger tubes and separator baffles until the sample points yield pure condensate.  
In the event of a catastrophic utility failure, such as a total loss of motive steam or instrument air, the pre-evaporation control architecture is designed to fail into a safe, isolated state. Upon loss of instrument air, the pneumatic actuators on the critical steam supply valves (such as the LP steam admission to the Pre-evaporator) are spring-loaded to fail closed, instantly arresting the heat input to prevent the stagnant urea from overheating and rapidly decomposing. Simultaneously, the forward feed valves fail closed while the recycle bypass valves fail open, ensuring that any residual hydraulic momentum is safely routed back to the atmospheric storage tanks rather than flooding the downstream granulation spray headers. If motive steam to the vacuum ejectors fails, the vacuum will decay rapidly. Operators must immediately manually vent the steam shells to atmosphere to drop the heating temperature, while utilizing the emergency steam condensate pumps (operating on backup diesel or battery power) to flood the pre-evaporator and separator with condensate, diluting the trapped urea melt before it can cool and solidify into an intractable mass within the vessels.

## **6\. Conclusion**

The pre-evaporation architecture within the Stamicarbon urea process represents an exceptionally elegant synthesis of thermodynamic manipulation, mechanical robustness, and dynamic process control. By isolating the transitional concentration phase between the 4.12 bar recirculation loop and the deep-vacuum final evaporation stages, the Flash Tank (323F004), Pre-evaporator (323E010), and Separator Pre-evaporator (323F010) successfully drive off volatile ammonia and water without crossing the thermal thresholds that initiate biuret degradation.  
The mechanical execution of these vessels, relying on ultra-low carbon BB.01 stainless steel and specialized internal deflector baffling, ensures long-term resistance to carbamate-induced intergranular corrosion. The process control philosophy is uniquely tailored to the demands of two-phase flashing flow, utilizing remote condenser backpressure to govern the primary flash, a strict thermodynamic cascade loop to govern vaporization heat input, and a passive gravity-overflow mechanism to minimize residence time in the final separator. Through seamless integration with the plant's broader low-pressure steam and condensate recovery networks, this equipment block ensures that downstream granulation units receive a continuously stabilized, high-purity urea melt.
