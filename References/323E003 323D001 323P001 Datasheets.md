# **Exhaustive Process and Instrumentation Analysis of the LP Recirculation and HP Carbamate Pumping Systems in Stamicarbon Urea Production**

## **The Stamicarbon Total-Recycle Process Architecture**

The synthesis of urea via the Stamicarbon total-recycle carbon dioxide stripping process represents a sophisticated integration of multiphase thermodynamics, extreme-pressure fluid mechanics, and stringent metallurgical engineering. In this process architecture, ammonia and carbon dioxide are reacted to form ammonium carbamate, which subsequently dehydrates into urea and water. Because the dehydration reaction is endothermic and equilibrium-limited, a significant portion of the reactants remains unconverted and must be systematically stripped, condensed, and recycled back into the high-pressure synthesis loop.  
The Low-Pressure (LP) Recirculation stage serves as the final primary recovery node for these unconverted reactants. Operating at approximately 3.2 to 4.1 bar absolute, this section captures vapors driven off from the rectifying column and flashes them into a liquid phase. The principal equipment facilitating this recovery—the LP Carbamate Condenser (323E003), the Level Tank for the LP Carbamate Condenser (323D001), and the High-Pressure (HP) Carbamate Pump (323P001 A/B)—must operate in flawless thermodynamic and hydrodynamic synchrony. This analysis provides a meticulous evaluation of the process flow streams, mechanical design criteria, advanced instrumentation matrices, and control loop topologies governing these assets.

## **Process Flow Stream Mapping and Thermodynamic Characterization**

The successful operation of the recirculation section is fundamentally dependent on precise phase control. The process streams entering and exiting the 323E003, 323D001, and 323P001 network encompass highly corrosive multiphase mixtures of ammonia, carbon dioxide, water, and traces of urea and biuret. The quaternary system of these components dictates that condensation must be tightly controlled to avoid azeotropic limits and solid-phase crystallization.  
The following tables synthesize the process flow streams acting as feeds and discharges for the equipment under review, derived directly from the plant's heat and mass balance parameters.

## **LP Carbamate Condenser (323E003)**

### **Structural and Mechanical Engineering**

The LP Carbamate Condenser is engineered as a vertical, single-pass shell, multipass U-tube heat exchanger, classified under TEMA standards as a BEU type. The unit's structural integrity and material selection are governed by the aggressive active corrosion mechanisms inherent to intermediate-temperature carbamate solutions.  
The heat exchanger provides an effective surface area of 631.00 m$^2$, composed of 908 U-tubes with an outer diameter of 25.0 mm, a wall thickness of 1.60 mm, and an effective straight length of 4,430 mm. The tubes are arranged in a 60-degree triangular pitch of 32 mm to maximize shell-side turbulence, thereby enhancing the forced-convection heat transfer coefficient to a calculated 327 W/m$^2$K under normal fouling conditions.  
Material selection is paramount. The tube side, handling benign circulating water, utilizes standard carbon steel components. However, the shell side, which contains the reacting ammonium carbamate, is entirely clad or constructed from Stamicarbon Specification 18005 material, designated as BC.09 (a highly specialized 316L Urea Grade stainless steel). The shell has an inside diameter of 1,600 mm, with the carbon steel outer pressure boundary clad with a minimum of 5 mm of BC.09 after machining to prevent electrochemical dissolution of the iron matrix. Finite element analysis (FEA) indicates that the primary membrane stress ratios near the shell-to-header junction remain well below the 1.5 $S\_m$ allowable limits, ensuring mechanical stability against both operational pressure and the substantial thermal expansion cycles (up to $165^\\circ$C design temperature).

### **Internal Fluid Mechanics and Mass/Heat Transfer**

The mass transfer phenomena within 323E003 are defined by gas-liquid absorption coupled with an instantaneous chemical reaction. The vapor feed from the rectifying column enters the bottom of the shell through nozzle N1 (DN 400), ascending through the baffled shell side. The shell utilizes six specialized BC.09 baffles with varying cuts (ranging from 27% to 50% of the diameter) to create a tortuous cross-flow path, maximizing the residence time and interfacial surface area between the rising vapor and the descending liquid absorbents.  
Lean carbamate solution and process condensate are introduced to the upper shell region. As the liquid descends, the ammonia and carbon dioxide vapors are absorbed into the aqueous phase. The heat liberated by this reaction ($\\Delta H \= \-117 \\text{ kJ/mol}$) must be transferred across the 1.60 mm thick U-tube walls to the cooling water. Because water acts as a medium-weight solvent in this quaternary system, it shifts the thermodynamic phase envelope, eliminating the binary azeotropy and establishing distinct dew and bubble point curves known as top-ridge lines. To optimize condensation, liquid ammonia is injected to maintain the overall nitrogen-to-carbon (N/C) molar ratio between 2.0 and 2.3. This specific stoichiometric balance aligns the mixture with the top-ridge liquid line, ensuring that the vapor pressure of the solution is minimized, thereby maximizing reactant recovery and limiting the volume of non-condensable gases vented through the level tank.

### **Instrumentation and Nozzle Architecture**

The physical integration of the condenser into the plant's control matrix is facilitated through its specific nozzle arrangement:

* **N1 (DN 400):** Main gas inlet from the recirculation section.  
* **N2 (DN 70, Lens Facing):** Carbamate inlet, handling the high-pressure differential of the lean solution return.  
* **N3 (DN 300):** Liquid and two-phase outlet dropping directly into the level tank.  
* **N5 / N6 (DN 400):** Cooling water inlet and outlet, heavily instrumented to measure the $10^\\circ$C temperature differential.

The primary control loop governing the condenser is TIC323013. By continually measuring the temperature of the cooling water inlet (Stream 1102), this loop modulates the bypass valves around the circulation cooler. If the temperature approaches $54^\\circ$C, the control logic infers an imminent risk of carbamate crystallization on the shell-side tubes. The secondary consequence of crystallization is a rapid loss of heat transfer, which cascades into an inability to condense the incoming vapors, subsequently causing a massive pressure spike in the upstream distillation columns.

## **Level Tank for LP Carbamate Condenser (323D001)**

### **Mechanical Configuration and Internals**

Directly coupled beneath the condenser is the Level Tank (323D001). Designed in accordance with AD 2000-Merkblatt codes, this vertical cylindrical vessel operates with an internal diameter of 2,178 mm and a tangent-to-tangent height of 2,350 mm, providing a nominal buffer volume of 11.10 m$^3$. The vessel is designed to withstand a pressure of 5.0 bar g at $165^\\circ$C.  
The internal hydrodynamics are carefully engineered to protect the downstream pumping equipment. The two-phase flow exiting the condenser enters the level tank via nozzle N1 (DN 300). To prevent this high-velocity stream from jetting directly into the liquid pool and causing severe frothing or vortexing, an impingement plate is rigidly welded below the inlet. At the vessel's base, liquid is extracted through nozzle N4 (DN 200). A cross-type vortex breaker is installed over this outlet. By breaking the angular momentum of the draining fluid, the vortex breaker guarantees that non-condensable gases (such as the passivation air introduced earlier in the synthesis process) are not entrained into the suction line of the high-pressure pump, an event that would lead to catastrophic cavitation and mechanical failure.

### **Instrumentation Topography and Control Logic**

The Level Tank serves as the central node for pressure and inventory control in the recirculation stage. The precise location and specification of its instruments dictate the plant's automated response to transient process changes.

* **Pressure Control (PIC323202):** The pressure transmitter (PT) for this loop is mounted on nozzle N5 (DN 80, Pad type flange). PIC323202 maintains the tank—and by extension the entire recirculation stage—at a setpoint of 3.2 bar a. It achieves this via a split-range cascade architecture. For fine control, it alters the setpoint of the cooling water temperature controller (TIC323013), thereby adjusting the condensation rate. For coarse control or during pressure spikes, it commands the vent valve (TV323013) located on nozzle N2 (DN 100\) to release non-condensable inerts and equilibrium vapor (Stream 321\) to the Flash Tank Condenser.  
* **Level Indication and Control (LIC323502):** The liquid inventory is measured via differential pressure (DP) transmitters mounted on nozzles N6A/B and N7A/B (DN 80, Pad type flanges). In aggressive urea/carbamate services, standard DP cells are prone to rapid plugging due to crystallization in the impulse lines. To mitigate this, the process incorporates continuous flush systems utilizing pure steam condensate to keep the transmitter diaphragms clear of scaling. Furthermore, some level indications rely on non-contact radioactive nucleonic gauges, which detect gamma-ray attenuation across the vessel diameter, rendering them entirely immune to the corrosive fluid.  
* **Control Actuation:** The output of LIC323502 is not sent to a standard throttling control valve. Throttling highly concentrated carbamate across a valve trim induces extreme shear forces and rapid localized pressure drops, leading to instantaneous flashing, erosion, and crystallization. Instead, LIC323502 outputs directly to the speed controller (SIC323901/02) of the HP Carbamate Pump. A rising level generates a command to accelerate the pump, whereas a falling level decelerates it.

## **HP Carbamate Pump (323P001 A/B)**

### **Mechanical Design of the Fluid End**

The extraction of the carbamate solution from the Level Tank and its pressurization for reinjection into the high-pressure synthesis loop is performed by the HP Carbamate Pump (323P001 A/B). The selected machinery is a URACA KD 825-Carb horizontal, triplex reciprocating plunger pump.  
The pump geometry features three plungers, each with a diameter of 130 mm and a stroke length of 220 mm. Operating at a normal speed of 64 rpm, the average plunger velocity is kept exceptionally low at 0.55 m/s. This slow kinematic profile is deliberate; it minimizes the required Net Positive Suction Head (NPSH), prevents fluid separation during the suction stroke, and extends the lifespan of the packing rings. The allowable plunger load is rated at 325 kN, comfortably exceeding the actual maximum operating load of 247.1 kN, ensuring robust mechanical margins against fatigue.  
Metallurgical constraints are rigidly enforced. The presence of copper or copper alloys anywhere in the wetted path is strictly prohibited due to rapid dissolution by ammonia. The manifold is forged from 1.4429 stainless steel, while the plungers are machined from 1.4593. The stuffing box utilizes Lattyflon No. 4757-64 PTFE packing rings supported by PEEK PVX guide rings. Because any leakage of carbamate past the primary packing would crystallize upon contact with atmospheric temperatures—scoring the plungers and destroying the seals—the stuffing box is continuously flushed with $1.8 \\text{ m}^3\\text{/h}$ of process condensate.

### **Hydrodynamic Speed Control System**

The drive train of the pump consists of a 355 kW Loher electric motor (ASHA-355LM-04A), a Flender single-helical gearbox (H2SH11), and a Voith EL 9 Hydrodynamic Torque Converter. The Voith fluid coupling is the master element enabling variable speed operation from a fixed-speed electric motor.  
The hydrodynamic torque converter operates via kinetic energy transfer between a primary impeller (driven by the motor) and a secondary runner (driving the pump gear) inside an oil-filled casing. The degree of hydrodynamic slip—and therefore the output speed—is directly proportional to the volume of oil actively circulating between the impeller and runner.  
The physical control mechanism is a pneumatically actuated "scoop tube". When the level controller (LIC323502) detects a rising level in 323D001, it sends a higher continuous signal to the speed controller (SIC323901). The pneumatic actuator responds by retracting the scoop tube radially outward. This retraction allows the working oil circuit to fill more deeply, reducing slip and increasing the rotational speed of the pump. Conversely, if the tank level falls, the actuator pushes the scoop tube deeper into the casing, skimming oil out of the working circuit into the sump, increasing slip, and slowing the pump.

### **Kinematic Deduction: Flow vs. Speed Profile**

Because 323P001 A/B is a positive displacement plunger pump, its volumetric flow rate is strictly a linear mathematical function of its rotational speed, independent of the discharge pressure curve. The theoretical swept volume ($Q\_{th}$) per hour is derived from the internal geometry:  
$$Q\_{th} \= \\left( \\frac{\\pi \\cdot D^2}{4} \\right) \\cdot L \\cdot z \\cdot n \\cdot 60$$  
Substituting the mechanical parameters ($D \= 0.130$ m, $L \= 0.220$ m, $z \= 3$ plungers):  
$$Q\_{th} \= \\left( \\frac{\\pi \\cdot (0.130)^2}{4} \\right) \\cdot 0.220 \\cdot 3 \\cdot n \\cdot 60 \\approx 0.5256 \\cdot n \\text{ m}^3\\text{/h}$$  
The pump is designed with a highly efficient valve dynamic, producing a stated volumetric efficiency ($\\eta\_v$) of 96%. Applying this efficiency to the theoretical swept volume yields the actual delivered flow rate ($Q\_{actual}$):  
$$Q\_{actual} \= 0.5256 \\cdot n \\cdot 0.96 \= 0.5046 \\cdot n \\text{ m}^3\\text{/h}$$  
Utilizing this derived linear constant, the exact operational profile of the pump across its turndown range is calculated as follows:

| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Minimum Turndown** | 19 | 9.99 | 96.0% | **9.6** |

This linear dynamic confirms that the tuning of the cascade loop between LIC323502 and SIC323901 requires minimal non-linear characterization. A $10\\%$ change in the pneumatic signal to the Voith coupling's scoop tube yields a predictable, strictly proportional response in the volumetric evacuation rate from the level tank.

### **Auxiliary Lube Oil and Interlock Matrices**

The mechanical longevity of the drive train is ensured by a comprehensive, forced-feed lube oil network. Dedicated Rickmeier gear pumps (model R35/31.5FU-Z-DB) circulate ISO VG 220 oil through the pump crankcase and ISO VG 460 oil through the Flender gearbox. The thermal energy dissipated by mechanical friction is removed by Gerberich shell-and-tube oil coolers (323P001A/BE01 and BE02), maintaining oil supply temperatures below $55^\\circ$C. The oil is continuously polished by Boll & Kirch duplex filters (2.04.5-90-25) operating at 25-micron absolute filtration.  
The entire pumping skid is rigorously interlocked. Pursuant to API 674 and API 670 standards, proximitors measure axial shaft displacement, while seismic accelerometers detect high-frequency mechanical anomalies indicating incipient bearing or valve failure. Any loss of pressure in the Rickmeier lube oil headers or detection of severe vibration instantly trips the 355 kW Loher motor to prevent catastrophic mechanical seizing. To protect the high-pressure piping from acoustic fatigue, a 210-liter suction stabilizer and a 382-liter discharge resonator suppress fluid pulsations to a maximum amplitude of $\\pm 2.5\\%$.

## **Passivation, Corrosion Dynamics, and Leak Detection**

The continuous handling of intermediate-temperature ammonium carbamate introduces severe corrosion risks. The fundamental protection mechanism for the BC.09 (316L Urea Grade) stainless steel network relies on the maintenance of a microscopic, self-healing chromium-oxide passive layer. This layer is chemically sustained by the continuous injection of passivation air (providing an oxygen content of approximately 0.6 vol% relative to the $CO\_2$ feed) into the synthesis loop.  
Should the oxygen partial pressure drop, the passive layer breaks down, initiating aggressive electrochemical dissolution (active corrosion) of the steel matrix. Because crevices and stagnant zones inherently deplete oxygen, the hydrodynamics within the condenser and level tank are designed to guarantee continuous fluid turnover.  
To mitigate the catastrophic consequences of a liner failure in high-pressure equipment, Stamicarbon relies on a sophisticated Leak Detection System (LDS). The pressure-bearing carbon steel shell is overlaid with the BC.09 liner. A network of longitudinal and lateral grooves is machined into the carbon steel behind the liner welds. This interstitial space is continuously purged with plant instrument air, heavily reduced to 1.3 bar absolute via a pressure control valve (PCV322211).  
The flow through these grooves is strictly regulated to 15 Normal Liters per hour (NL/hr) utilizing precision rotameters. If a microscopic breach occurs in the BC.09 liner, carbamate leaks into the groove network. At the near-atmospheric pressure of the purge system, the ammonium carbamate instantly dissociates into gaseous ammonia and carbon dioxide. The continuous air purge sweeps this ammonia vapor to an exhaust header, where a Draeger PAC III Polytron gas detector (equipped with an XS $NH\_3$ sensor capable of 5 ppm resolution) continuously samples the exhaust. An alarm threshold is set at 25 ppm. Because the system is continuously purged, the lag time between a mechanical breach and analytical detection is reduced to under one hour, allowing operators to execute a controlled shutdown before the carbon steel pressure boundary is compromised.  
Through the flawless integration of quaternary thermodynamics, specialized metallurgical applications, and highly responsive hydrodynamic control loops, the LP Recirculation and HP Carbamate pumping systems maintain the foundational efficiency and safety of the modern urea production facility.
