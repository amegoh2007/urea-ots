# **Advanced Process Engineering Analysis of the Stamicarbon Desorption and Hydrolysis Section**

## **Introduction to the Stamicarbon Urea Process and Effluent Recovery**

# **The synthesis of urea via the Stamicarbon total-recycle carbon dioxide stripping process is a highly integrated thermodynamic and chemical operation. In this architecture, ammonia and carbon dioxide are introduced into a high-pressure carbamate condenser, where they react exothermically to form ammonium carbamate. This intermediate is subsequently dehydrated in a urea reactor to form the final urea product alongside water. Because the dehydration of ammonium carbamate is an endothermic equilibrium reaction that does not proceed to absolute completion, a significant fraction of unreacted ammonia and carbon dioxide remains dissolved in the urea solution. The Stamicarbon design mitigates this inefficiency by counter-currently stripping the reactor effluent with carbon dioxide in a high-pressure heat exchanger, which lowers the partial pressure of ammonia and forces the carbamate equilibrium toward dissociation.**

# **Despite these highly efficient internal recycling mechanisms, the stoichiometric chemistry of urea formation inherently produces water as a byproduct—approximately 0.3 tons of water per ton of synthesized urea. This reaction water, alongside steam condensates and necessary flush waters introduced into the high-pressure equipment, must be separated from the urea melt to achieve the required final product concentration. This separation occurs during the low-pressure evaporation stages. The resulting separated water, termed process condensate, is heavily contaminated with ammonia, carbon dioxide, and unreacted urea. If discharged directly into the environment, this nitrogen-rich effluent would cause severe ecological degradation, promoting massive algal blooms and demonstrating acute toxicity to aquatic life forms due to the high ammonia concentration.**

# **To achieve stringent environmental compliance and maximize raw material utilization efficiency, the Stamicarbon technology employs a dedicated Desorption and Hydrolysis unit, designated in the engineering documentation as System 328\. This unit is meticulously engineered to thermally decompose the residual urea back into ammonia and carbon dioxide, and subsequently strip these dissolved gases from the aqueous phase to yield a highly purified process condensate. The purified effluent, containing less than 1 part per million (ppm) by weight of urea and less than 2 ppm by weight of ammonia, can then be repurposed as make-up water for the cooling tower or polished for use as boiler feed water. This recovery loop effectively renders the Stamicarbon process a zero-wastewater-effluent technology.**

# **This report provides an exhaustive, expert-level evaluation of the primary equipment within System 328\. Specifically, the analysis targets the Desorber Heat Exchanger (328E007), Hydrolizer Heat Exchanger (328E021A/B), Recycle Cooler (328E001), Desorber Feed Pump (328P003A/B), and Hydrolizer Feed Pump (328P006A/B). By integrating process flow diagrams (PFDs), piping and instrumentation diagrams (P\&IDs), and OEM technical datasheets, this document delineates the fluid dynamics, mass and heat transfer phenomena, thermodynamic modeling parameters, and control loop topologies governing the desorption and hydrolysis operations.**

## **Process Flow Stream Tracking and Composition Mapping**

# **The flow path through the System 328 Desorption and Hydrolysis unit represents a sequential cascade of pre-heating, desorption, thermal hydrolysis, and final stripping. The operational topology begins at the Ammonia Water Tank (328D003), a large multi-compartment vessel that collects the contaminated process condensate from the synthesis section's flash tank and the evaporation condensers. The blending of these streams in the tank normalizes the concentration fluctuations of ammonia, carbon dioxide, and urea before the fluid enters the continuous treatment process.**

# **From the Ammonia Water Tank, the Desorber Feed Pump (328P003A/B) pressurizes the contaminated condensate and routes it through the cold side of the Desorber Heat Exchanger (328E007). Here, the feed recovers sensible heat from the purified effluent exiting the bottom of Desorption Column II (328C004). The pre-heated condensate then enters the top tray of Desorption Column I (328C002), where initial steam stripping removes the bulk of the free dissolved ammonia and carbon dioxide into the vapor phase.**

# **The bottom effluent from Desorption Column I, which has been stripped of free gases but still contains stable, unreacted urea, is extracted by the Hydrolizer Feed Pump (328P006A/B). This pump provides the substantial hydraulic head required to push the fluid into the high-pressure reaction environment. The fluid is driven through the cold side of the Hydrolizer Heat Exchanger (328E021A/B), where it utilizes the extreme heat of the hydrolyzer's outgoing effluent to pre-heat the incoming feed to the requisite reaction activation temperatures.**

# **The pre-heated feed then enters the top of the Hydrolyzer (328C003), a vertical vessel operating at approximately 16.8 bar and 200 °C, where the urea undergoes thermal decomposition. The completely hydrolyzed liquid exits the bottom of the reactor, transfers its residual high-grade heat in 328E021A/B, and enters the top of Desorption Column II (328C004). In this final stripping column, low-pressure steam is injected at the bottom to strip the newly formed ammonia and carbon dioxide (generated during hydrolysis) out of the liquid phase, producing a highly purified process condensate. This purified stream acts as the hot fluid in 328E007, transferring its remaining thermal energy to the incoming fresh feed, before passing through the Recycle Cooler (328E001) for final temperature reduction prior to battery limit discharge.**

## **Comprehensive Equipment Analysis: Thermodynamics and Heat Transfer**

# **The thermal optimization of the desorption and hydrolysis section relies exclusively on advanced plate heat exchangers rather than conventional shell-and-tube configurations. Plate heat exchangers utilize highly corrugated metal plates compressed together to form narrow, tortuous flow channels. This geometry maximizes the heat transfer surface area per unit volume and induces exceptionally high-turbulence fluid flow even at relatively low velocities. This turbulence disrupts the laminar sublayer, significantly enhancing overall convective heat transfer coefficients and minimizing the volumetric footprint of the equipment. Furthermore, plate heat exchangers can achieve much closer temperature approaches (often down to 1-2 °C) compared to shell-and-tube units, making them ideal for the highly integrated heat recovery networks required in modern urea plants. All heat exchangers in this section adhere to the strict AD-Merkblätter design codes for pressure vessels and are engineered with an excess surface area of 20.0% to provide a robust operational margin against long-term chemical fouling and scaling.**

# **To quantitatively evaluate the thermal efficiency of these specific units, the Overall Heat Transfer Coefficient ($U$) must be derived. Because the OEM datasheets leave the calculated $U$ value fields blank , $U$ is mathematically reconstructed herein utilizing the fundamental macroscopic heat transfer equation:**

# **$$Q \= U \\cdot A \\cdot \\Delta T\_{LMTD}$$**

# **Where $Q$ is the total thermal heat duty (in Watts), $A$ is the effective heat transfer surface area (in square meters), and $\\Delta T\_{LMTD}$ is the Logarithmic Mean Temperature Difference (in Kelvin). For a purely counter-current flow arrangement, the LMTD is defined as:**

# **$$\\Delta T\_{LMTD} \= \\frac{\\Delta T\_1 \- \\Delta T\_2}{\\ln(\\Delta T\_1 / \\Delta T\_2)}$$**

# **Where $\\Delta T\_1$ represents the temperature difference between the hot and cold fluids at one end of the exchanger, and $\\Delta T\_2$ represents the temperature difference at the opposite end.**

It captures sensible heat from the highly purified process condensate discharging from the sump of Desorption Column II, utilizing it to pre-heat the raw ammonia water feed destined for Desorption Column I. The addition of molybdenum provides superior resistance to pitting and crevice corrosion, which is absolutely critical when handling solutions containing highly aggressive ammonium carbamate and trace chlorides from cooling water systems. The structural frame is fabricated from robust carbon steel (CS) with P265GH pressure-retaining end plates. The frame tie rods are specifically sized to accommodate a future physical expansion capacity, allowing for the installation of at least 10% additional plates if plant debottlenecking is required. Because the operating temperatures and pressures are relatively moderate (design limits of 170 °C and 8.0 bar g), the unit utilizes EPDM (Ethylene Propylene Diene Monomer) elastomeric plate gaskets. EPDM provides exceptional elastomeric resilience and chemical compatibility against aqueous ammonia up to its thermal degradation limit. The primary piping nozzles (N1 through N4) feature a DN 100 nominal diameter, PN 16 pressure rating, and are internally lined with 1.4404 metallurgy to prevent the carbon steel flanges from experiencing galvanic or general corrosion.**

### **Level Measurement and Indication Theory**

# **Level Indication Controllers (LIC) are ubiquitous across the desorption columns and the hydrolyzer. Measuring the liquid level inside vessels operating at 200 °C and 16.8 bar containing highly corrosive, volatile, and toxic ammonia-carbamate-urea mixtures presents severe instrumentation challenges. Conventional sight glasses are mechanically insufficient and inherently unsafe for operators.**

# **Level in these extreme environments is typically measured using Differential Pressure (DP) transmitters equipped with remote chemical seal diaphragms, or increasingly, via advanced Guided Wave Radar (GWR) or radiometric (nuclear) sensors. For DP measurement, the transmitter measures the hydrostatic head of the liquid column at the bottom sump of the vessel. Because the vapor space above the liquid contains highly pressurized $CO\_2$ and $NH\_3$ that can easily condense, the low-pressure leg of the DP cell must be carefully compensated (often via a continuous purge or a capillary fluid seal) to prevent vapor condensation in the impulse lines from inducing massive calibration errors. The measured variable (PV) is continuously transmitted to the Distributed Control System (DCS), which compares it against the programmed setpoint (SP) and modulates the corresponding control valves (LV) in real-time.**

### **Control Loop Architecture**

# **The overarching control strategy is designed to minimize utility steam consumption while absolutely ensuring that the purified condensate meets the stringent environmental discharge limits.**

# **1\. Desorber I Control (328C002):**

* # **Level Control: The liquid sump level in Desorption Column I must be strictly maintained to ensure a steady feed to the downstream hydrolyzer. LIC328503 monitors this level and directly actuates valve LV328503, which is situated on the discharge line of the Hydrolizer Feed Pump (328P006A/B). This proportional-integral (PI) feedback loop ensures that the hydraulic extraction of liquid perfectly matches the incoming condensate feed rate, preventing the column from flooding or running dry.**

* # **Temperature and Reflux Control: TIC328008 monitors the top vapor temperature of Desorber I, typically maintaining it at 117 °C. The overhead temperature dictates the precise mass ratio of water vapor escaping alongside the desorbed ammonia and $CO\_2$. If the temperature rises above setpoint, excess water is vaporizing. This excess water would dilute the lean carbamate solution formed in the downstream Reflux Condenser (328E004), disrupting the water balance of the entire urea plant. To counteract a temperature rise, TIC328008 operates in a cascade configuration, adjusting the setpoint of FIC328404. This flow controller regulates the volumetric flow of cold reflux liquid (pumped by 328P002A/B) back into the top tray of the column, quenching the rising vapor and driving the water back into the liquid phase.**

# **2\. Hydrolyzer Control (328C003):**

* # **Level Control: LIC328504 maintains the liquid level inside the hydrolyzer at a constant 50%. This 50% target is not arbitrary; it ensures a fixed volumetric liquid hold-up inside the vessel. This specific volume guarantees the precise one-hour kinetic residence time required for the relatively slow urea decomposition reaction to reach thermodynamic equilibrium.**

* # **Temperature Control (Advanced Cascade): The massive thermal energy required for the endothermic hydrolysis reaction is provided by medium-pressure (MP) steam, injected directly into the bottom of the column via control valve FV329402. The control logic employs an advanced differential temperature calculation to optimize steam usage. ## **Thermodynamic Modeling, Mass Transfer, and Reaction Kinetics**

# **The predictive modeling, simulation, and operational mastery of the Desorption and Hydrolysis section require a deep understanding of fluid phase equilibria, complex reaction kinetics, and fundamental mass transfer principles. Industrial modeling of the Stamicarbon process—often executed in advanced chemical engineering software platforms like Aspen Plus or gPROMS—relies heavily on MESH equations (Mass balance, Equilibrium relationships, Summation equations, and Heat balance) applied rigorously to each theoretical tray of the columns.**

### **The Hydrolysis Reaction Kinetics**

# **Inside the Hydrolyzer (328C003), residual urea is subjected to thermal decomposition in a water-rich, high-temperature environment. The reaction proceeds via a complex two-step kinetic mechanism. First, urea isomerizes into ammonium isocyanate, which represents the rate-limiting step of the process. Second, the isocyanate reacts extremely rapidly with water to form ammonia and carbon dioxide:**

# **$$NH\_2CONH\_2 \\rightleftharpoons NH\_4^+ \+ NCO^-$$**

# **$$NH\_4^+ \+ NCO^- \+ H\_2O \\rightleftharpoons 2NH\_3 \+ CO\_2$$**

# **The overall global reaction is highly endothermic. The kinetic rate of consumption of urea ($r\_{urea}$) is typically modeled using a pseudo-first-order kinetic equation, which is heavily dependent on the Arrhenius temperature relationship and the activity coefficients of the chemical species in the highly non-ideal liquid phase :**

# **$$r\_{urea} \= k \\cdot x\_{urea}$$**

# **Where $k$ is the reaction rate constant and $x\_{urea}$ is the concentration of urea. Because the reaction is fully reversible, the continuous stripping of the product gases ($NH\_3$ and $CO\_2$) by the injected MP steam bubbles forces the equilibrium drastically to the right (in accordance with Le Chatelier's Principle). This continuous removal of products allows the reaction to achieve near-total conversion, reducing urea concentrations from bulk percentages down to parts-per-million levels. The 50% liquid level maintained by LIC328504 provides the physical volume necessary for the residence time variable ($t$) to satisfy the kinetic integration $\\int r\_{urea} \\, dt$ required for complete hydrolysis.**

### **Mass Transfer in Sieve Trays**

# **The physical separation of the ammonia and carbon dioxide from the aqueous phase in Desorber I (15 trays), the Hydrolyzer (17 trays), and Desorber II (22 trays) is facilitated by sieve trays equipped with downcomers. The liquid condensate flows horizontally across the perforated metal tray, creating a liquid hold-up layer, before dropping through the downcomer to the tray below. Simultaneously, hot steam and flashed vapor rise vertically through the 6 mm perforations (sieve holes), bubbling violently through the liquid layer to create an intensely turbulent froth regime.**

# **This intimate vapor-liquid contact maximizes the interfacial surface area, facilitating rapid mass transfer of ammonia from the liquid to the vapor phase. The efficiency of this mass transfer is thermodynamically modeled using the Murphree Tray Efficiency ($E\_{MV}$), which mathematically quantifies how closely the vapor leaving a specific tray approaches theoretical thermodynamic equilibrium with the liquid leaving that same tray :**

# **$$E\_{MV} \= \\frac{y\_n \- y\_{n-1}}{y\_n^\* \- y\_{n-1}}$$**

# **Where $y\_n$ is the actual mole fraction of the component in the vapor leaving tray $n$, $y\_{n-1}$ is the mole fraction entering from the tray below, and $y\_n^\*$ is the theoretical equilibrium mole fraction predicted by Henry's Law and Raoult's Law. The Stamicarbon column design meticulously optimizes the hole diameter, weir height, and downcomer clearance to maximize $E\_{MV}$ without inducing catastrophic column flooding or excessive liquid entrainment.**

### **Vapor-Liquid Equilibrium (VLE) Non-Ideality**

# **The multi-component chemical system comprising $NH\_3 \- CO\_2 \- H\_2O \- \\text{Urea}$ exhibits extreme thermodynamic non-ideality due to powerful ionic interactions, reversible carbamate formation, and extensive hydrogen bonding in the aqueous phase. As temperatures decrease in the downstream condensers (like the Reflux Condenser 328E004), gaseous ammonia and carbon dioxide readily react to form liquid ammonium carbamate.**

# **This highly complex phase behavior is modeled mathematically using rigorous thermodynamic equations of state and activity coefficient models, such as the SR-POLAR (Soave-Redlich-Kwong with polar extensions) or modified UNIQUAC property packages. These sophisticated thermodynamic models are absolutely essential for process engineers to accurately predict the phase envelopes (bubble points and dew points) of the mixtures. Accurately modeling these envelopes ensures that the overarching pressure controllers (like PIC328202) are programmed to setpoints that prevent the premature crystallization of solid carbamate salts in the piping networks, which would rapidly cause catastrophic blockages and plant shutdowns.**

## **Conclusion**

# **The Stamicarbon Desorption and Hydrolysis section (System 328\) represents a masterclass in modern chemical engineering, seamlessly integrating extreme thermodynamic environments, rigorous mechanical equipment, and highly non-ideal physical chemistry. Through a meticulously orchestrated cascade of heat exchangers—specifically the highly efficient, corrugated plate-and-frame architectures of 328E007, 328E021A/B, and 328E001—the system achieves exceptional internal thermal integration. By capturing sensible heat from the discharging purified effluents and recycling it to the raw feed, the design drastically minimizes the external utility steam required to drive the highly endothermic hydrolysis of urea.**

# **Furthermore, the hydraulic stability provided by the heavy-duty API 610 compliant KSB centrifugal pumps (328P003A/B and 328P006A/B) ensures that kinetic residence times within the high-pressure hydrolyzer are strictly maintained, enabling the pseudo-first-order decomposition reactions to run to absolute completion. Governed by a sophisticated network of cascaded Level, Temperature, and Pressure controllers mapped out in the P\&ID topology, the system safely and automatically navigates the hazardous, highly corrosive interplay of high-pressure ammonia and carbon dioxide. Ultimately, the engineering synergy of System 328 converts a highly toxic, environmentally destructive process condensate into a benign, reusable water source, solidifying the Stamicarbon total-recycle $CO\_2$-stripping technology's position as a sustainable, zero-effluent benchmark in global urea production.**

## **Introduction to the Urea Production Process and Control Architecture**

The modern industrial production of urea fundamentally relies on the Stamicarbon total-recycle carbon dioxide stripping process, representing a highly optimized closed-loop thermodynamic system designed to maximize conversion yields while minimizing utility consumption and environmental discharge. The foundational chemistry of this process involves a rapid, highly exothermic reaction between liquid ammonia and gaseous carbon dioxide to form an intermediate ammonium carbamate, followed by a slower, endothermic dehydration of the carbamate into urea and water. Because the dehydration reaction is equilibrium-limited and does not proceed to completion, unconverted reactants must be continuously separated from the urea product and recycled back into the high-pressure synthesis loop.  
This unyielding requirement mandates the division of the plant into distinct operational zones, each governed by its own specialized Distributed Control System (DCS) architecture. These zones typically include high-pressure synthesis, low-pressure recirculation, deep-vacuum evaporation, and high-temperature desorption and hydrolysis. The phase behavior of the quaternary system comprising ammonia, carbon dioxide, water, and urea is highly non-ideal. The introduction of water changes the azeotropy of the mixture and establishes maximum dew points and bubble points, often referred to as the top-ridge line. The entire DCS framework is mapped and calibrated to manipulate these phase boundaries, leveraging pressure drops to induce flashing, and utilizing precise temperature control to force either the dissociation of carbamate or the evaporation of water without triggering the thermal degradation of the urea product.  
In the synthesis loop, which operates at approximately 140 bar, the bulk of the conversion occurs, and a high-pressure stripper removes the majority of the unreacted ammonia and carbon dioxide. The effluent from the synthesis loop, which still contains residual carbamate and dissolved gases, is subsequently flashed and processed in the recirculation section. The resulting aqueous urea solution is then sent to the evaporation section, where multiple stages of deep vacuum evaporation concentrate the product from approximately 80 percent by weight to a 98.6 percent urea melt suitable for fluid-bed granulation. Simultaneously, the process condensate generated during these flashing and evaporation steps—which is heavily contaminated with ammonia, carbon dioxide, and entrained urea—must be rigorously treated in the desorption and hydrolysis section. High-temperature steam stripping and thermal decomposition reduce the effluent concentrations to less than five parts per million of ammonia and one part per million of urea before the water is discharged or reused as cooling tower makeup.  
The sophisticated coordination of these highly integrated thermodynamic and chemical processes is entirely dependent on the DCS. A meticulous mapping of the equipment and instrumentation within the recirculation, evaporation, and desorption sections reveals a complex network of cascade controls, split-range loops, and precise thermodynamic regulation designed to maintain the delicate balance of the plant.

## **Recirculation Stage Analysis**

The primary function of the recirculation stage is to extract the remaining unconverted reactants from the urea solution exiting the high-pressure stripper and condense them into a recoverable liquid carbamate solution for recycling. This section operates at significantly lower pressures than the synthesis loop, relying on precise temperature manipulation and sudden depressurization to force the dissociation of ammonium carbamate while preventing the formation of undesired byproducts.

### **Process Dynamics and Thermodynamic Control of Recirculation**

The urea-carbamate solution discharged from the bottom of the high-pressure heat exchanger is initially expanded across a dedicated level control valve, dropping the system pressure to approximately 4.1 bar. This sudden depressurization forces a flash vaporization event that violently cools the solution from $173^{\\circ}\\text{C}$ down to roughly $119^{\\circ}\\text{C}$, simultaneously causing a significant portion of the remaining ammonium carbamate to decompose into ammonia and carbon dioxide gas. The resulting multiphase mixture is injected tangentially into the top of the rectifying column. As the liquid descends through a packed bed of Pall rings within the column, it contacts rising vapours that effectively strip out additional reactants from the descending liquid phase.  
To ensure maximum carbamate dissociation, the solution flows from the bottom of the rectifying column into a recirculation heater, where low-pressure steam raises its temperature back up to $135^{\\circ}\\text{C}$. The system is delicately balanced at this juncture; exceeding $140^{\\circ}\\text{C}$ would rapidly accelerate the formation of biuret, an undesirable and toxic byproduct that severely degrades the final fertilizer quality. The stripped urea solution then passes through another level control boundary into a flash tank operating at near-atmospheric pressure, precisely regulated at 1.13 bar. This secondary flashing further cools the solution to $106^{\\circ}\\text{C}$ and evaporates additional water and ammonia before the solution enters the pre-evaporator, where it is concentrated to 80.4 percent by weight.  
The gases liberated from the rectifying column, primarily consisting of ammonia, carbon dioxide, and water vapour, are directed into the low-pressure carbamate condenser. Here, they are mixed with a lean carbamate solution recycled from the desorption section. The condensation of these gases into a liquid carbamate solution is a highly exothermic process, releasing significant thermal energy that must be continuously removed by a closed-loop conditioned cooling water system. A highly sophisticated control strategy is required for this heat rejection: the cooling water temperature must be maintained strictly near $55^{\\circ}\\text{C}$. If the temperature drops below this operating range, the carbamate will precipitate and crystallize, severely scaling the condenser tubes and crippling heat transfer. Conversely, if the temperature is too high, inadequate condensation occurs, increasing the system pressure and overloading downstream atmospheric scrubbers.

## **Evaporation Stage Analysis**

The evaporation section exists to elevate the intermediate urea concentration from an aqueous state to an almost entirely dehydrated melt, suitable for final product formation. Because the fundamental melting point of pure urea is $132.6^{\\circ}\\text{C}$, the concentration process must operate above this temperature boundary to prevent the product from crystallizing and solidifying inside the equipment. However, prolonged exposure to temperatures above $140^{\\circ}\\text{C}$ exponentially increases the rate of biuret formation. To remove the strongly bound water rapidly at these relatively low temperatures, the system employs a staged, deep-vacuum architecture driven by multiple, cascading steam ejectors.

### **Process Dynamics of Vacuum Evaporation**

The urea solution, entering from the pre-evaporator at approximately 80.4 percent by weight, is pumped from the small compartment of the urea solution tank into the first evaporation stage. The tank is deliberately compartmentalized by design to strictly minimize the residence time of the bulk flow, thereby suppressing the undesirable hydrolysis and biuret generation that occurs during extended thermal exposure.  
In the first stage, Evaporator I operates at an absolute pressure of 0.33 bar and is heated by low-pressure steam to exactly $130^{\\circ}\\text{C}$. The aggressively boiling solution enters the first separator, where the sudden volumetric expansion allows the bulk of the water to flash off into the vapour phase. A specialized droplet separator equipped with directional vanes captures entrained urea mist, returning it to the liquid phase while the vapours proceed to the first-stage condenser. To prevent localized accumulation and blockages, this droplet separator is equipped with a spray ring header that periodically flushes the vanes with process condensate. The effluent liquid from this stage reaches a precise concentration of 95 wt.%, containing approximately 0.7 wt.% biuret and 4.0 wt.% water.  
The 95 wt.% solution is continuously drawn via a barometric leg into Evaporator II, effectively utilizing the differential pressure between the two stages. This second stage requires extreme conditions to drive off the remaining strongly bound moisture. Utilizing medium-pressure (9 bar) steam, the temperature is elevated to the critical boundary of $140^{\\circ}\\text{C}$ while the system pressure is aggressively pulled down to 0.13 bar by a secondary array of steam ejectors and condensers. The liquid undergoes a final flash separation in Separator II, yielding a 98.6 wt.% urea melt. To prevent channeling and ensure a uniform liquid film along the heat exchanger walls, the inlet tubes of the second-stage evaporator are fitted with specialized 7 millimeter orifice plugs. The final melt, containing a mere 1.4 wt.% water and 0.9 wt.% biuret, drops directly into the barometric suction leg of the urea melt pumps. Prior to being pumped to the fluid-bed granulators, liquid urea-formaldehyde is injected into the melt at a continuous rate of 0.5 wt.% to act as an essential granulation aid and long-term anti-caking agent.  
The vacuum generation relies entirely on steam ejectors, as the high volume of non-condensable gases and the necessity for deep vacuum preclude the use of mechanical vacuum pumps. The false air injection control loops govern these ejectors. By introducing controlled amounts of atmospheric air into the ejector suction lines, the DCS can reduce the effective vacuum pulled by the continuous ejector operation, allowing for highly granular, precise pressure tuning. This directly dictates the boiling point of the urea solution, maintaining the critical $140^{\\circ}\\text{C}$ maximum boundary while ensuring sufficient moisture removal.

## **Desorption and Absorption Stage Analysis**

Water introduced into the system—whether originating from steam condensation, pump seals, or generated by the dehydration of carbamate—must eventually be removed to maintain the continuous mass balance of the plant. However, this process condensate is thoroughly saturated with dissolved ammonia, carbon dioxide, and entrained urea. The desorption and hydrolysis stage is meticulously designed to strip these volatile components and thermally destroy the dissolved urea, allowing the recovery of the reactants and the environmental discharge of completely clean water.

### **Process Dynamics of Desorption and Hydrolysis**

Contaminated process condensate from various plant drains, condenser barometric legs, and scrubbers accumulates in the partitioned ammonia water tank. The bulk condensate is pumped through a primary feed/effluent heat exchanger, recovering sensible heat from the purified water exiting the system, which elevates the feed temperature to $114^{\\circ}\\text{C}$. The preheated stream enters the top of Desorption Column I. As it descends through the column's 15 sieve trays, it contacts hot, rising vapours generated by the downstream units. This aggressive counter-current stripping action drives the bulk of the free ammonia and carbon dioxide out of the liquid phase. The top temperature of this column is meticulously controlled at $117^{\\circ}\\text{C}$ via a reflux stream of lean carbamate; this ensures the overhead vapour contains exactly the correct ratio of water to prevent crystallization in the downstream reflux condenser.  
The effluent from the bottom of Desorption Column I still contains trace amounts of ammonia and all of the dissolved urea. To address the urea, the liquid is pumped through a high-pressure heat exchanger and injected into the Hydrolyzer. Operating under severe thermodynamic conditions—$200^{\\circ}\\text{C}$ and 16.8 bar—the hydrolyzer forces the reverse of the urea synthesis reaction. Medium-pressure steam is injected into the bottom of the vessel, providing the immense thermal energy necessary to break the stable urea molecules back into ammonia and carbon dioxide. The control strategy here relies on an ingenious temperature-difference algorithm. Measuring the delta-T between the bottom liquid and the third tray ensures that the bulk of the endothermic reaction has completed in the upper and middle trays, and the bottom is simply stripping out the last traces of ammonia. If the delta-T drops, the reaction front is moving too far down the column, risking urea breakthrough into the final effluent.  
The fully hydrolyzed liquid, now containing only dissolved gases, flows into the top of Desorption Column II. Low-pressure steam is injected underneath the 22nd tray, executing a final, aggressive stripping operation. The liquid emerging from the bottom of this column is purified process condensate, boasting a concentration of less than 5 ppm ammonia and 1 ppm urea. This clean water transfers its thermal energy to the incoming feed in the primary heat exchanger before being safely discharged to the cooling tower makeup system.  
Concurrently, the overhead vapours from Desorption Column I are entirely condensed in the Reflux Condenser. The resulting lean carbamate solution is collected in a level tank, where a precise portion is utilized as reflux for the first desorber, and the remainder is pumped back to the LP carbamate condenser in the recirculation section to resume the synthesis cycle. Any non-condensable inert gases accumulating in these systems are routed to the LP Absorber and the Atmospheric Absorber, where counter-current water scrubbing captures the last traces of ammonia before the inert gases are safely vented to the atmosphere.

## **Conclusion**

The operational efficacy of the urea synthesis plant is deeply rooted in the continuous, real-time interplay between its thermodynamic requirements and the Distributed Control System. A granular review of the recirculation, evaporation, and desorption/absorption sections demonstrates a highly refined engineering philosophy designed to maximize resource recovery while minimizing degradation of the final product. The recirculation stage relies on precisely controlled decompression and heat integration to efficiently recycle unreacted ammonia and carbon dioxide without generating excessive biuret. The evaporation stage takes advantage of highly complex cascade concentration-to-vacuum controls and false air injection to drive off stubbornly bound water at relatively low temperatures, ensuring the structural and chemical integrity of the final 98.6 wt.% urea melt.  
Finally, the desorption and hydrolysis architecture applies extreme thermal conditions and intricate temperature-differential steam controls to chemically destroy dissolved urea, ensuring full reactant recovery and the strict environmental compliance of the effluent water. Together, the comprehensively mapped equipment, instrumentation, and control logic form a cohesive, closed-loop network that perfectly balances chemical equilibrium, thermal efficiency, product quality, and environmental safety.
