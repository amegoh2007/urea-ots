# Stamicarbon Urea Production Process: High-Pressure Equipment Technical Reference

## 1. High-Pressure (HP) Scrubber (322-E-003)

The High-Pressure (HP) Scrubber (typically tagged as **322-E-003** in standard Stamicarbon urea plants) is a critical component of the high-pressure synthesis loop. Operating at the full synthesis pressure (approximately 140 to 145 bar), its primary role is to recover unconverted ammonia ($NH_3$) and carbon dioxide ($CO_2$) from the inert purge gases before these gases are released from the high-pressure section.

This recovery ensures high raw material efficiency, protects downstream low-pressure equipment from gas overloading, and plays a vital role in maintaining the explosive limits of the inert gas mixture (passivation air).

![Stamicarbon urea synthesis loop High Pressure Scrubber diagram](hp_scrubber_diagram.png)

Here is a comprehensive breakdown of its role, the internal hydrodynamics, and the coupled mass and heat transfer processes.

### 1.1 Functional Role in the Synthesis Loop

In the Stamicarbon $CO_2$ stripping process, the synthesis loop is not a closed system; inert gases (such as nitrogen, oxygen from passivation air, and argon) enter with the $CO_2$ feed. To prevent these inerts from accumulating and blanketing the HP Carbamate Condenser or Reactor, they must be continuously purged.

However, the purge gas leaving the Reactor or HP Carbamate Condenser is saturated with vaporized $NH_3$, $CO_2$, and water vapor. The HP Scrubber's job is to "wash" this off-gas using a relatively cooler, lower-concentration liquid stream—typically the weak carbamate solution recycled from the Low-Pressure (LP) recirculation section.

By the time the gas exits the top of 322-E-003, the bulk of the valuable $NH_3$ and $CO_2$ has been absorbed, leaving primarily inert gases to be throttled down to the LP absorber for final polishing. The enriched carbamate solution from the bottom of the scrubber is then fed back into the synthesis loop (usually via the HP Carbamate Condenser) by gravity or an ejector.

---

### 1.2 Internal Configuration & Hydrodynamics

The HP Scrubber usually operates as a **falling-film heat exchanger** or a **vertical shell-and-tube absorber**.

* **Tube Side (Process Side):** The inert-rich gas flows upward counter-currently against the downward-falling film of the weak carbamate solution.
* **Shell Side (Cooling Medium):** Tempered cooling water is circulated on the shell side. This cooling is absolutely essential because the absorption of $NH_3$ and $CO_2$, and the subsequent formation of ammonium carbamate, are highly exothermic.

---

### 1.3 Highlighted Mass Transfer Processes

The operation inside 322-E-003 is a textbook example of **reactive absorption**, where physical mass transfer is significantly enhanced by instantaneous chemical reactions in the liquid phase. The mass transfer can be broken down into the following interacting steps:

#### A. Gas-Phase Diffusion
$NH_3$ and $CO_2$ molecules must first diffuse from the bulk gas mixture (which is rich in non-condensable inerts) through the gas boundary layer to the gas-liquid interface. The presence of inert gases creates a mass transfer resistance, meaning the partial pressure of $NH_3$ and $CO_2$ at the interface is lower than in the bulk gas.

#### B. Interfacial Absorption and Condensation
At the gas-liquid interface, three physical phenomena occur simultaneously:
1.  **Physical Dissolution:** $NH_3$ and $CO_2$ dissolve into the aqueous liquid film, driven by the concentration gradient between the interface and the bulk liquid.
2.  **Water Condensation:** Because the weak carbamate feed from the LP section is cooler than the incoming gas stream from the reactor, water vapor from the gas phase condenses into the liquid film.
3.  **Latent Heat Release:** The condensation and dissolution processes release significant latent heat right at the interface, which must be transported through the liquid film to the tube wall.

#### C. Liquid-Phase Chemical Reaction (Enhancement Factor)
Once $NH_3$ and $CO_2$ cross the interface into the liquid film, they do not remain as dissolved gases. They immediately react to form ammonium carbamate:

$$2NH_{3(aq)} + CO_{2(aq)} \rightleftharpoons NH_2COONH_{4(aq)} + \Delta H$$

**The Mass Transfer Impact:** Because this reaction is practically instantaneous, it acts as a chemical "sink." It rapidly consumes the free $NH_3$ and $CO_2$ in the liquid boundary layer, keeping their local liquid-phase concentrations near zero. This maintains an aggressively steep concentration gradient across the interface. In mass transfer theory, this increases the **Enhancement Factor ($E$)**, allowing the absorption rate to far exceed what would be possible through purely physical absorption (Henry’s Law).

![Reactive absorption mass transfer concentration profile with chemical sink](reactive_absorption_profile.png)

#### D. Mass Transfer Limitations (The Role of Heat)
The mass transfer in 322-E-003 is intimately coupled with heat transfer. The formation of ammonium carbamate releases substantial heat (≈ 160 kJ/mol).

* If this heat is not rapidly removed across the tube wall into the cooling water, the temperature of the liquid film will rise.
* According to Le Chatelier's principle, higher temperatures shift the carbamate equilibrium backwards, increasing the equilibrium vapor pressure of $NH_3$ and $CO_2$ over the solution.
* **Consequently:** If cooling is inadequate, the driving force for mass transfer ($P_{bulk} - P_{equilibrium}$) drops to zero, absorption stops, and the scrubber experiences high "slip" (unabsorbed gases venting to the LP section).

### 1.4 Summary of Driving Forces in 322-E-003

| Process | Driving Force | Limiting Factors |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Gas-Phase Diffusion** | Partial pressure gradient ($P_{bulk} - P_{interface}$) | Boundary layer thickness, high inert gas concentration. |
| **Gas-Liquid Absorption** | Chemical potential difference | Interfacial area (wetted tube surface), contact time. |
| **Chemical Reaction** | Thermodynamics of carbamate formation | Liquid temperature (exothermic reaction requires cooling). |
| **Heat Transfer** | Temperature gradient ($T_{film} - T_{cooling\_water}$) | Scale/fouling on water side, film thickness on process side. |

Proper operation of 322-E-003 relies on balancing the weak carbamate feed rate and the tempered water cooling flow to ensure the maximum possible absorption of reactants, keeping the synthesis loop running at peak raw material efficiency.

## 2. High-Pressure (HP) Ejector (322-F-001)

The High-Pressure (HP) Ejector (typically tagged as **322-F-001** in the Stamicarbon urea process) is a remarkably elegant piece of equipment. Unlike the HP Scrubber, the HP Ejector has no moving parts and does not utilize a counter-current gas-liquid flow scheme. Instead, it operates as a **liquid-liquid jet pump**.

Its primary purpose is to transport the enriched carbamate solution from the low-pressure zone at the bottom of the HP Scrubber (322-E-003) back into the high-pressure synthesis loop—specifically routing it to the HP Carbamate Condenser.

![Liquid-liquid ejector internal diagram](ejector_diagram.png)

By utilizing the HP Ejector, the Stamicarbon design completely eliminates the need for a mechanical HP carbamate pump for this recycle stream. Pumping highly corrosive, hot ammonium carbamate at synthesis pressures is mechanically difficult and maintenance-intensive; the ejector solves this problem using fluid dynamics.

### 2.1 Functional Role & The Venturi Effect

The HP Ejector relies on the kinetic energy of a motive fluid to entrain and compress a suction fluid.

* **Motive Fluid:** High-pressure liquid ammonia ($NH_3$) delivered directly from the HP Ammonia Feed Pump at pressures significantly higher than the synthesis loop (often 150 bar to 160 bar).
* **Suction Fluid:** The enriched ammonium carbamate solution dripping from the bottom of the HP Scrubber, which is slightly below the synthesis loop pressure due to system pressure drops.

The operation is governed by Bernoulli's principle for incompressible fluids:

$$P + \frac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho gh = \text{constant}$$

The motive liquid $NH_3$ is forced through a converging nozzle. As its cross-sectional area decreases, its velocity ($v$) increases dramatically, causing a proportional drop in localized static pressure ($P$). This low-pressure zone in the suction chamber draws in the enriched carbamate solution. The two fluids then enter the mixing throat, and finally pass through a diverging diffuser where the kinetic energy of the mixed fluid is converted back into static pressure, sufficient to push the mixture into the HP Carbamate Condenser.

---

### 2.2 Highlighted Mass Transfer & Mixing Processes

While the HP Scrubber is a classic gas-liquid reactive absorption column, the HP Ejector is primarily a **momentum transfer** device. However, because two distinct chemical streams are being violently combined, intense **liquid-liquid mass transfer** and mixing occur.

![Fluid velocity and turbulence profile in an ejector throat](ejector_turbulence_profile.png)

The mass transfer inside 322-F-001 can be categorized into the following interacting steps:

#### A. Macroscopic Mixing (Turbulent Shear)
As the high-velocity jet of liquid $NH_3$ (often exceeding 30 m/s) exits the motive nozzle, it creates immense shear forces against the relatively slow-moving carbamate solution entering the suction chamber.
* This shear rips the carbamate fluid into smaller and smaller eddies.
* Convective mass transfer dominates here, as bulk volumes of pure liquid $NH_3$ and carbamate solution are folded into one another inside the mixing throat.

#### B. Micro-Mixing and Molecular Diffusion
Once the bulk fluids are broken down into micro-eddies, mass transfer transitions to the molecular level.
* Liquid $NH_3$ must diffuse across the concentration gradient into the aqueous carbamate matrix.
* Because both fluids are liquids, the diffusion coefficients are orders of magnitude lower than in gas-phase systems. However, the exceedingly high interfacial area generated by the violent turbulent shear compensates for this, allowing the mixture to approach a homogeneous concentration profile before it exits the diffuser.

#### C. Localized Flashing and Direct-Contact Condensation (Two-Phase Mass Transfer)
Although designed to operate as a liquid-liquid system, extreme localized pressure drops can induce phase changes.
* **Cavitation/Flashing:** If the static pressure at the tip of the motive nozzle drops below the vapor pressure of the hot carbamate mixture, localized flashing occurs. Water, $NH_3$, and $CO_2$ rapidly vaporize into micro-bubbles.
* **Condensation:** As the fluid travels into the diffuser and the static pressure recovers, these vapor bubbles aggressively collapse back into the liquid phase. This involves intense, instantaneous gas-to-liquid mass transfer and direct-contact condensation.
* *Note:* While minor flashing aids in mixing, excessive cavitation is highly detrimental as the collapsing bubbles cause severe mechanical erosion to the ejector's internal throat and diffuser walls.

#### D. Heat of Mixing (Thermal Transfer)
The mass transfer is coupled with heat transfer. The motive $NH_3$ is typically colder than the warm carbamate coming from the scrubber. As the pure $NH_3$ diffuses into the carbamate solution, the heat of mixing alters the local fluid density and viscosity. This thermal equilibration happens concurrently with the concentration equilibration in the mixing throat.

---

### 2.3 Summary of Driving Forces in 322-F-001

| Process | Primary Driving Force | Limiting Factors |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Fluid Entrainment** | Pressure gradient ($\Delta P$) created by kinetic energy | Motive $NH_3$ supply pressure, nozzle wear. |
| **Macroscopic Mixing** | Momentum transfer and turbulent shear forces | Fluid viscosity, geometry of the mixing throat. |
| **Molecular Diffusion** | Concentration gradient between $NH_3$ and carbamate | Low liquid-phase diffusivity, residence time in the ejector. |
| **Flashing/Condensation** | Static pressure dropping below/above fluid vapor pressure | Suction fluid temperature, ejector discharge pressure. |

Ultimately, the mass transfer inside the HP Ejector is a rapid, violent homogenization process. It ensures that the fresh ammonia feed and the recycled carbamate are thoroughly integrated into a single, uniform liquid phase before entering the highly sensitive environment of the HP Carbamate Condenser.

## 3. High-Pressure (HP) Stripper (322-E-001)

The High-Pressure (HP) Stripper (typically tagged as **322-E-001**) is the defining technological advancement of the Stamicarbon $CO_2$ stripping process. Operating at the full synthesis loop pressure (approximately 140 to 145 bar), it eliminates the need to depressurize the reactor effluent to separate the unconverted reactants from the urea product.

Its primary role is to thermally decompose unconverted ammonium carbamate and aggressively strip the resulting ammonia ($NH_3$) and carbon dioxide ($CO_2$) out of the liquid phase, returning them to the high-pressure loop.

### 3.1 Hydrodynamics and Material Transfer

The HP Stripper operates as a **vertical falling-film shell-and-tube heat exchanger** utilizing two-phase counter-current flow.

* **Liquid Phase (Downwards):** The reactor effluent (a mixture of urea, water, unconverted carbamate, and free ammonia) enters the top liquid divider. It is distributed uniformly to form a thin liquid film falling down the inner walls of the tubes (typically Sandvik 2RE69 or Safurex material to withstand the extreme corrosivity).
* **Gas Phase (Upwards):** Fresh, high-pressure $CO_2$ gas from the compressor enters the bottom of the stripper and flows upward through the hollow core of the tubes, acting as the stripping agent.
* **Heating Medium (Shell Side):** High-pressure saturated steam (typically 20-25 bar) condenses on the outside of the tubes.

#### The Chemical Reaction (Material Transfer)
As the liquid film heats up, the unconverted ammonium carbamate undergoes an endothermic decomposition reaction within the liquid phase:

$$NH_2COONH_4 (l) \rightleftharpoons 2NH_3 (aq) + CO_2 (aq) \quad \Delta H > 0$$

*Note: A secondary, much slower reaction (urea hydrolysis and biuret formation) also occurs, but carbamate decomposition dominates the material balances in this vessel.*

### 3.2 Coupled Heat and Mass Transfer

The stripper's efficiency relies on the precise synchronization of heat flux from the shell and mass transfer at the gas-liquid interface.

#### Heat Transfer
Heat flows from the condensing steam on the shell, through the tube wall, and into the liquid film. This heat flux ($q$) serves three purposes:
1.  **Sensible Heat:** Raises the liquid effluent temperature to ≈ 205-210°C.
2.  **Heat of Reaction:** Provides the substantial energy required to break the carbamate bonds.
3.  **Latent Heat of Vaporization:** Vaporizes the generated $NH_3$ and $CO_2$, as well as a significant amount of $H_2O$.

#### Mass Transfer (The "Stripping" Effect)
The genius of the Stamicarbon design is the use of $CO_2$ as a stripping agent.
1.  As fresh $CO_2$ flows upward, it acts as a diluent for the $NH_3$ in the vapor phase, drastically lowering the partial pressure of $NH_3$ in the gas core.
2.  According to Henry's Law, this maximizes the concentration gradient (driving force) between the dissolved $NH_3$ in the liquid film and the gas core.
3.  As $NH_3$ aggressively desorbs into the gas phase, Le Chatelier's principle dictates that the liquid-phase carbamate equilibrium must shift to the right, driving further decomposition without requiring a drop in total system pressure.

### 3.3 Modelling Equations (1D Boundary Value Problem)

To mathematically model 322-E-001, the tube bundle is treated as a 1D distributed-parameter system along the axial coordinate $z$ (from $z=0$ at the top to $z=L$ at the bottom).

#### A. Mass and Species Balances
For a given component $i$ (Urea, $H_2O$, $NH_3$, $CO_2$, Carbamate) in the liquid phase ($L$) and gas phase ($G$):

**Liquid Phase:**
$$\frac{d(\dot{m}_L x_i)}{dz} = \left( r_i M_{w,i} \frac{\pi D^2}{4} \epsilon_L \right) - J_i \pi D$$

**Gas Phase (Counter-current, so flows opposite to $z$):**
$$-\frac{d(\dot{m}_G y_i)}{dz} = J_i \pi D$$

*Where:*
* $\dot{m}$ = Mass flow rate (kg/s)
* $x_i, y_i$ = Mass fractions in liquid and gas
* $r_i$ = Net reaction generation rate (mol/m³·s)
* $M_{w,i}$ = Molecular weight
* $J_i$ = Interfacial mass transfer flux (kg/m²·s)
* $D$ = Tube inner diameter (m)
* $\epsilon_L$ = Liquid holdup fraction (cross-sectional area occupied by film)

#### B. Mass Transfer Flux ($J_i$)
The mass transfer is typically modeled using the two-film theory. Because $CO_2$ and $NH_3$ transfer simultaneously, a Maxwell-Stefan approach is highly accurate, but a simplified driving force equation is often used for real-time simulation:

$$J_i = K_{G,i} M_{w,i} (P_{i}^* - P_{i, bulk})$$

*Where:*
* $K_{G,i}$ = Overall gas-phase mass transfer coefficient.
* $P_{i}^*$ = Equilibrium partial pressure of component $i$ at the interface (calculated via thermodynamic models like the Extended UNIQUAC or PC-SAFT for the urea-carbamate-water system).
* $P_{i, bulk}$ = Actual partial pressure in the bulk gas core.

#### C. Energy Balances
The heat integration requires tracking the enthalpy ($h$) profiles of both phases.

**Liquid Phase Energy Balance:**
$$\frac{d(\dot{m}_L h_L)}{dz} = U \pi D (T_{steam} - T_L) - \sum_{i} (J_i \Delta H_{vap,i}) + \left( \sum r_j \Delta H_{rxn,j} \right) \frac{\pi D^2}{4} \epsilon_L$$

**Gas Phase Energy Balance:**
$$-\frac{d(\dot{m}_G h_G)}{dz} = h_c \pi D (T_L - T_G) + \sum_{i} (J_i h_{i,vap})$$

*Where:*
* $U$ = Overall heat transfer coefficient from the steam, through the wall, to the bulk liquid film.
* $h_c$ = Convective heat transfer coefficient at the gas-liquid interface.
* $\Delta H_{vap}$ and $\Delta H_{rxn}$ = Enthalpies of vaporization and reaction.

> **Key Modeling Insight:** The boundary conditions for this BVP are "split." The liquid feed conditions are known at $z=0$ (top), while the gas feed conditions ($CO_2$ flow and temp) are known at $z=L$ (bottom). Simulating this requires an iterative shooting method or orthogonal collocation techniques to converge the profiles.
