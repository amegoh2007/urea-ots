# **Comprehensive Process Engineering and Instrumentation Analysis of Urea Solution Tank 323D002**

## **1\. Introduction to the Stamicarbon Urea Production Architecture**

The production of granular urea via the Stamicarbon total-recycle carbon dioxide stripping process represents a highly integrated feat of chemical and physical engineering. In this process architecture, the transformation of raw ammonia and carbon dioxide into fertilizer-grade urea proceeds through a series of strictly controlled reaction and separation stages. The process is initiated in the high-pressure synthesis loop, where ammonia and carbon dioxide are reacted at pressures of approximately 140 bar to form ammonium carbamate, an intermediate compound that subsequently undergoes endothermic dehydration to yield urea and water. Because the chemical equilibrium of this dehydration reaction inherently prevents total conversion, the reactor effluent contains a substantial fraction of unconverted reactants alongside the desired urea and water products.  
To achieve economical production, these unconverted reactants must be recovered and recycled. This separation is primarily achieved in the high-pressure stripper, where the counter-current flow of carbon dioxide drives the dissociation of ammonium carbamate, and subsequently in the low-pressure recirculation stage. Following the low-pressure rectifying column, which operates at approximately 4.1 bar, the resulting aqueous urea solution contains roughly 68.7% urea by weight, alongside residual water, trace ammonia, and carbon dioxide. However, for the final product to be successfully granulated in a fluid-bed granulator, the water content must be almost entirely eliminated, necessitating a highly efficient vacuum evaporation section.  
Situated at the critical atmospheric boundary between the low-pressure recirculation stage and the vacuum evaporation train is the Urea Solution Tank, designated by the equipment tag 323D002. This vessel operates not merely as a static storage reservoir, but as a dynamic, compartmentalized hydraulic buffer. It is designed to decouple the continuous upstream synthesis reactions from the downstream finishing processes, absorbing process fluctuations while strictly managing the residence time of the hot urea solution to suppress the formation of toxic byproducts. This report provides an exhaustive process engineering and instrumentation analysis of the 323D002 Urea Solution Tank, detailing its structural metallurgy, internal compartmentalization strategy, comprehensive process stream mapping, thermodynamic mass and heat transfer dynamics, and the precise distributed control system (DCS) architecture governing its operation.

## **2\. Structural Metallurgy and Mechanical Fabrication of Equipment 323D002**

The mechanical design and material selection for the 323D002 Urea Solution Tank are dictated by the aggressive chemical nature of the fluid it contains. Although the urea solution entering the tank has already been stripped of the vast majority of unconverted reactants, it still contains residual ammonia and carbon dioxide, which in the liquid phase can exist as trace ammonium carbamate. Ammonium carbamate is notoriously corrosive to standard carbon steels, rapidly attacking the metal lattice if proper passivation is not maintained.

### **2.1 Austenitic Stainless Steel Selection**

To ensure long-term structural integrity and prevent catastrophic failure, the entire 323D002 vessel is fabricated from 1.4306 grade stainless steel. In metallurgical terms, 1.4306 is an ultra-low carbon austenitic chromium-nickel steel, closely equivalent to AISI 304L. The selection of this specific alloy by the process licensor is a deliberate engineering countermeasure against intergranular corrosion. During the welding processes required to assemble the large plates of the tank, the localized heat input can cause chromium carbides to precipitate at the grain boundaries if the carbon content is too high, leading to a phenomenon known as weld decay or sensitization. By utilizing the 1.4306 alloy, which limits carbon to a maximum of 0.03%, the risk of carbide precipitation is effectively eliminated.  
Furthermore, the general corrosion resistance of this austenitic stainless steel relies on the continuous maintenance of a passive chromium oxide layer on the metal surface. In the Stamicarbon process, this passivation is supported by the continuous injection of passivation air (oxygen) into the primary carbon dioxide feed compressor. The oxygen dissolves into the process fluid and permeates the entire plant, ensuring that the internal wetted surfaces of 323D002 remain in a passive state, even when exposed to fluctuating carbamate concentrations during plant upsets.

### **2.2 Plate Dimensions and Mechanical Tolerances**

The mechanical fabrication datasheet for 323D002 reveals a robust construction designed to withstand the hydrostatic loads of an aqueous urea solution with an effective density exceeding 1,150 kg/m³. All steel plates are specified to adhere to DIN EN 10088-1/2 technical delivery conditions, with flatness conforming to DIN EN 10029 Class N standards, and surface finishes strictly meeting DIN EN 10163 Class B requirements to prevent microscopic crevices where localized pitting corrosion could initiate.

## **3\. Kinetic Management Through Compartmentalization**

The most defining architectural feature of the 323D002 Urea Solution Tank is its internal division into two distinct compartments, separated by the heavy 10.0 mm baffle plates detailed in the structural analysis. This compartmentalization is not a matter of structural convenience; rather, it is a deliberate process engineering intervention designed to control the reaction kinetics of a highly undesirable side reaction: the formation of biuret.

### **3.1 The Thermodynamics and Kinetics of Biuret Formation**

In the context of urea production, biuret ($NH\_2CONHCONH\_2$) is a parasitic byproduct formed through the endothermic, reversible dimerization of two urea molecules, a reaction that concurrently liberates one molecule of ammonia. Because biuret exhibits strong phytotoxic properties, agricultural standards strictly dictate that fertilizer-grade urea must maintain a biuret concentration well below 1.0% by weight.  
The kinetics of the forward biuret formation reaction are highly dependent on four primary variables: the concentration of urea in the solution, the partial pressure of ammonia (which drives the equilibrium backward if high), the temperature of the bulk fluid, and the residence time of the fluid at that temperature. As the urea solution exits the pre-evaporator stage and enters 323D002, it has been concentrated to 80% by weight, its temperature is approximately 99°C, and the vast majority of the protective ammonia has been flashed off into the vapor recovery system. Under these thermodynamic conditions, the urea solution is highly susceptible to rapid biuret formation if it is allowed to stagnate in a large holding vessel.

### **3.2 The Active Flow-Through Zone: Compartment I**

To actively suppress the kinetic progression of biuret formation, the 323D002 tank is designed with a small primary chamber, designated as Compartment I. This compartment holds a restricted total volumetric capacity of 80 cubic meters.  
Under normal, steady-state process conditions at 100% plant load, Compartment I serves as the exclusive active flow-through zone for the entire facility's production. The 80% urea solution descending from the separator of the pre-evaporator (323F010) feeds directly and exclusively into this small compartment. Concurrently, the primary Urea Solution Pumps (323P003A/B) draw their suction strictly from the bottom of Compartment I to pressurize the fluid and feed it forward into the first-stage vacuum evaporator (324E001).  
To further minimize residence time, standard operating protocols require the automated control system to maintain the fluid level in Compartment I at approximately 10% during stable operations. At a 10% liquid level, the active fluid volume is restricted to roughly 8 cubic meters. Given that the volumetric flow rate of the incoming feed is approximately 80.6 cubic meters per hour, the average residence time of any given urea molecule within the tank is limited to less than six minutes. This exceptionally brief hydraulic retention time effectively arrests the biuret dimerization reaction before the fluid is subjected to the higher temperatures of the downstream vacuum evaporation stages.  
In addition to the primary forward flow, Compartment I is the designated receiving point for several intermittent and recycle flows. These include the minimum continuous bypass flow from the discharge of the 323P003A/B pumps, as well as the highly concentrated urea melt recycle flow that is routed backwards from the downstream finishing section during unit startups, plant turndowns, or sudden trips of the fluid-bed granulator.

### **3.3 The Passive Buffer Zone: Compartment II**

Adjacent to the active chamber is Compartment II, a significantly larger volume boasting a capacity of 300 cubic meters. The 10.0 mm internal baffle separates this massive volume from Compartment I, functioning as an overflow weir.  
Under normal, steady-state production parameters, Compartment II is maintained entirely empty (0% level). It acts as a passive, dry emergency storage buffer. Fluid only enters Compartment II if the level in Compartment I exceeds the height of the internal baffle, an event that triggers high-level alarms in the distributed control system. The utilization of Compartment II is strictly reserved for specific operational anomalies and maintenance interventions:

1. **Finishing Section Decoupling:** If the downstream vacuum evaporation train or the fluid-bed granulation package experiences a sudden mechanical failure or requires a short-term shutdown for maintenance, the 300 cubic meter buffer allows the upstream high-pressure synthesis and low-pressure recirculation sections to continue operating uninterrupted. The continuous production of 80% urea solution overflows the baffle into Compartment II, decoupling the front end of the plant from the back end until the massive storage capacity is entirely exhausted.  
2. **Synthesis Section Evacuation:** If the high-pressure synthesis loop (comprising the reactor, stripper, and carbamate condenser) requires a complete shutdown and depressurization—a procedure that can span six to eight hours—Compartment II serves as the primary receiving vessel. It possesses the volumetric capacity to hold the massive inventory of valuable urea solution evacuated from the synthesis vessels, preventing environmental discharge and mitigating total product loss.

## **5\. Thermodynamic Mass and Heat Transfer Dynamics**

While 323D002 operates as an atmospheric vessel, the fluid dynamics within Compartment I are characterized by continuous sensible heat transfer and, during non-standard operations, violent mass transfer driven by phase equilibria shifts.

### **5.1 Steady-State Thermal Profiles**

Under continuous 100% capacity operations, the 80% urea solution enters the tank at 99°C. The tank is physically vented to the plant's atmospheric vent stack (328V001), ensuring that the internal pressure remains strictly isobaric with the atmosphere. Because the fluid residence time is tightly constrained to a few minutes, bulk thermal decay via sensible heat transfer through the stainless steel shell to the ambient environment is minimal. This rapid throughput is essential to prevent the solution from cooling toward its crystallization point, as the solubility curve of an 80% water-urea binary mixture dictates that solid urea will precipitate rapidly if the fluid temperature drops significantly.

### **5.2 Dynamic Equilibria During Recycle Operations**

The thermodynamic stability of the tank is severely disrupted when the downstream evaporation or granulation sections are placed on recycle. During such an event, the split-range level control valve LV324501B opens, injecting Stream 609—a 98.6% urea melt at 140°C—directly into Compartment I.  
The introduction of this high-enthalpy, high-concentration stream induces rapid sensible heat transfer, elevating the bulk temperature of the fluid in Compartment I. Concurrently, a critical mass transfer phenomenon occurs. At 140°C, the vapor pressure of the water dissolved in the 80% bulk solution rises sharply. The sudden increase in thermal energy causes flash evaporation of water at the liquid-vapor interface, transferring water mass from the liquid phase into the tank's headspace, where it is vented to 328V001.  
**The Crystallization Paradox and the Dilution Mechanism:** This flash evaporation creates a perilous paradox. As water vaporizes, the bulk concentration of urea in the tank escalates. If the highly concentrated solution is subsequently drawn by the 323P003A/B pumps and experiences any sensible heat loss in the piping, the thermodynamic state of the fluid will cross the saturation curve, resulting in the instantaneous, catastrophic crystallization of solid urea. The formation of solid urea blocks the pump suction lines, causing severe cavitation, mechanical seal failure, and an inevitable total plant shutdown.  
To arrest this mass and heat transfer upset, process engineering protocols dictate a precise intervention. Whenever the melt recycle line is active, operators must manually or automatically inject a continuous stream of cool steam condensate into the recycle line immediately downstream of valve LV324501B. The injected condensate serves a dual thermodynamic purpose. First, it absorbs sensible heat from the 140°C melt, suppressing the overall temperature rise in the tank and halting the flash evaporation of the bulk fluid. Second, through direct mass transfer, it dilutes the concentration of the returning melt back down to a safe margin, ensuring that the bulk fluid in Compartment I remains securely within the stable liquid phase region of the phase diagram.

## **6\. Instrumentation, DCS Architecture, and Control Logic**

The successful, automated management of the Urea Solution Tank relies on a sophisticated network of sensors, transmitters, and pneumatically actuated control valves coordinated through the plant's Distributed Control System (DCS). A meticulous analysis of the Process and Instrumentation Diagram (P\&ID No. 105/1) and the operating manual reveals the precise control architecture governing equipment 323D002.

### **6.1 Level Measurement via Pneumatic Purge**

Measuring the level of highly concentrated urea solutions presents a severe instrumentation challenge. Traditional differential pressure (DP) transmitters rely on static fluid columns in their impulse lines to transmit hydrostatic head pressure to the sensor diaphragm. However, if an 80% urea solution is allowed to enter a static, unheated impulse line, it will rapidly cool and crystallize, plugging the line and rendering the transmitter useless.  
To circumvent this, the level transmitters on 323D002 employ a continuous pneumatic purge mechanism. The P\&ID indicates that the instrumentation is tied to line **32318002**, which is explicitly labeled "FROM INSTRUMENT AIR HEADER". Clean, dry instrument air is metered into the impulse lines leading to the tank nozzles. This air slowly bubbles into the urea solution inside the compartments. The backpressure generated within the impulse line is directly proportional to the hydrostatic head of the fluid above the bubbling point. The DP transmitters measure this clean air backpressure, thereby completely isolating their sensitive diaphragms from the corrosive, crystallization-prone urea fluid. Additional connections surrounding the tank, such as 3295-10-1-17, 3295-10-1-18, and 3290-10-3-3, further map the complex network of instrument air and utility tracing required to keep the sensor nodes operational.

### **6.2 Primary Control Loops and Cascade Algorithms**

The DCS architecture for 323D002 utilizes advanced cascade control algorithms to seamlessly balance the hydraulic throughput of the facility.  
**Compartment I Level Control Loop (LIC323507):**

* **Primary Function:** Senses and controls the liquid level in the active 80 cubic meter compartment.  
* **Alarms:** Configured in the DCS with High Level Alarm (LAH) and Low Level Alarm (LAL) thresholds to alert the control room of mass-balance deviations.  
* **Cascade Architecture:** LIC323507 operates as the master controller in a cascade loop. Rather than manipulating a valve directly, its output provides a dynamic, remote setpoint to a secondary slave controller, **FIC324401**.  
* **Execution:** FIC324401 continuously measures the actual discharge flow of the Urea Solution Pumps (323P003A/B) utilizing a flow element. It modulates the pneumatic control valve **FV324401**, which is located downstream in the feed line to Evaporator I. As the level in Compartment I naturally fluctuates with upstream production, LIC323507 commands FIC324401 to proportionally increase or decrease the flow rate, ensuring a perfectly smooth, self-balancing feed to the vacuum evaporators without sudden hydraulic shocks.

**Compartment II Level Indication (LI323504):**

* **Primary Function:** Monitors the fluid depth in the passive 300 cubic meter storage compartment.  
* **Alarms:** Equipped with High and Low level alarms. This loop does not execute any automated control actions; it serves purely as an indicator. If LI323504 registers a rising level, it immediately alerts the operator that fluid is spilling over the internal baffle from Compartment I, signaling an upstream over-production event or a downstream blockage.

### **6.3 Temperature Diagnostics**

* **TI323008 (Temperature Indicator):** This instrument measures the bulk fluid temperature within Compartment I. It is equipped with a Low Temperature Alarm (TAL). A dropping temperature warns operators that the solution is approaching its crystallization boundary, prompting immediate investigation into upstream steam supplies or inadvertent cold condensate leakage.  
* **Upstream Thermal Control (TIC323012):** While physically located on the upstream pre-evaporator (323E010), this controller dictates the thermal baseline of 323D002. TIC323012 monitors the temperature of the fluid leaving the pre-evaporator and modulates the setpoint of the low-pressure steam controller, **PIC329208**, to maintain the feed flowing into the tank at precisely 99°C.

### **6.4 Finishing Section Split-Range Interlocks**

The ultimate destination of the fluid passing through 323D002 is the fluid-bed granulator. The flow of the final 98.6% urea melt is governed by a critical split-range controller, **LIC324501**, which senses the level in the barometric leg feeding the final melt pumps (335P001A/B).  
This controller outputs a single signal that commands two distinct valves moving in opposite directions:

* **LV324501A:** Directs the forward flow of the melt to the main distribution header of the granulator.  
* **LV324501B:** Opens to recycle the melt backward into Compartment I of the Urea Solution Tank 323D002.

During a total power failure, an instrument air failure, or a sudden trip of the granulator fans, an emergency interlock matrix engages. The logic solver immediately forces LV324501A closed and drives the recycle valve LV324501B fully open. Concurrently, the main granulator header drain valve, **HV335602**, is commanded open to evacuate the piping manifold. This automated sequence prevents the highly concentrated melt from freezing solid within the extensive piping network by dumping the entire inventory safely backward into the thermal mass of 323D002.

## **7\. Procedural Management and Operational Scenarios**

The operational procedures governing the Urea Solution Tank dictate stringent interventions to maintain the delicate mass and energy balances across all phases of the plant's lifecycle.

### **7.1 Pre-Commissioning and the Water Run**

Prior to the introduction of live ammonia and carbon dioxide into the synthesis loop, the entire facility undergoes a comprehensive pre-commissioning phase known as the "water run." This procedure simulates the hydraulic behavior of the plant, clears construction debris from the piping, and verifies the tuning of the DCS control loops.  
During the water run preparation, temporary spool pieces and flexible hoses are installed to connect the Urea Solution Tank 323D002 to the Ammonia Water Tank 328D003. Furthermore, a temporary DN150 pipe is routed from the discharge of the Urea Solution Pumps (323P003A/B) directly back to the suction of the high-pressure ammonia pumps (321P002A/B). Steam condensate is fed into 323D002 until a 25% level is achieved. The pumps are then energized in a closed loop, allowing instrumentation engineers to stroke the control valves, verify the cascade action of LIC323507 to FIC324401, and confirm that the pneumatic bubble-tubes accurately reflect the hydrostatic head.

### **7.2 The Cold Startup Sequence**

A cold startup of the facility is a highly choreographed sequence where 323D002 plays a pivotal role in matching the front-end production to the back-end finishing capacity.

1. **Synthesis Activation:** The high-pressure synthesis loop is brought online first. As the initial, weak urea solution cascades through the rectifying and pre-evaporation stages, the fluid level in Compartment I of 323D002 begins to rise.  
2. **Downstream Holding:** The operator holds the vacuum evaporators offline, allowing the fluid to accumulate until the level on LIC323507 reaches 80%. This ensures sufficient hydraulic inventory to prevent pump cavitation when the downstream sections are engaged.  
3. **Forward Circulation:** Once the 80% threshold is reached, the operator breaks the vacuum in the downstream condensers and energizes the 323P003A/B pumps, establishing a forward flow of roughly 56 $m^3$/h via FIC324401.  
4. **Recycle and Dilution:** Because the massive steel shells of the vacuum evaporators are cold, the resulting urea melt exiting the second stage will not immediately meet the strict 98.6% concentration specification required for granulation. Consequently, the split-range control logic is manipulated to close LV324501A and open the recycle valve LV324501B. The sub-spec melt is pumped continuously back into Compartment I of 323D002. As detailed in the thermodynamic analysis, the operator must simultaneously inject cooling steam condensate into the recycle line to dilute the solution, preventing catastrophic crystallization in the tank.  
5. **Steady State:** Once the evaporators achieve stable thermal profiles (130°C in Stage I, 140°C in Stage II) and the vacuum reaches ultimate depth, the concentration analyzer (AIC324701) will confirm a 98.6% melt. The operator then closes the recycle valve LV324501B and opens LV324501A, sending the melt to the granulator and transitioning the plant into steady-state operation.

### **7.3 Managing Plant Shutdowns and 72-Hour Block-Ins**

When the facility faces an emergency or a planned maintenance outage, the management of the fluid inventories dictates the recovery timeline.  
If the synthesis section experiences a trip but the reactor wall temperatures remain above 125°C, the process licensor permits the synthesis loop to be "blocked-in" for a maximum of 72 hours. During this window, the high-pressure vessels hold their fluid inventories under pressure, isolated by automated block valves. Because no new fluid is flowing forward, the level in 323D002 will drop as the 323P003A/B pumps evacuate the remaining inventory to empty the downstream evaporators.  
If the outage extends beyond 72 hours, or if the reactor temperature drops below the critical 125°C threshold, the synthesis loop must be entirely depressurized and drained. This massive evacuation procedure utilizes Compartment II of 323D002. The draining sequence is strictly choreographed: the reactor is drained via the liquid outlet line of the high-pressure stripper (322E001) down through the rectifying column and eventually into 323D002. The level control valve LV322501 on the stripper is manually modulated to ensure the massive influx of fluid does not overwhelm the tank's capacity. From 323D002, the fluid is subsequently transferred to the Ammonia Water Tank (328D003) for long-term storage and eventual recovery in the desorption section.  
Once the tank is emptied, standard maintenance protocols require aggressive flushing. The Urea Solution Pumps (323P003A/B) and their associated suction and discharge piping must be flushed with hot steam condensate until sample point AE335751 yields perfectly clear condensate, guaranteeing that no residual urea remains to crystallize as the equipment cools to ambient conditions.

## **8\. Conclusion**

The 323D002 Urea Solution Tank embodies a masterful application of chemical process engineering. Through its specialized 1.4306 ultra-low carbon austenitic stainless steel construction, it provides robust resistance against the aggressive corrosive mechanisms inherent to urea and carbamate solutions. Its defining architectural feature—the dual-compartment internal geometry—brilliantly resolves the conflicting operational requirements of the Stamicarbon total-recycle process. By routing standard production strictly through the small 80 cubic meter Compartment I, the plant achieves exceptionally brief fluid residence times, effectively arresting the endothermic dimerization reaction that produces toxic biuret. Conversely, the passive 300 cubic meter Compartment II provides the massive volumetric buffer required to absorb emergency process upsets and safely receive the immense liquid inventory during synthesis loop evacuations.  
Coupled with a highly sophisticated distributed control system utilizing air-purged pneumatic level measurement, split-range recycle interlocks, and precise cascade flow architectures, 323D002 securely decouples the extreme pressures of the synthesis loop from the delicate thermal balances of the vacuum evaporation and granulation stages. Rigorous adherence to the documented thermodynamic and hydraulic procedures—particularly the condensate dilution mechanism during high-concentration melt recycling—remains absolutely imperative to prevent catastrophic crystallization, mitigate equipment fouling, and ensure the continuous, high-efficiency operation of the Helwan urea facility.
