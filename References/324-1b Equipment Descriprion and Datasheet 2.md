# **Engineering Analysis of the Evaporation and Vacuum Condensation Network in a Stamicarbon Urea Synthesis Facility**

## **Introduction to the Stamicarbon Process and Evaporation Thermodynamics**

The industrial synthesis of urea from ammonia and carbon dioxide is governed by a delicate thermodynamic equilibrium, demanding highly optimized process conditions to maximize yield and minimize energy consumption. In the Stamicarbon carbon dioxide stripping process, the synthesis section operates at elevated pressures (typically 140 to 175 bar), utilizing a high-pressure stripper, a pool condenser or pool reactor, and a high-pressure scrubber to convert the reactants into an aqueous urea solution. The effluent leaving the synthesis and subsequent medium- and low-pressure recirculation stages is an aqueous solution containing approximately 70% to 80% urea by weight, alongside residual water, unconverted ammonium carbamate, and dissolved non-condensable gases.  
To prepare this intermediate solution for final solid-phase finishing processes, such as fluid-bed granulation or prilling, the urea concentration must be elevated to a highly pure melt, typically 99.7% concentration by weight, requiring a residual moisture content of less than 0.3%. Because urea possesses a negligible vapour pressure, the partial pressure of the liquid mixture is overwhelmingly dominated by the aqueous solvent. Evaporating this massive volume of water at atmospheric pressure would mandate elevated thermal inputs. However, urea is highly sensitive to thermal degradation; prolonged exposure to high temperatures accelerates the formation of biuret, an undesirable dimer of urea that is toxic to certain agricultural crops, and simultaneously promotes the hydrolysis of urea back into ammonia and carbon dioxide.  
To suppress these parasitic degradation reactions, the Stamicarbon process employs a specialized sub-atmospheric evaporation network. By subjecting the urea solution to a deep vacuum, the boiling point of the mixture is substantially depressed, permitting the rapid volatilization of water at temperatures low enough to preserve the chemical integrity of the urea melt. The vacuum required for this operation is generated and maintained by a precisely engineered ejector-condenser train. This report provides an exhaustive analysis of the vacuum and condensation equipment within the evaporation section of the Helwan Fertilizers Company (HFC) urea plant. The scope of this analysis encompasses Condenser II (324E005), Ejector II (324F004), Condenser III (324E006), Ejector III (324F005), and Condenser IV (324E007), detailing their mechanical design, metallurgical constraints, thermodynamic heat and mass transfer mechanisms, fluid machinery hydraulics, and the sophisticated instrumentation architecture required for stable operation.

## **Mechanical Design, Metallurgy, and Fabrication Standards**

The harsh chemical environment of a urea plant necessitates stringent metallurgical and mechanical design standards. Ammonium carbamate is highly corrosive to standard carbon steels, requiring the deployment of specialized austenitic stainless steels or advanced duplex stainless steels for all process-wetted surfaces. The heat exchangers within the Helwan vacuum train (324E005, 324E006, and 324E007) are designed as vertical shell-and-tube units, adhering strictly to the thermal and mechanical guidelines of the Tubular Exchanger Manufacturers Association (TEMA) Class R standards, as well as the German AD-Merkblätter pressure vessel codes.  
The equipment is specified to utilize Stamicarbon Specification 18005, which mandates high-grade materials such as Material BB.01 and AA.01 (specialized stainless steel grades corresponding to Stamicarbon's MS 17 material specification) for the process-side components, including the shells, internal baffles, tubes, and channel flanges. The tube-side components, which circulate non-corrosive cooling water, are fabricated from standard carbon steel to optimize capital expenditure. In modern Stamicarbon iterations, the traditional 316L-UG stainless steels are frequently upgraded to E-type Safurex duplex stainless steels (such as X2CrNiMo25-22-2) to combat chloride stress corrosion cracking, a phenomenon that typically afflicts the top tubesheets of heat exchangers due to the accumulation of trace chlorides from the steam or cooling water.  
To prevent the cross-contamination of the process fluid with the cooling medium, all condensers in this network are mandated to meet a strict DK3 leakage class. The Inspection Data Sheets (IDS) for these vessels enforce rigorous non-destructive testing (NDT) protocols. The tube-to-tubesheet joints must undergo 100% liquid penetrant testing (PT) on the first layer, followed by extensive ultrasonic testing (UT) according to SEP 1915 and eddy current testing (SEP 1925 or SEP 1914\) to verify metallurgical integrity. Furthermore, the vessels are subjected to radiographic examination (RT) on the attachment seams and mandatory helium or ammonia leak detection tests prior to final hydrostatic pressurization.

### **Compositional Dynamics of the Primary Suction Stream**

Stream 712 represents the foundational suction load entering Ejector II, representing the off-gases that survived the primary cooling phase within Condenser II. The chemical composition of this vapour mixture is highly specific, consisting of 48.1% $H\_2O$, 20.4% $NH\_3$, 27.9% $CO\_2$, and 3.6% air by weight, yielding an average molecular weight of 21.6 kg/kmol.  
This compositional profile is indicative of the underlying mass transfer phenomena occurring in the upstream evaporation separator. The overwhelming prevalence of water vapour confirms the primary operational mandate of the unit: the aggressive dehydration of the urea melt. The significant concentrations of ammonia and carbon dioxide are the direct thermodynamic consequence of the equilibrium vapor pressure of the residual, unconverted ammonium carbamate, coupled with a minor degree of unavoidable urea hydrolysis triggered by the thermal input of the evaporators. As this gaseous mixture propagates through the cooling zones of the condenser train, the ammonia and carbon dioxide molecules will undergo an exothermic recombination reaction, forming aqueous ammonium carbamate in the liquid phase, which must be drained and recycled to the process.  
The presence of 3.6% air within the vapour stream introduces a critical operational challenge. In the Stamicarbon process, a continuous stream of passivation air is deliberately injected into the high-pressure synthesis section. The oxygen in this air maintains the passivated oxide layer on the austenitic stainless steel surfaces, establishing a vital metallurgical barrier against the aggressive, highly corrosive nature of the carbamate solutions. However, oxygen and nitrogen are entirely non-condensable under the thermal and pressure regimes of the evaporation section. Consequently, this passivation air acts as an inert gas load that must be continuously purged through the entire vacuum train, resisting condensation, impairing heat transfer, and ultimately venting to the atmosphere via Stream 721\.

## **Mass and Heat Transfer Mechanisms for Mathematical Modeling**

### **Condenser II (324E005): The Primary Vapor Sink**

Condenser II operates as the primary vapor sink for the second-stage evaporation cycle, representing the largest and most critical heat exchanger in the vacuum network. The unit is a vertically oriented TEMA-type exchanger featuring a shell inside diameter of 925 mm. It houses an extensive bundle of 486 stainless steel tubes, each with an outer diameter of 25 mm and a wall thickness of 1.6 mm. The effective tube length stretches to 4,900 mm, providing a massive total heat exchange surface area of 187.00 m².  
The fluid movement within Condenser II is designed to maximize convective heat transfer. Vapours evaporated from the second-stage separator evaporator (324F003) enter the shell side of the vessel through the massive 600 mm (DN) N1 inlet gas nozzle, which is welded with a 610 x 7.1 mm pipe dimension. Inside the shell, the vapours navigate through an arrangement of baffles featuring an impingement plate, which protects the tube bundle from the kinetic impact and potential erosion caused by high-velocity droplets.  
Simultaneously, the tube side is subjected to a massive, continuous flow of 470,000 kg/h of cooling water. The cooling water enters through the 300 mm N5 inlet nozzle at 30 °C, traverses the two-pass tube bundle, and exits through the 300 mm N4 outlet nozzle at 34 °C. As the low-pressure vapours contact the cold outer surfaces of the tube bundle, rapid condensation initiates. The heat transfer mechanism is twofold: the cooling water absorbs the sensible heat of the cooling gas and the latent heat of vaporization from the condensing water, while simultaneously absorbing the heat of formation generated by the chemical recombination of gaseous $NH\_3$ and $CO\_2$ into aqueous ammonium carbamate.  
This rigorous thermal exchange results in a specified heat duty of 2,122 kW. The total mass flow of 3,674 kg/h entering the shell side is largely liquefied, resulting in 3,040 kg/h of liquid process condensate at 40 °C. This liquid phase falls by gravity to the bottom head and exits through the 80 mm N3 outlet condensate nozzle. From there, it flows down a barometric leg, discharging safely into the ammonia water tank (328D003). The barometric leg is a critical physical construct; its vertical height utilizes the static head of the liquid column to create a hydraulic seal, overcoming the differential pressure between the deep vacuum of the condenser shell (0.132 bar a) and the atmospheric pressure of the receiving tank, thereby preventing catastrophic vacuum loss.  
The remaining 634 kg/h of uncondensed water vapour, ammonia, carbon dioxide, and inert passivation air is continuously drawn out through the 350 mm N2 outlet nozzle by the suction force of Ejector II. Furthermore, to mitigate the risk of flow restriction caused by the crystallization of carbamate or the entrainment and subsequent solidification of urea droplets, Condenser II is equipped with a specialized wash water injection system. Process condensate can be manually introduced into the vapour inlet line at a controlled rate of approximately 3 m³/h. This flow is continuously monitored by flow indicator FI322460 and serves to dissolve and wash away any solid deposits forming on the internal impingement plates or the upper portions of the tube bundle. Auxiliary connections on the vessel include the N6 and N7 25 mm vent nozzles, the N8 and N9 25 mm drain nozzles, and the N10 25 mm nozzle dedicated to the PI324203 pressure transmitter.

### **Determination of the Overall Heat Transfer Coefficient ($U$)**

The thermal performance of Condenser II can be mathematically modeled by determining its Overall Heat Transfer Coefficient ($U$), a metric that encapsulates the conductive resistance of the stainless steel tube walls and the convective film resistances of the boundary layers on both the shell and tube sides.  
The heat duty ($Q$) absorbed by the cooling water is derived from the mass flow ($m$), the specific heat capacity of water ($c\_p \\approx 4.184 \\text{ kJ/kg} \\cdot \\text{K}$), and the measured temperature differential ($\\Delta T$):  
$$m\_{cw} \= \\frac{470,000 \\text{ kg/h}}{3,600 \\text{ s/h}} \= 130.55 \\text{ kg/s}$$  
$$\\Delta T\_{cw} \= 34 \\, ^\\circ\\text{C} \- 30 \\, ^\\circ\\text{C} \= 4 \\text{ K}$$  
$$Q \= m\_{cw} \\cdot c\_p \\cdot \\Delta T\_{cw} \= 130.55 \\cdot 4.184 \\cdot 4 \\approx 2,185 \\text{ kW}$$  
This mathematically derived duty of 2,185 kW closely aligns with the OEM-specified nominal heat duty of 2,122 kW, with the minor variance accommodating standard safety margins and ambient thermal losses through the 30 mm shell insulation.  
To isolate the $U$-value, the Logarithmic Mean Temperature Difference (LMTD) must be established. Assuming a standardized counter-current flow profile across the exchanger:

* Hot side inlet (vapour): $T\_{h,in} \= 140 \\, ^\\circ\\text{C}$  
* Hot side outlet (vapour): $T\_{h,out} \= 40 \\, ^\\circ\\text{C}$  
* Cold side inlet (water): $T\_{c,in} \= 30 \\, ^\\circ\\text{C}$  
* Cold side outlet (water): $T\_{c,out} \= 34 \\, ^\\circ\\text{C}$

The terminal temperature differences ($\\Delta T\_1$ and $\\Delta T\_2$) are calculated as follows:  
$$\\Delta T\_1 \= T\_{h,in} \- T\_{c,out} \= 140 \- 34 \= 106 \\, ^\\circ\\text{C}$$  
$$\\Delta T\_2 \= T\_{h,out} \- T\_{c,in} \= 40 \- 30 \= 10 \\, ^\\circ\\text{C}$$  
$$\\text{LMTD} \= \\frac{\\Delta T\_1 \- \\Delta T\_2}{\\ln(\\Delta T\_1 / \\Delta T\_2)} \= \\frac{106 \- 10}{\\ln(10.6)} \= \\frac{96}{2.36} \\approx 40.66 \\, ^\\circ\\text{C}$$  
Given the established heat exchange surface area ($A$) of 187.00 m²:  
$$U \= \\frac{Q}{A \\cdot \\text{LMTD}} \= \\frac{2,122,000 \\text{ W}}{187 \\text{ m}^2 \\cdot 40.66 \\text{ K}} \= 279.06 \\text{ W/(m}^2\\text{K)}$$  
The resulting $U$-value of approximately 279 W/(m²K) is highly characteristic for vacuum condensation systems burdened with significant concentrations of non-condensable gases, such as the 3.6% passivation air present in the feed stream. In thermodynamic applications, non-condensable gases accumulate at the boundary layer of the cold tube surface, creating a stagnant, insulating gas film that drastically impedes the transfer of latent heat from the condensing vapour to the tube wall. Consequently, vacuum condensers must be engineered with substantially larger surface areas to offset this severely depressed heat transfer coefficient. Furthermore, the design incorporates a specified fouling factor of 0.00034 m²K/W for the tube side, ensuring that the condenser maintains its required duty even as cooling water deposits scale over the vessel's operational lifespan.

### **Condenser III (324E006) and Condenser IV (324E007): Inter- and After-Condensation**

Following the primary condensation phase, the subsequent inter- and after-condensers serve to sequentially strip the condensable fluids from the ejector discharge streams. By aggressively condensing the motive steam and residual process vapours into a liquid state, the actual volumetric flow of gas is drastically reduced, enabling the downstream ejector—or the final atmospheric vent—to handle the mass flow without choking.  
Condenser III (324E006) operates as the intermediate cooling stage. It is a vertical unit featuring a 550 mm diameter shell, housing 250 tubes with a 3,600 mm length and 26 mm pitch, yielding 56.50 m² of surface area. The mixed stream discharged from Ejector II enters at 77 °C through the 250 mm N1 inlet nozzle. Operating at a design pressure of 0.33 bar a (monitored by PI324257), the vapours are cooled against the tube-side cooling water. The condensation yields a liquid drain stream (Stream 759\) at 50 °C with a maximum flow of 535 kg/h, which exits through the 80 mm N3 nozzle to join the barometric leg to the ammonia water tank. The surviving non-condensable gases exit through the 80 mm N2 outlet to be entrained by Ejector III.  
Condenser IV (324E007) serves as the final after-condenser, operating at near atmospheric pressure (1.05 bar a). It is the smallest of the three units, featuring a 324 mm diameter shell, 70 tubes over a 2,500 mm length, and 11.00 m² of surface area. The discharge from Ejector III enters at 122 °C through the 80 mm N1 vapour inlet. The final condensation phase yields a minor liquid stream (Stream 722\) exiting the 50 mm N3 nozzle at a maximum rate of 31.5 kg/h and 55 °C. The entirely non-condensable inert gases, consisting primarily of the original passivation air and trace levels of unreacted ammonia, are finally vented at 45 °C through the 50 mm N2 outlet, proceeding as Stream 721 to the main atmospheric vent stack (328V001).

## **Vacuum System Ejector Dynamics and Pull Curves**

### **Compressible Fluid Dynamics of Ejector II and III**

Ejectors are highly specialized thermodynamic compressors that contain no moving parts. They operate on the principles of compressible fluid dynamics, converting the pressure energy of a motive fluid into velocity to induce a vacuum. In the Helwan plant, Ejector II (324F004) and Ejector III (324F005) utilize low-pressure saturated steam as their driving force.  
The steam (Stream 927 for Ejector II and Stream 929 for Ejector III) is supplied at 146 °C and a pressure of 4.1 bar a, entering through the 80 mm N2 steam inlet flanges. Inside the ejector body, the steam is forced through a precisely machined converging-diverging motive nozzle. As the steam passes the throat of the nozzle, it undergoes an isentropic expansion, converting its high potential pressure energy into massive kinetic energy. The steam exits the nozzle throat at supersonic velocities, typically operating in the range of Mach 3.0 to Mach 4.0 (approximately 3000 to 4000 feet per second).  
This high-velocity jet shears the surrounding atmosphere inside the suction chamber, creating a localized zone of extreme low pressure that induces the flow of the process vapours through the 250 mm N1 vapour inlet nozzle. As the supersonic motive steam and the relatively stagnant suction gas enter the mixing chamber, momentum is transferred through highly turbulent shear mixing. The resulting mixture remains supersonic as it enters the converging section of the diffuser.  
Within the diverging section of the diffuser, a critical aerodynamic phenomenon occurs. The mixture undergoes a sonic shock wave—a sudden, violent deceleration from supersonic to subsonic flow. Across this shock wave, the kinetic energy of the gas is converted back into static pressure. This "sonic boost" or compression allows the gas mixture to exit the 250 mm N3 discharge nozzle possessing a pressure high enough to overcome the downstream resistance of the subsequent inter-condenser.

### **Pull Curve Characteristics and Operational Stability**

The operational efficiency and stability of an ejector are mapped on a performance curve, commonly referred to as a "pull curve." This curve graphically plots the achievable Suction Pressure (typically in mm Hg absolute or bar a) on the Y-axis against the Equivalent Vapour Load (kg/h) on the X-axis.  
At its design capacity, Ejector II is engineered to entrain a normal vapour load of 634 kg/h while maintaining a steady suction pressure of 0.122 bar a. As the vapour load from the evaporator increases toward the maximum design flow of 761 kg/h, the ejector's capacity to evacuate the gas diminishes proportionally, resulting in a gradual rise in suction pressure along the trajectory of the pull curve.  
The absolute stability of the pull curve is dictated by the Maximum Discharge Pressure (MDP). Because an ejector operates across a fixed compression ratio determined by the kinetic energy of the motive steam, it can only discharge against a specific maximum backpressure. If the backpressure exerted by Condenser III exceeds the MDP—a scenario that frequently occurs due to cooling water supply failure, excessive ambient temperatures, or internal tube fouling—the supersonic shockwave within the ejector's diffuser will collapse. This thermodynamic failure is known as "breaking" operation. When an ejector breaks, the high-pressure motive steam bypasses the diffuser entirely and blows back into the suction chamber, causing a catastrophic spike in suction pressure within the upstream evaporator, entirely halting the concentration process.  
Furthermore, the integrity of the pull curve is highly sensitive to the quality of the motive steam. The steam must remain at or slightly above the design pressure of 4.1 bar a. If the steam is "wet" and contains entrained moisture droplets, these droplets will accelerate through the converging-diverging nozzle at supersonic speeds, acting as abrasive projectiles that cause severe erosion of the nozzle throat over time. This physical enlargement of the throat cross-section alters the expansion ratio, permanently degrading the ejector's pull curve and permanently reducing its entrainment capacity.

## **Instrumentation, Advanced Control Architecture, and Level Measurement**

The thermodynamic stability of the evaporation and vacuum stages is autonomously maintained by a highly integrated network of electronic control loops, utilizing advanced cascade and split-range control logic to manage pressures, temperatures, and liquid levels.

### **Barometric Level Measurement and Split-Range Control**

Accurate level control of the concentrated urea melt is a paramount safety and operational requirement. A low level causes the downstream extraction pumps to cavitate and breaks the vacuum seal of the system, while a high level results in the entrainment of liquid urea directly into the vacuum condensers, severely fouling the tube bundles. Due to the violent boiling, flashing, and splashing occurring inside the main body of Separator Evaporator II (324F003), the liquid level cannot be reliably measured inside the vessel itself.  
To overcome this, the level is physically measured within the barometric leg—the long, vertical suction line leading downward to the Urea Melt Pump. This localized section provides a calm, stabilized column of liquid unperturbed by the flashing vapours above. A physical sight glass is installed at this location to allow plant operators visual verification of the level. The electronic level transmitter feeds the primary Level Controller, designated as **LIC324501**.  
LIC324501 operates two distinct control valves via a sophisticated split-range algorithm:

* **LV324501A:** Positioned on the forward melt discharge line feeding the main distribution header of the granulator.  
* **LV324501B:** Positioned on the recycle line returning the melt to the urea solution tank (323D002).

During normal, steady-state operation, the recycle valve LV324501B remains fully closed, and the controller dynamically modulates the forward valve LV324501A to match the production rate of the evaporator. However, in the event of a granulator trip, or if the melt concentration drops below the critical 98.6 wt% threshold, the plant interlock system triggers. To prevent the pump from dead-heading and the highly concentrated melt from solidifying inside the piping, the instrument air supply to LV324501A is cut, causing it to fail closed. Simultaneously, the air to LV324501B is exhausted, causing it to fail open, safely routing the hot urea melt back to the storage tank in a continuous circulation loop. High and low-level deviations trigger immediate alarms via LAH324501 and LAL324501. Modern Stamicarbon upgrades to this system often replace traditional radioactive or differential pressure level transmitters with advanced non-contact radar level measurement devices to enhance plant safety and availability.

### **Vacuum Pressure Control via False Air Modulation**

The operating pressure in Separator Evaporator II directly dictates the boiling point of the solution and, consequently, the final concentration of the urea melt. The absolute pressure is maintained at a precise 0.13 bar a by the primary Pressure Controller, **PIC324203**.  
Because the motive steam supplied to the ejectors cannot be easily throttled without dropping below the critical expansion ratio (which would induce ejector "breaking"), vacuum control is not achieved by modulating the ejectors themselves. Instead, control is achieved by throttling the condensation rate within Condenser II. PIC324203 receives a remote setpoint cascaded from the upstream concentration controller (**AIC324701**). To adjust the pressure, PIC324203 commands an air-bleed control valve (**PV324203**) located on the vapour line immediately upstream of Condenser II.  
This valve is colloquially known as a "false air" valve. When PV324203 opens, it introduces ambient atmospheric air into the process vapour stream. Because air is a non-condensable gas, this influx physically blankets the cold cooling tubes inside Condenser II, artificially depressing the Overall Heat Transfer Coefficient ($U$). The heavily reduced condensation rate causes water vapour to accumulate within the shell, thereby raising the system pressure back to the setpoint. Conversely, when the valve closes, the non-condensable blanket is purged by the ejector, restoring maximum condensation efficiency and pulling the system back into a deeper vacuum. In the event of a total instrument air failure, PV324203 is designed to fail closed.

### **Transmitter Purging Systems**

A pervasive, critical challenge in urea instrumentation is the rapid crystallization of process fluids inside the dead legs of impulse lines leading to pressure transmitters. If ammonium carbamate vapour enters a cool impulse line, it immediately solidifies into a dense salt plug, permanently blinding the transmitter and causing a catastrophic loss of process control.  
To mitigate this inherent risk, the Stamicarbon design incorporates continuous instrument air purges. A constant volumetric flow of clean, dry instrument air is metered through a series of dedicated rotameters. Specifically, **FI324456** supplies purge air to the PI324204 transmitter at Evaporator II, **FI324453** supplies purge air to the PIC324203 controller at Condenser II, and **FI324455** supplies purge air to the PI324257 transmitter at Condenser III. This air flows backwards through the impulse lines into the process vessels, creating a positive pressure barrier that physically prevents the corrosive, crystallizing vapours from ever reaching the sensitive diaphragms of the electronic pressure transmitters. For added redundancy, critical instrumentation lines are heavily wrapped with medium-pressure steam tracing to ensure any rogue carbamate vapours remain above their crystallization temperature.

## **Fluid Machinery Hydraulics: Urea Melt Pump Performance**

The Urea Melt Pump, designated as **335P001A/B**, serves as the primary rotating equipment bridging the evaporation section and the downstream finishing and granulation units. The pump's role is to extract the concentrated 99.7% urea melt from the bottom of Separator Evaporator II (324F003) and pressurize it sufficiently to feed the distribution headers and spray nozzles of the fluid-bed granulator (335R001).  
Operating this pump presents extreme hydraulic and mechanical challenges due to the fluid's rheology; 99.7% urea melt possesses a high dynamic viscosity and an elevated solidification point of approximately 132.8 °C. To prevent the catastrophic solidification of the melt within the pump casing, the pump volute, as well as the entire suction and discharge piping network, must be continuously steam-jacketed or heavily traced with low-pressure steam (operating at 3.6 bar and roughly 140 °C). Furthermore, an anti-caking agent and process aid, Urea-Formaldehyde (UF), is precisely dosed directly into the suction line of this pump by a dedicated UF Dosing Pump (**335P002A/B**). Injecting the UF precondensate at the suction side leverages the turbulent action of the pump impeller to ensure a perfectly homogeneous blend prior to granulation.

The pump achieves its Best Efficiency Point (BEP) at a flow rate of 59.1 m³/h, providing a substantial 137.5 meters of hydraulic head while operating at 56% total efficiency and consuming 53 kW of shaft power. At zero flow (shut-off head), the radial impeller generates a maximum of 150 meters of head, providing a stable 8% continuous rise to shut-off, which is critical for maintaining steady spray pressure at the granulator nozzles.  
A critical operational constraint for the Urea Melt Pump is the severe risk of cavitation. Given that the urea melt is drawn directly from a vessel operating under deep vacuum (0.13 bar a), the fluid is at its boiling point, providing virtually zero absolute static pressure margin. To secure an adequate Net Positive Suction Head Available (NPSHa) and prevent the flashing of the melt inside the impeller eye, the pump is physically positioned several meters beneath Separator Evaporator II. The long, vertical suction piping acts as a barometric leg, utilizing the density of the 1,220 kg/m³ urea melt to artificially generate the required static pressure at the pump inlet. The operating manual strictly warns against flushing these pumps with water during normal operations, as the introduction of cold water would instantly trigger the massive crystallization of the urea melt, destroying the mechanical seals and seizing the impeller.
