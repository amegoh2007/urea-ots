# **Process and Instrumentation Analysis of the Desorption and Hydrolysis Section in a Stamicarbon Urea Plant**

## **1\. Introduction to the Desorption and Hydrolysis Architecture**

In industrial urea production, the synthesis of urea from ammonia ($NH\_{3}$) and carbon dioxide ($CO\_{2}$) generates a significant volume of process water as an inherent byproduct. The stoichiometric reaction governing this process dictates that for every metric ton of urea produced, approximately 0.3 tons of water is formed. This water, primarily discharged from the urea concentration and evaporation sections, forms a highly contaminated process condensate containing unreacted ammonia (typically 2.0 to 9.0 wt.%), carbon dioxide (0.8 to 6.0 wt.%), and entrained urea (0.3 to 1.5 wt.%).  
The desorption and hydrolysis section, designated as System 328 within the Stamicarbon urea stripping process framework, is engineered to process this contaminated condensate. The system's primary thermodynamic objective is to recover these valuable reactants and recycle them back to the high-pressure synthesis or low-pressure recirculation stages, thereby closing the mass balance loop and maximizing overall plant yield. Simultaneously, the system must purify the effluent water to stringent environmental and operational standards—specifically, reducing the ammonia concentration to less than 5 ppm-wt and the urea concentration to less than 2 ppm-wt—rendering the water suitable for reuse as cooling tower make-up or as feed for the granulation scrubbers.  
The architectural design of the desorption and hydrolysis loop operates via a cascading sequence of pressure and temperature gradients across three primary fractionating and reacting vessels: Desorption Column I (328C002), the Hydrolizer (328C003), and Desorption Column II (328C004). To optimize the energy efficiency of this sequence, the system incorporates intensive thermal integration utilizing plate heat exchangers (328E001, 328E007) and relies on highly robust hydraulic transport mechanisms via centrifugal pumps (328P003, 328P006, 328P007) engineered to withstand extreme corrosive environments.  
This report provides a comprehensive phenomenological, mechanical, and control-loop analysis of the core equipment within this section. It integrates process flow dynamics, thermodynamic modeling principles, fluid transport mechanics, and digital control theory to elucidate the operational behavior of the Stamicarbon desorption and hydrolysis block.

## **2\. Process Flow Streams and Mass Balance Mapping**

The fluid network through the desorption and hydrolysis loop is governed by stringent thermodynamic requirements designed to prevent ammonium carbamate crystallization and ensure deep stripping of volatile components. The interaction between the liquid and vapour phases in this system is characterized by severe non-idealities caused by electrolytic species (ammonium carbamate and ammonium bicarbonate). To accurately model these interactions, modern simulation software employs the extended Universal QuasiChemical (UNIQUAC) model or the Non-Random Two-Liquid (NRTL) thermodynamic model for the liquid phase, alongside a perturbed-hard-sphere (PHS) equation of state or virial equation for the vapour phase.

### **2.1 Feed and Discharge Routing**

The continuous steady-state operation of the loop relies on the following primary process routes, which can be tracked across the Process Flow Diagrams (PFD No. 22\) and Piping and Instrumentation Diagrams (P\&ID No. 104):

1. **Contaminated Condensate Feed:** Process water originating from the evaporation section is collected in the second compartment of the Ammonia Water Tank (328D003). This contaminated water is pressurized by the Desorber Feed Pump (328P003 A/B) and directed through the cold side of the Desorber Heat Exchanger (328E007). The feed recovers heat from the purified effluent before entering the upper section of Desorption Column I (328C002).  
2. **Intermediate Hydrolysis Feed:** The partially stripped bottoms liquid from Desorption Column I, which has been stripped of bulk free ammonia and carbon dioxide but still contains unhydrolyzed urea, is transported via the Hydrolizer Feed Pump (328P006 A/B). The fluid passes through the Hydrolyzer Heat Exchanger (328E021 A/B) for further pre-heating before being injected into the top tray (Tray 1\) of the Hydrolizer (328C003).  
3. **Deep Stripping Feed:** Within the Hydrolizer, the application of high temperature and medium-pressure (MP) steam drives the endothermic decomposition of urea back into $NH\_{3}$ and $CO\_{2}$. The liquid leaving the Hydrolizer, now rich in these newly formed gases, flows downward through the Hydrolyzer Heat Exchanger (releasing heat to the incoming feed) and enters the top tray of Desorption Column II (328C004).  
4. **Vapour Recycles and Condensation:** The process relies heavily on vapour counter-flow for stripping energy. Vapours generated in Desorption Column II and the Hydrolizer flow counter-currently back into the bottom of Desorption Column I. The combined overhead vapour leaving Desorption Column I is then routed to the Reflux Condenser (328E004). Here, the vapours are condensed into a lean carbamate solution.  
5. **Reflux Loop:** The condensed lean carbamate from the Reflux Condenser flows into the Reflux Level Tank (328D001) and is subsequently pumped by the Reflux Pump (328P002 A/B). A portion is returned to the top tray of Desorption Column I as reflux to control the top temperature, while the remainder is exported to the low-pressure (LP) carbamate condenser (323E003) for recycling to the synthesis section.  
6. **Purified Condensate Discharge:** The bottom effluent of Desorption Column II, having been deep-stripped by low-pressure (LP) steam, passes through the hot side of the Desorber Heat Exchanger (328E007), where it is cooled. It is then either discharged to the cooling tower as make-up water or pumped by the Purified Process Condensate Pump (328P007 A/B) to the granulation unit. During upset conditions or start-up, if the water does not meet purity specifications, it can be routed through the Recycle Cooler (328E001) and returned to the Ammonia Water Tank.

## **3\. Phenomenological Modeling and Mechanical Design of Process Vessels**

The fractionating and reacting vessels within System 328 must withstand highly corrosive operating environments while facilitating maximum interfacial contact area for mass and heat transfer. To mitigate stress corrosion cracking associated with ammonium carbamate, the wetted surfaces of these vessels are predominantly constructed from Stamicarbon material specification 18005, corresponding to BC.09 (1.4404 / 316L modified) or advanced duplex stainless steels such as Safurex (25Cr22Ni2Mo modified).

### **3.1 Desorption Column I (328C002)**

Desorption Column I serves as the preliminary stripping unit, designed to remove the bulk of the free ammonia and carbon dioxide from the incoming process condensate before hydrolysis.  
**Mechanical Configuration:** The vessel is a vertical column with an outer shell diameter of 1,274 mm and an inside diameter of 1,250 mm. The cylindrical shell height is 10,470 mm. The entire upper and lower shell, as well as the internal components, are fabricated from BC.09 stainless steel. The vessel has an empty delivery weight of approximately 17,600 kg (in conjunction with column II), increasing to 30,300 kg with process fluid, and requires a water-filled test weight of 50,900 kg. It operates under a design pressure of 5.0 bar g and a design temperature of $200^\\circ\\text{C}$. The structural design adheres to AD-Merkblätter codes, incorporating seismic factors (UBC-Zone II b) and specific external nozzle load tolerances (e.g., maximum moments of 30 kNm on the N3 liquid inlet nozzle).  
**Internal Mass Transfer Mechanisms:** The column is equipped with 15 execution sieve trays. The trays are spaced at 500 mm intervals, with an extended 800 mm separation between trays 4 and 5 to accommodate vapor distribution. The trays are designed for severe dynamic loading, capable of withstanding the dead weight plus 600 N/m² across the perforated area, and 2,000 N/m² under the downcomers. Each tray is precision-engineered with exactly 3,125 holes of 6 mm diameter. As the liquid flows across the trays and through the downcomers, ascending vapours bubble through the 6 mm perforations, generating high-turbulent froth regimes.  
**Heat and Mass Transfer Dynamics:** Operating at a nominal pressure of 3.5 bar (range 3.3 to 3.7 bar), the pre-heated process condensate enters the top trays and encounters the hot vapours rising from Desorption Column II and the Hydrolizer. The difference in chemical potential drives the desorption of $NH\_{3}$ and $CO\_{2}$ from the liquid into the vapour phase. Sensible and latent heat is transferred from the vapour to the liquid, elevating the liquid temperature from its feed state (approximately $114^\\circ\\text{C}$ to $124^\\circ\\text{C}$) to $138^\\circ\\text{C}$ at the bottom sump.  
A critical operational constraint governing this vessel is the moisture content of the top vapour leaving the column via nozzle N1. To prevent ammonium carbamate crystallization in the downstream Reflux Condenser, the water content in the gas phase must be maintained at approximately 46 vol.% (equivalent to 40 wt.%). Phenomenologically, this is achieved by tightly regulating the column's top temperature to $117^\\circ\\text{C}$. If the temperature rises, an excessive amount of water vaporizes; to counteract this, cool, lean carbamate reflux (approx. 31 wt.% concentration at $57^\\circ\\text{C}$) is sprayed onto the top tray, absorbing the excess latent heat and condensing the water back into the liquid phase.

### **3.2 The Hydrolizer (328C003)**

The Hydrolizer operates as a continuous liquid-phase chemical reactor. Its function is to process the bottom effluent of Desorption Column I, providing the necessary activation energy and residence time to decompose the remaining aqueous urea.  
**Mechanical Configuration:** The Hydrolizer is a massive, highly pressurized vertical vessel. It features an outside shell diameter of 1,998 mm, an inside diameter of 1,950 mm, and a cylindrical shell height of 20,850 mm. Given its severe operating conditions, it is constructed entirely of BC.09 stainless steel with a joint efficiency of 1.0 and a 1.0 mm corrosion allowance. The empty delivery weight of the vessel is 47,050 kg, which balloons to 105,410 kg when filled with process fluid, and 107,400 kg during hydrostatic testing. It is engineered for a design pressure of 21.0 bar g and a design temperature of $230^\\circ\\text{C}$. Due to the high temperature, the vessel utilizes external steam tracing coils on specific nozzles (N8, N9) to maintain thermal stability during transient operations.  
**Reaction Kinetics and Fluid Movement:** Urea hydrolysis is the reverse of the urea synthesis reaction. It is an endothermic process that requires continuous heat input and the constant removal of reaction products to drive the chemical equilibrium to the right: $NH\_{2}CONH\_{2} \+ H\_{2}O \\rightleftharpoons 2NH\_{3} \+ CO\_{2}$  
The vessel operates at an elevated pressure of 16.8 bar (range 16 to 18 bar) to suppress bulk liquid boiling, allowing the liquid temperature to be raised safely to $200^\\circ\\text{C}$. The liquid effluent from Desorption Column I is pumped into the first tray of the Hydrolizer. The column operates almost completely flooded (except for the vapour space above the first tray) to guarantee a liquid residence time of nearly one hour. The fluid flows slowly downwards through 17 sieve trays, spaced 1,100 mm apart.  
Medium Pressure (MP) saturated steam is injected through a sparger at the bottom of the column (underneath tray 17\) at a nominal rate of approximately 1,100 kg/h. This steam serves a dual thermodynamic purpose:

1. **Thermal Energy:** It supplies the sensible heat necessary to raise the liquid from its $190^\\circ\\text{C}$ feed temperature to $200^\\circ\\text{C}$, and provides the requisite enthalpy for the endothermic decomposition of the urea molecule.  
2. **Mass Transfer (Stripping):** As the steam bubbles rise through the 6 mm perforations of the sieve trays, the partial pressures of $NH\_{3}$ and $CO\_{2}$ within the vapour bubbles are initially zero. According to Henry's Law, this immense concentration gradient drives the newly formed $NH\_{3}$ and $CO\_{2}$ out of the aqueous phase and into the rising steam bubbles, continuously shifting the reaction equilibrium toward complete urea destruction. The resulting vapours, rich in ammonia and carbon dioxide, reach the top of the Hydrolizer at about $188^\\circ\\text{C}$ and are released back to Desorption Column I.

### **3.3 Desorption Column II (328C004)**

The effluent descending from the Hydrolizer requires a final deep-stripping step to expel the dissolved $NH\_{3}$ and $CO\_{2}$ that were liberated during the hydrolysis reaction.  
**Mechanical Configuration and Stripping Dynamics:** Desorption Column II features 22 sieve trays inside a 1,250 mm inside diameter shell, with a total cylindrical height of 13,030 mm. It operates at a bottom pressure of 3.9 bar (range 3.7 to 4.1 bar). The liquid enters tray 1 after being cooled to $147^\\circ\\text{C}$ by the intermediate heat exchanger. Low Pressure (LP) steam is introduced at the bottom of the column (underneath tray 22).  
Operating at 3.9 bar, the column maintains the boiling point of the water at approximately $143^\\circ\\text{C}$. The vast volume of LP steam creates extreme turbulence and expands the vapour-liquid interfacial area across the 22 trays. This intensive stripping action reduces the ammonia concentration to below 5 ppm-wt and the urea concentration to below 2 ppm-wt. The overhead vapours discharge from the top of the column at an average temperature of $140^\\circ\\text{C}$ and are routed directly into Desorption Column I via an internal funnel structure. The purified bottom liquid leaves the column at $143^\\circ\\text{C}$ for heat recovery and final discharge.

## **4\. Instrumentation Architecture, Level Measurement, and Control Loop Logic**

The complex thermodynamic interactions and phase equilibria across the three vessels demand a sophisticated, highly integrated instrumentation and control architecture. To decouple the fast dynamics of steam flow from the slower dynamics of column thermal mass, the control strategies rely heavily on ratio and cascade control loops.

### **4.1 Pressure and Venting Control Logic**

System stability requires precise management of the vapour-phase pressure to maintain the required boiling points and saturation limits of the fluids.

* **Desorption Loop Pressure (PIC328202):** The pressure in both Desorption Columns I and II is collectively maintained at 3.5 bar by the pressure controller **PIC328202**. Rather than utilizing a traditional throttling valve on a vapour release line, this loop controls the pressure thermodynamically. The controller modulates valve **PV328202** (alternatively designated TV328002 in some loop diagrams), which regulates the cooling water flow through the Reflux Condenser (328E004). Increasing the cooling water flow increases the heat removal rate, accelerating the condensation of the overhead vapours and thereby lowering the system pressure.  
* **Hydrolizer Pressure (PIC328203):** The Hydrolizer pressure is maintained strictly at 16.8 bar via controller **PIC328203**. The pressure impulse is taken from the bottom liquid phase of the Hydrolizer. The controller actuates the vapour release valve **PV328203**, which throttles the overhead vapours exiting the Hydrolizer into the lower-pressure environment of Desorption Column I.

### **4.2 Temperature and Steam Cascade Logic**

Thermal energy input must be rigorously tied to process mass flows to prevent energy waste, column flooding, or incomplete reactions.

* **Desorption Column I Top Temperature (TIC328008 $\\rightarrow$ FIC328404):** To maintain the critical $117^\\circ\\text{C}$ top temperature required to prevent carbamate crystallization, temperature controller **TIC328008** continuously calculates the deviation from the setpoint. Because the boiling point of the mixture fluctuates with system pressure, **TIC328008** is pressure-compensated by **PIC328202** through the calculation unit **TY328008**. The output of the temperature controller acts as the remote setpoint for the reflux flow controller **FIC328404**, which modulates the control valve feeding lean carbamate to the top tray.  
* **Hydrolizer Differential Temperature (TIC328012 $\\rightarrow$ FIC329402):** Merely controlling the absolute bottom temperature of the Hydrolizer is insufficient because the feed's urea concentration fluctuates, changing the total endothermic heat load. Therefore, a temperature difference ($\\Delta T$) is utilized. The calculation unit **TDY328012** measures the difference between the bottom effluent (indicated at $200^\\circ\\text{C}$ on TI328013) and the third tray (indicated at $193^\\circ\\text{C}$ on TI328012). This $\\Delta T$ (typically $5^\\circ\\text{C}$ to $12^\\circ\\text{C}$) provides a direct proxy for the progress of the endothermic reaction. **TIC328012** uses this $\\Delta T$ to output a setpoint to the MP steam flow controller **FIC329402**, which actuates the steam injection valve **FV329402**.  
* **Desorption Column II Ratio Control (FFIC329401 $\\rightarrow$ FIC329401):** The LP steam requirement is strictly stoichiometric relative to the mass of liquid being stripped. Ratio controller **FFIC329401** continuously monitors the incoming process condensate feed flow (**FIC328402**) and multiplies it by a fixed ratio (typically 0.25). This calculated value becomes the active setpoint for the LP steam flow controller **FIC329401**, which throttles the steam valve **FV329401**. For example, a 20 $m^3$/h condensate feed mathematically dictates a 5,000 kg/h steam injection rate.

### **4.3 Level Measurement and Hydraulic Transport**

Level measurement throughout the highly corrosive, scaling environment of the vessels is predominantly achieved using differential pressure (DP) transmitters with diaphragm seals. The level controllers are directly linked to the centrifugal pumps managing inter-vessel transport.

* **Desorption Column I Level (LIC328503):** The liquid level is measured at the bottom sump of 328C002. The controller **LIC328503** maintains a 50% level by actuating the control valve **LV328503**, which is physically located on the discharge piping of the Hydrolizer Feed Pump (328P006 A/B). Throttling the pump discharge dynamically matches the exit flow to the incoming feed and condensate. In case of severe upset, this valve can bypass the Hydrolizer and route the fluid directly to Desorption Column II.  
* **Hydrolizer Level (LIC328504):** Because the Hydrolizer is designed to operate liquid-filled to maximize residence time, the level is measured at the *top* of the column (above the first tray). **LIC328504** maintains this high level at 50% of the upper span by actuating **LV328504**, controlling the liquid blowdown from the bottom of the Hydrolizer into Desorption Column II.  
* **Desorption Column II Level (LIC328505):** Measured at the bottom sump. The controller maintains a 50% level by actuating **LV328505** on the discharge path of the Purified Process Condensate Pump, directing the fluid to the cooling tower make-up.

### **4.4 Analytical and Safety Instrumentation**

In addition to the primary control loops, the system is equipped with an array of analytical and safety instruments. Conductivity analyzer **AI328701** is installed at the outlet of the Desorber Heat Exchanger (328E007) to continuously monitor the purified process condensate. A high conductivity reading indicates an incomplete stripping process (excess ammonia/carbamate slipping through), triggering a DCS alarm. Manual sampling points (AE328752, AE328753, AE328754) are strategically located throughout the loop to allow for laboratory verification of the 5 ppm $NH\_3$ and 2 ppm urea limits. Overpressure protection is provided by safety relief valves (SV32850/151 on Desorption Column I and SV32852 on the Hydrolizer steam inlet).

They must transport highly corrosive fluids at high temperatures ($93^\\circ\\text{C}$ to $138^\\circ\\text{C}$), operating near the fluid's vapor pressure, which presents a constant risk of cavitation. To withstand these conditions, all wetted parts adhere to Stamicarbon material specification 18005 (BB.01), utilizing advanced duplex stainless steels such as 1.4462 and 1.4593. Mechanical power is transmitted through FLENDER ARPEX couplings configured with a service factor $\>1.5$, and the pump shafts are sealed using Burgmann mechanical seals operating on an API Plan 11 flush arrangement.

## **6\. Heat Exchanger Thermal Duty, Mechanical Design, and Overall Heat Transfer Coefficients ($U$)**

Energy optimization within the Stamicarbon process relies heavily on aggressive heat integration. The plant utilizes high-performance plate heat exchangers (328E001, 328E007) designed in accordance with AD-Merkblätter codes and subjected to rigorous non-destructive testing (EN 10204-3.1B material certificates, 100% penetrant testing on critical welds). The heat exchange plates are constructed from 1.4401 stainless steel (equivalent to Stamicarbon BC.09 specs), separated by EPDM gaskets, and compressed within a robust P265GH carbon steel frame.  
Plate heat exchangers are specifically selected for their ability to induce exceptionally high turbulence at low Reynolds numbers. This turbulence minimizes the thermal boundary layer and mitigates scaling, resulting in massive overall heat transfer coefficients ($U$). To quantify the thermal efficiency of these units, the Logarithmic Mean Temperature Difference (LMTD) and the $U$-values are derived using the thermodynamic boundary parameters extracted from the OEM engineering datasheets.  
The fundamental governing equation for heat transfer is:  
$Q \= U \\cdot A \\cdot \\Delta T\_{\\text{LMTD}}$  
Where:

* $Q$ is the total thermal duty (in Watts).  
* $A$ is the total installed heat transfer surface area (in $m^2$).  
* $\\Delta T\_{\\text{LMTD}}$ is the log-mean temperature difference.

### **6.1 Desorber Heat Exchanger (328E007)**

This unit pre-heats the incoming cold process condensate feed by recovering the sensible heat from the hot purified effluent leaving Desorption Column II.

* **Area ($A$):** $10.58 \\text{ m}^2$ (comprising 25 active plates).  
* **Duty ($Q$):** $2,649 \\text{ kW}$ ($2,649,000 \\text{ W}$).  
* **Hot Fluid (Purified Condensate):** Inlet $T\_{h,in} \= 143.0^\\circ\\text{C}$, Outlet $T\_{h,out} \= 93.5^\\circ\\text{C}$.  
* **Cold Fluid (Ammonia Water Feed):** Inlet $T\_{c,in} \= 60.4^\\circ\\text{C}$, Outlet $T\_{c,out} \= 111.3^\\circ\\text{C}$.

**LMTD Calculation:**  
$\\Delta T\_{1} \= T\_{h,in} \- T\_{c,out} \= 143.0 \- 111.3 \= 31.7^\\circ\\text{C}$  
$\\Delta T\_{2} \= T\_{h,out} \- T\_{c,in} \= 93.5 \- 60.4 \= 33.1^\\circ\\text{C}$  
$\\Delta T\_{\\text{LMTD}} \= \\frac{\\Delta T\_{2} \- \\Delta T\_{1}}{\\ln(\\frac{\\Delta T\_{2}}{\\Delta T\_{1}})} \= \\frac{33.1 \- 31.7}{\\ln(\\frac{33.1}{31.7})} \= \\frac{1.4}{\\ln(1.0441)} \\approx 32.4^\\circ\\text{C}$  
**Overall Heat Transfer Coefficient ($U$):**  
$U \= \\frac{2,649,000}{10.58 \\cdot 32.4} \= \\frac{2,649,000}{342.79} \\approx 7,727 \\text{ W/m}^2\\text{K}$  
The calculated $U$-value of $7,727 \\text{ W/m}^2\\text{K}$ is exceptionally high. This is highly characteristic of the highly turbulent, thin-channel flow profiles present in corrugated plate heat exchangers handling low-viscosity, high-velocity aqueous solutions.

### **6.2 Recycle Cooler (328E001)**

During start-up, shut-down, or upset conditions, if the process condensate does not meet the strict environmental parameters (\< 5 ppm $NH\_3$), it must be recycled to the ammonia water tank. To prevent flashing and vapor-lock within the tank, the recycle stream is actively cooled using return cooling water.

* **Area ($A$):** $36.40 \\text{ m}^2$ (comprising 72 active plates).  
* **Duty ($Q$):** $2,834 \\text{ kW}$ ($2,834,000 \\text{ W}$).  
* **Hot Fluid (Recycled Condensate):** Inlet $T\_{h,in} \= 93.5^\\circ\\text{C}$, Outlet $T\_{h,out} \= 40.0^\\circ\\text{C}$.  
* **Cold Fluid (Cooling Water Return):** Inlet $T\_{c,in} \= 30.0^\\circ\\text{C}$, Outlet $T\_{c,out} \= 40.0^\\circ\\text{C}$.

**LMTD Calculation:**  
$\\Delta T\_{1} \= T\_{h,in} \- T\_{c,out} \= 93.5 \- 40.0 \= 53.5^\\circ\\text{C}$  
$\\Delta T\_{2} \= T\_{h,out} \- T\_{c,in} \= 40.0 \- 30.0 \= 10.0^\\circ\\text{C}$  
$\\Delta T\_{\\text{LMTD}} \= \\frac{53.5 \- 10.0}{\\ln(\\frac{53.5}{10.0})} \= \\frac{43.5}{1.677} \\approx 25.9^\\circ\\text{C}$  
**Overall Heat Transfer Coefficient ($U$):**  
$U \= \\frac{2,834,000}{36.40 \\cdot 25.9} \= \\frac{2,834,000}{942.76} \\approx 3,006 \\text{ W/m}^2\\text{K}$  
The comparatively lower $U$-value here ($3,006 \\text{ W/m}^2\\text{K}$ versus $7,727 \\text{ W/m}^2\\text{K}$) is expected. The lower overall fluid temperatures increase the dynamic viscosity of the liquid streams, thickening the boundary layer. Furthermore, cooling water systems inherently carry a risk of bio-fouling and mineral scaling; therefore, the unit is intentionally designed with a 20% excess surface area margin, which artificially depresses the calculated operational $U$-value relative to its clean state potential.

## **7\. Advanced Materials and Safety Metallurgy**

The extreme conditions within the Stamicarbon urea process—particularly the presence of ammonium carbamate at high temperatures—create a severe risk of stress corrosion cracking and general uniform corrosion. The traditional approach of injecting continuous passivation air to maintain an oxide layer on standard stainless steels has largely been superseded by the integration of advanced duplex stainless steels.  
The wetted components of the desorption and hydrolysis loop are extensively specified with Stamicarbon material code 18005 (e.g., BC.09) or proprietary duplex grades such as Safurex. Safurex (a modified 25Cr-22Ni-2Mo duplex steel) was exclusively developed to provide absolute resistance to carbamate solutions with little or no oxygen present, fundamentally improving plant on-stream time by eliminating the need for extensive passivation air injection.  
However, the fabrication of these vessels requires strict adherence to metallurgical protocols. Duplex stainless steels exhibit a known susceptibility to "475°C embrittlement," a phenomenon where the ferrite phase decomposes if exposed to temperatures between $300^\\circ\\text{C}$ and $500^\\circ\\text{C}$ for prolonged periods. While the operational temperatures of the Hydrolizer ($200^\\circ\\text{C}$) and Desorber columns ($138^\\circ\\text{C}$ to $143^\\circ\\text{C}$) remain safely below this threshold, the fabrication and welding processes during vessel construction must be tightly controlled. The OEM inspection data sheets mandate 100% liquid penetrant testing (PT) on the first and top layers of fillet seams and full ultrasonic testing (UT) on clad plates after forming to ensure structural integrity and the absence of ferrite phase decomposition.

## **8\. Process Synergy and Operational Conclusions**

The comprehensive analysis of the Stamicarbon desorption and hydrolysis architecture reveals a highly optimized integration of thermodynamic principles, hydraulic design, and digital control theory.  
The system's efficiency is fundamentally rooted in exploiting Henry’s Law and Le Chatelier’s principle. By elevating the pressure in the Hydrolizer to 16.8 bar, the aqueous solution is prevented from boiling, allowing the bulk liquid to be heated safely to $200^\\circ\\text{C}$. This immense thermal elevation provides the activation energy required to break the robust urea bonds via an endothermic reaction. Concurrently, the continuous sparging of MP steam alters the partial pressure dynamics within the fluid. The steam bubbles act as a mass-transfer vacuum, sweeping the newly formed $NH\_3$ and $CO\_2$ out of the aqueous solution. This continuous removal prevents the reaction equilibrium from shifting backward toward urea formation, driving the hydrolysis reaction to near absolute completion.  
Furthermore, the cascading nature of the pressure gradient—stepping down from 16.8 bar in the Hydrolizer to roughly 3.9 bar in Desorption Column II and 3.5 bar in Desorption Column I—allows the latent heat contained in the stripping vapours to be reused sequentially. The overhead vapours from the Hydrolizer and Desorption Column II act as the free-energy stripping medium for Desorption Column I. This sequential reuse of energy significantly reduces the total specific LP and MP steam consumption of the plant, directly addressing the energetic bottlenecks inherent in older urea manufacturing processes.  
The instrumentation matrix is specifically designed to buffer process disturbances. By controlling the Hydrolizer heat input via a differential temperature ($\\Delta T$) cascade to the steam flow—rather than relying on an absolute temperature setpoint—the Distributed Control System (DCS) automatically adjusts the steam mass to the actual endothermic load. Similarly, the ratio control of LP steam to the incoming process condensate feed mass ensures that steam usage scales linearly with plant load. This prevents both energy waste during plant turndown and incomplete stripping during capacity peaks.  
Mechanically, the equipment demonstrates robust metallurgy capable of surviving the aggressive electrolytic nature of hot carbamate solutions. The reliance on advanced duplex stainless steels ensures that stress corrosion cracking is mitigated. The centrifugal pump hydraulics, rigorously designed to ISO 5199 and API 610 standards, provide sufficient NPSH margins and minimum continuous stable flow protection, guaranteeing uninterrupted transport across steep thermodynamic boundaries. In sum, this system transforms a highly contaminated waste stream into pure industrial water (\< 5 ppm $NH\_3$, \< 2 ppm urea) while recovering essential raw materials, forming the cornerstone of modern, ecologically compliant urea production.
