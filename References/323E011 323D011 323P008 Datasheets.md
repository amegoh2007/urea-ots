# **Comprehensive Engineering Analysis of Recirculation Equipment 323E011, 323D011, and 323P008 in Stamicarbon Urea Production**

## **Introduction to the Low-Pressure Recirculation System**

The production of synthetic urea is a cornerstone of the modern agricultural chemical industry. The process involves the complex, high-pressure reaction of anhydrous ammonia and carbon dioxide to form an intermediate compound, ammonium carbamate, which is subsequently dehydrated to yield urea and water. Within the globally utilized Stamicarbon Urea 2000plus™ stripping process, the recovery and recirculation of unreacted feedstocks are as critical to plant economics and environmental compliance as the primary synthesis loop. The facility under analysis is the Helwan Fertilizer Company (HFC) Ammonia/Urea Plant, located geographically proximate to New Cairo City, Egypt. This facility operates within a challenging climatic envelope, characterized by ambient temperatures reaching up to 46 °C and atmospheric conditions occasionally laden with silica dust and fertilizer particulates. Such environmental factors deeply influence the design of utility systems, particularly cooling water networks, and the selection of mechanical equipment parameters.  
This expert-level engineering report focuses on a highly specific node within the low-pressure recovery section of this 1750 Metric Tonnes Per Day (MTPD) capacity plant: the equipment cluster comprising the Flash Tank Condenser (323E011), the associated Level Tank (323D011), and the Lean Carbamate Pumps (323P008 A/B). The primary engineering objective of this cluster is to receive off-gases generated from downstream depressurization and evaporation stages, condense and absorb these gases into a weak aqueous solution, and effectively pump the resulting "lean carbamate" back upstream to the Reflux Condenser and high-pressure scrubbing systems.  
The analysis detailed herein meticulously tracks all process flow streams entering and discharging from this node, extracting Original Equipment Manufacturer (OEM) data regarding mass flows, thermodynamic properties, and multi-component compositions. Furthermore, it maps the instrumentation and distributed control system (DCS) architecture governing the node, elucidates the internal mass and heat transfer phenomena within the equipment, and defines the hydraulic performance and mechanical efficiency of the centrifugal pumping systems.

## **Process Flow Stream Tracking and Characterization**

The operational efficacy of the 323 equipment cluster is fundamentally dictated by the thermodynamic state and chemical composition of the process streams it manages. In the Stamicarbon process, following the primary synthesis and high-pressure stripping stages, the urea solution contains significant amounts of dissolved, unreacted ammonia and carbon dioxide. As the solution is sequentially depressurized through the recovery section, these volatile components flash off into the vapor phase.

### **Flash Tank Condenser (323E011) Feed and Discharge Streams**

The Flash Tank Condenser acts as the primary thermal sink and absorption vessel in the low-pressure section. It operates at an internal pressure of approximately 1.0 to 1.1 bar absolute, a deliberate design choice that facilitates the flashing of water, ammonia, and carbon dioxide from the upstream 4.1 bar rectifying column.  
The shell side of the condenser receives a mixed-phase feed. According to the process data sheets, the total mass flow entering the shell side under nominal design conditions is 9,400 kg/h, which is subdivided into 7,200 kg/h of vapor/gas and 2,200 kg/h of liquid. The primary gaseous streams routing into this vessel originate from the Flash Tank (323F004) and the level tanks of the LP Carbamate Condenser (323D001) and Reflux Condenser (328D001). Process modeling data specific to the 1750 MTPD load identifies distinct gas streams feeding this section, most notably Stream 701 and Stream 702\.  
Stream 701 represents a substantial carbamate gas feed, operating at 106 °C and 1.1 bar absolute. It carries a mass flow of 4,430 kg/h with an effective density of 0.68 kg/m³. The composition of Stream 701 is predominantly water vapor (61.64 weight percent) and ammonia (34.08 weight percent), with a smaller fraction of carbon dioxide (4.28 weight percent). The high water content is critical, as it provides the necessary solvent capacity to dissolve the ammonium carbamate formed during the subsequent cooling phase. Stream 702, another gaseous feed, operates at a significantly lower temperature of 45 °C and 1.0 bar absolute, delivering 440 kg/h of material. Unlike Stream 701, Stream 702 is exceptionally rich in ammonia, comprising 89.4 weight percent, alongside minor fractions of water (6.35 weight percent), nitrogen (3.3 weight percent), oxygen (0.81 weight percent), and carbon dioxide (0.14 weight percent). The presence of oxygen is a calculated necessity in Stamicarbon plants, as it continuously passivates the urea-grade stainless steel surfaces, protecting them against the highly corrosive carbamate solutions.  
The combined shell-side inlet conditions stabilize at a design operating temperature of 82 °C. As the fluid traverses the shell side and rejects heat to the cooling water, it undergoes partial condensation. The discharge from the condenser is bipartite: a non-condensable gas phase and a condensed liquid phase. The non-condensable vapor fraction, leaving at a rate of 3,100 kg/h, consists of inert gases (nitrogen, oxygen) and equilibrium concentrations of ammonia and carbon dioxide. This off-gas stream is vented directly to the Atmospheric Absorber (323C005) under strict pressure control.  
The condensed liquid phase, which falls via gravity from the bottom of the Flash Tank Condenser, sees its mass flow increase to 6,300 kg/h due to the successful phase transition and absorption of the incoming vapors. This liquid drops into the associated Level Tank (323D011) at a reduced temperature of 45 °C and a density of 961.00 kg/m³.

### **Lean Carbamate Recirculation (Stream 718\)**

The liquid accumulated in the Level Tank (323D011) is the lean carbamate solution. It is drawn by the Lean Carbamate Pump (323P008 A/B) and discharged as Stream 718 (and its routed variants 718A and 718B). The mechanical work imparted by the pump raises the pressure of this liquid stream from the atmospheric baseline of 1.0 bar absolute up to 4.1 bar absolute, maintaining a temperature of 45 °C.  
Stream 718 has a characterized mass flow of 7,123 kg/h and a liquid density of 1,065 kg/m³. The chemical composition confirms its identity as a weak carbamate solution: water is the dominant component at 57.24 weight percent, followed by ammonia at 25.21 weight percent, and carbon dioxide at 17.38 weight percent. A trace amount of urea (0.18 weight percent) is also present, likely carried over via process condensate or minor entrainment. This pressurized stream is subsequently routed to the Reflux Condenser (328E004), where it will absorb further ammonia and carbon dioxide, gradually becoming a "rich" carbamate solution before being returned to the high-pressure synthesis loop.

### **Cooling Water System Dynamics**

The thermodynamic driving force for the condensation and absorption processes in 323E011 is provided by the plant's centralized cooling water network. The climatic conditions of Helwan, Egypt, impose strict design limitations on the cooling water system, establishing a nominal supply temperature of 30 °C, with an allowable temperature rise of 10 Kelvin across heat exchangers.  
The tube side of the Flash Tank Condenser is supplied by Stream 1012, delivering cooling water at 30 °C and 3.6 bar absolute pressure. The physical properties of this incoming water dictate a density of 995.84 kg/m³. Under the 1750 MTPD operational load, the mass flow is regulated to 302,000 kg/h (302 t/h), although the heat exchanger is designed to accommodate up to 345,000 kg/h. The cooling water traverses the two tube passes, absorbing the latent heat of vaporization from the condensing gases and the exothermic heat of reaction from the carbamate formation.  
The heated cooling water exits the equipment as Stream 1013 at a temperature of 40 °C. The pressure of the return stream drops to 2.2 bar absolute, reflecting a 1.4 bar frictional pressure drop across the exchanger tubes and associated piping. This 10 Kelvin temperature differential conforms precisely to the plant's utility design specifications, indicating highly efficient thermal transfer across the heavily fouled (design fouling factor of 0.00018 m²K/W) boundary layers expected in cooling water services.

## **Instrumentation and Process Control Architecture**

The precise coordination of mass inventories and operating pressures within the 323 equipment cluster is achieved through a sophisticated Distributed Control System (DCS). Analysis of the Process and Instrumentation Diagrams (P\&IDs) and DCS interfaces reveals a control architecture designed to prioritize steady-state continuous operation while mitigating the risks of crystallization, flooding, and uncontrolled emissions.

### **Level Measurement and Pump Control (LIC-323503)**

The primary inventory control mechanism for this node revolves around the Level Tank (323D011). Because the Flash Tank Condenser operates essentially at atmospheric pressure, the Level Tank acts as a hydrostatic buffer, ensuring that the Lean Carbamate Pump (323P008) is constantly provided with sufficient Net Positive Suction Head (NPSH) to prevent cavitation.  
The liquid level within 323D011 is monitored by a Level Transmitter, designated as LT-323503. Mechanically, this instrument is interfaced with the vessel via two dedicated 80 mm (DN 80\) pad-type nozzles, labeled N2A and N2B, which serve as the upper and lower taps for a differential pressure or displacer-type measurement device. The 20 mm excess bolt length specified for these pad flanges indicates the accommodation of specialized instrument isolation valves or flush rings.  
The electronic signal from LT-323503 is transmitted to the DCS Level Indicator Controller, LIC-323503. On the DCS interface, the operator visualizes this level dynamically via a vertical bar graph superimposed on the 323D011 vessel icon. The controller employs a Proportional-Integral (PI) algorithm to compare the process variable against the operator setpoint. The controller's output modulates the designated level control valve, LV-323503, which is physically installed on the high-pressure discharge line of the Lean Carbamate Pump (323P008).  
By throttling LV-323503, the control loop dynamically adjusts the frictional resistance of the pump's discharge system. If the level in the tank begins to rise, the controller opens the valve, decreasing system resistance, which allows the centrifugal pump to "run out" on its performance curve and deliver a higher flow rate, thereby draining the tank faster. Conversely, a dropping level causes the valve to pinch closed, pushing the pump back along its curve to a lower flow rate. The DCS screenshot captures a snapshot of this continuous modulation, indicating a steady discharge flow of 4.1 m³/h being routed downstream towards the 328C002 process node.

### **System Pressure Regulation (PIC-323203)**

While the rectifying column upstream operates at 4.1 bar absolute, the fluid entering the Flash Tank undergoes an isenthalpic expansion, dropping to approximately 1.1 bar absolute. The 323E011/323D011 system must strictly maintain this lower pressure to ensure the thermodynamic equilibrium favors the flashing of off-gases from the incoming urea solution, while remaining high enough to provide the driving force for non-condensable gas venting.  
Pressure control is achieved through the action of Pressure Indicator Controller PIC-323203. The pressure transmitter for this loop monitors the vapor space of the Flash Tank Condenser system. The corresponding control valve is situated on the overhead off-gas line that exits the shell of 323E011. The DCS schematic clearly illustrates this gas line (colored yellow to denote vapor phase) departing the condenser and passing through a control valve before terminating at the Atmospheric Absorber (323C005).  
During normal operations, inert gases (introduced continuously as passivating air) and unabsorbed ammonia/carbon dioxide accumulate in the condenser's vapor space, causing the pressure to gradually build. PIC-323203 commands the control valve to open, venting these gases to maintain the setpoint. The captured DCS state shows this pressure control valve operating at a 25.0% open position, demonstrating active venting. In the event of a sudden thermal upset or upstream pressure surge, this valve can open fully to rapidly evacuate the vapor space.

### **Safety Instrumented Systems and Auxiliary Monitoring**

Beyond continuous regulatory control, the cluster is equipped with hardware safety layers to protect against catastrophic overpressure. Both the heat exchanger and the level tank feature flanged connections specifically designed for pressure safety valves (PSVs). The Level Tank (323D011) utilizes a 50 mm (DN 50\) nozzle, designated N6, for the installation of safety valve SV 32357\.  
A unique challenge in urea plant instrumentation is the propensity for ammonium carbamate to crystallize and solidify if its temperature drops below the saturation point or if water is evaporated from the solution. If carbamate solution were to leak into the inlet piping of a safety valve and solidify, it could plug the orifice, rendering the safety device useless. To mitigate this hazard, SV 32357 is equipped with a continuous steam injection or steam tracing system. This ensures that the valve body and inlet piping remain at an elevated temperature, preventing the deposition of carbamate crystals and guaranteeing the valve's availability during an overpressure event.  
Furthermore, process monitoring relies on auxiliary temperature and analytical instrumentation. While specific Temperature Indicator Controllers (TICs) governing cooling water flow to 323E011 are not actively modulating in the provided schematics, operators continuously monitor the thermal performance. The DCS displays a cooling water return temperature of 48.8 °C on the line exiting 323E011, which serves as an indirect indicator of the condenser's heat load and overall fouling state. For chemical quality control, a sample point (AE323758) is engineered into the suction piping of the Lean Carbamate Pump (323P008). This allows laboratory technicians to draw physical samples of the lean carbamate to verify the ammonia-to-carbon dioxide ratio and total water content, ensuring the process chemistry remains within optimal stoichiometric boundaries.

## **Mechanical Design and Transport Phenomena**

The design of the 323 equipment cluster requires specialized mechanical engineering due to the extreme corrosivity of the ammonium carbamate fluid. The selection of materials, vessel geometry, and internal components directly impacts the mass and heat transfer efficiencies required by the Stamicarbon Urea 2000plus™ technology.

### **The Flash Tank Condenser (323E011)**

The Flash Tank Condenser is structurally defined as a vertical shell-and-tube heat exchanger, fabricated in accordance with TEMA Class R standards and the ASME Boiler and Pressure Vessel Code Section VIII, Division 1\. The most critical mechanical specification is the material of construction for all wetted parts on the shell side. Ammonium carbamate is notoriously aggressive toward standard carbon steels and lower-grade stainless steels. Consequently, the shell, shell-side nozzles, baffles, and tubes are specified as "BC.09". In Stamicarbon nomenclature (Specification 18005), BC.09 refers to a specialized urea-grade austenitic stainless steel, typically 316L Urea Grade (1.4435) or a higher alloy like 25-22-2 (1.4466). These alloys are characterized by low carbon content (to prevent carbide precipitation and intergranular corrosion) and precisely controlled ferrite levels.  
The exchanger shell has an inner diameter of 800 mm and houses a tube bundle consisting of 382 individual BC.09 tubes. Each tube has an outer diameter of 25 mm, a wall thickness of 1.50 mm, and an effective length of 5,900 mm. The tubes are arranged in a 32 mm pitch. This geometry yields a total calculated heat exchange surface area of 177.00 m², which incorporates a 20% conservative safety margin over the bare thermodynamic requirement. Seven baffles, featuring a 25% diametrical cut, are arranged along the shell to direct the flow of the condensing vapors, inducing cross-flow across the tube bank to maximize the overall heat transfer coefficient.  
The thermal and mass transfer mechanisms occurring within this 177 m² volume are highly complex. As the 82 °C vapor stream enters the shell via the 500 mm (DN 500\) N1 inlet nozzle, it impacts the cooler tube surfaces maintained by the 30 °C cooling water inside the tubes. Sensible heat is rapidly rejected from the gas phase, reducing its temperature toward the dew point. As the saturation temperature is breached, water vapor and ammonia gas undergo a phase transition, condensing into an aqueous film on the outer surface of the tubes. This condensation releases massive amounts of latent heat, which must be conducted through the 1.5 mm tube wall into the turbulent cooling water stream.  
Simultaneously, the non-condensable carbon dioxide gas diffuses through the bulk gas phase toward the liquid film. Carbon dioxide dissolves into the aqueous ammonia solution, immediately triggering an exothermic chemical reaction to form ammonium carbamate ($2NH\_3 \+ CO\_2 \\rightleftharpoons NH\_2COONH\_4$). The heat of formation generated by this reaction supplements the thermal load on the cooling water system, culminating in a total equipment heat duty of 3,440 kW. To ensure complete absorption and to prevent the carbamate from exceeding its solubility limit (which would cause destructive crystallization on the tubes), additional process condensate is typically sprayed or injected into the upper sections of the condenser. The resulting liquid cascades down the tube bundle, gathering in the lower dished head before exiting through the 100 mm (DN 100\) N2 nozzle directly into the Level Tank.  
Mechanically, the vessel must withstand significant external nozzle loads due to thermal expansion of the connected piping. For instance, the massive 500 mm N1 vapor inlet nozzle is designed to endure a bending moment ($M\_x$) of up to 48.0 kNm and a longitudinal force ($F\_y$) of \-22.0 kN. The entire assembly has a dry delivery weight of 5,250 kg, which swells to an operating weight of 9,180 kg when flooded with process fluids and cooling water.

### **The Level Tank (323D011)**

Situated directly beneath the Flash Tank Condenser, the Level Tank (323D011) acts as a specialized hydrostatic receiver. It is a vertically oriented cylindrical pressure vessel designed with an internal diameter of 1,334 mm and a straight cylindrical shell height of 1,800 mm. Like the condenser, its process-wetted components—including the shell, heads, and nozzle liners—are constructed entirely from Stamicarbon spec BC.09 (316L Urea Grade) stainless steel to resist the corrosive 45 °C lean carbamate liquid.  
The mechanical design accounts for complex internal fluid dynamics. The lean carbamate falls from the condenser into the tank through the 100 mm top-mounted N1 inlet nozzle. To prevent this incoming fluid stream from plunging directly into the accumulated liquid pool—which would cause severe turbulence, entrain gas bubbles, and disrupt the sensitive level measurements of LT-323503—the tank is equipped with an internal impingement plate directly beneath the N1 inlet. This baffle disperses the kinetic energy of the falling liquid, allowing it to sheet gently down the vessel walls.  
Furthermore, the 80 mm N4 discharge nozzle located on the bottom head is fitted with a vortex breaker. As the centrifugal Lean Carbamate Pump rapidly draws liquid from the bottom of the tank, the conservation of angular momentum can cause the fluid to form a localized vortex, similar to a draining sink. In a chemical processing environment operating near atmospheric pressure, the core of such a vortex drops below the vapor pressure of the hot carbamate fluid, leading to massive localized outgassing and the entrainment of vapor directly into the pump suction. The cross-shaped internal vortex breaker physically interrupts these rotational fluid vectors, ensuring that only pure, non-aerated liquid enters the suction piping, thereby safeguarding the pump against cavitation and vapor lock.  
The vessel is designed for an internal pressure of 6.0 bar gauge and a temperature of 160 °C, providing robust margins over the normal operating conditions of 1.0 bar absolute and 45 °C. The structural integrity calculations factor in a 3 mm corrosion allowance for all non-stainless carbon steel support elements. When fully operational and filled with the dense carbamate solution (1,218 kg/m³ reference density), the vessel weighs 4,590 kg.

## **Centrifugal Pump Hydraulics and Efficiency (323P008 A/B)**

The energetic heart of the recirculation node is the Lean Carbamate Pump (323P008 A/B). The plant utilizes a heavy-duty, single-stage, horizontally mounted, overhung centrifugal pump manufactured by KSB AG (Model CPK-CX 32-250). This equipment complies with ISO 5199 and API 610 standards for general refinery and chemical service.

### **Mechanical and Metallurgical Construction**

Handling ammonium carbamate at high velocities poses extreme erosion-corrosion risks. Standard materials passivate poorly under high shear stresses. To combat this, the pump casing, impeller, and wear rings are cast from 1.4581 (a niobium-stabilized austenitic cast steel equivalent to 316Nb), while the highly stressed pump shaft is machined from 1.4462 duplex stainless steel, offering superior yield strength and resistance to chloride stress corrosion cracking. The manufacturer's data sheet strictly mandates that "NO COPPER OR COPPER ALLOY SHALL BE USED" in any wetted or auxiliary components, as ammonia aggressively attacks copper, leading to rapid, catastrophic metallurgical failure.  
A critical vulnerability in handling carbamate is the shaft sealing mechanism. The pump utilizes a mechanical stuffing box with a lantern ring. Due to the propensity of carbamate to form highly abrasive crystals if allowed to dry or cool upon exposure to the atmosphere, a continuous seal flush system is mandatory. The design incorporates an API Plan 32 flushing arrangement. Clean steam condensate at 46 °C is injected into the lantern ring at a maximum pressure of 15 bar absolute. This condensate flush establishes a hydrodynamic barrier, ensuring that only clean water weeps across the seal faces into the atmosphere, while simultaneously flushing any migrating carbamate back into the pump volute.

### **Hydraulic Performance Curves**

The hydraulic behavior of the KSB CPK-CX 32-250 pump dictates the operational flexibility of the entire recirculation node. Fitted with a closed impeller trimmed to a diameter of 219 mm and driven by a 7.5 kW IEC-132S electric motor operating at 2,950 rpm, the pump must consistently overcome the system resistance to inject the fluid into the 4.1 bar Reflux Condenser system.

**Analysis of Performance Dynamics:**

1. **Head-Capacity Curve ($H$-$Q$):** The pump demonstrates a stable, continuously drooping head curve. At a zero-flow condition (shut-off against a closed discharge valve), the impeller generates a maximum deadhead pressure of 74.00 meters of liquid. As the downstream level control valve (LV-323503) opens and flow begins, the head decreases. At the rated design point of $11.0 \\text{ m}^3\\text{/h}$, the pump delivers 56.00 meters of differential head. This 56-meter head successfully bridges the pressure differential between the 1.0 bar Level Tank and the 4.1 bar Reflux Condenser, overcoming all intermediate pipe friction.  
2. **Mechanical Efficiency ($\\eta$):** The efficiency curve rises parabolically from zero at shut-off to a peak (the Best Efficiency Point, or BEP) of approximately 35.00% near a flow rate of $13.0 \\text{ m}^3\\text{/h}$. The rated operating point ($11.0 \\text{ m}^3\\text{/h}$ at 33.30% efficiency) is deliberately positioned slightly to the left of the BEP. In centrifugal pump engineering, sizing the pump such that its normal operating point resides slightly left of the BEP is a conservative strategy; it allows the pump to "run out" to higher flows (up to the maximum allowable limit of $17.0 \\text{ m}^3\\text{/h}$) without a catastrophic drop in efficiency or excessive mechanical vibration if process demands surge.  
3. **Power Consumption ($P$):** The power drawn at the coupling reflects the actual mechanical energy required from the electric motor. At shut-off, $3.50 \\text{ kW}$ is consumed purely through fluid friction, disk friction, and bearing drag. Power scales up smoothly with flow, hitting $6.00 \\text{ kW}$ at the rated point. The maximum power draw for the 219 mm impeller at the end of its curve is $7.20 \\text{ kW}$, which is safely below the driver's $7.5 \\text{ kW}$ rated nameplate, ensuring the motor will not trip on thermal overload under any process condition.  
4. **Net Positive Suction Head (NPSH):** The NPSHr curve defines the minimum absolute pressure required at the pump suction nozzle to prevent the liquid from boiling and causing destructive cavitation. At the rated flow of $11.0 \\text{ m}^3\\text{/h}$, the impeller geometry requires an NPSHr of $1.70 \\text{ meters}$. The Level Tank (323D011) operates at 1.0 bar absolute, which equates to roughly 10 meters of liquid head (depending on exact specific gravity). Combined with the static elevation of the liquid in the tank above the pump centerline, the NPSH Available (NPSHa) is calculated at a highly conservative 11.7 meters. This leaves a robust 10.0-meter safety margin, ensuring that even under severe temperature excursions or rapid pressure drops in the Flash Tank, the pump will remain fully primed and free of cavitation damage.

## **Conclusion**

The 323 low-pressure recirculation cluster—encompassing the Flash Tank Condenser (323E011), Level Tank (323D011), and Lean Carbamate Pump (323P008)—represents a marvel of integrated chemical, mechanical, and control engineering within the Stamicarbon Urea 2000plus™ process. By precisely controlling the thermodynamic environment at 1.0 bar absolute and leveraging a massive 177 m² of cooling surface area, the system effectively condenses and chemically absorbs over 6,300 kg/h of vaporized ammonia and carbon dioxide into a recyclable liquid carbamate stream.  
The mechanical integrity of the cluster is safeguarded through stringent material selections (BC.09 urea-grade stainless steel) and internal fluid dynamic controls, such as vortex breakers and impingement plates. Furthermore, the DCS architecture, utilizing highly responsive pressure and level control loops (PIC-323203 and LIC-323503), maintains strict inventory balances and prevents the dangerous release of unscrubbed gases or the cavitation of mechanical equipment. Ultimately, the KSB centrifugal pumps operate with a vast hydraulic safety margin, returning the lean carbamate to the synthesis loop with high reliability, thereby driving the exceptional raw material efficiency and environmental performance expected of a modern Egyptian fertilizer complex.
