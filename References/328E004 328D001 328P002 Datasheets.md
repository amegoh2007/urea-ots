# **Engineering Analysis of Desorption Section Equipment in Stamicarbon Urea Production: Reflux Condenser, Level Tank, and Reflux Pump**

## **Introduction to the Stamicarbon Desorption and Hydrolysis Process**

In the Stamicarbon carbon dioxide stripping urea production process, the efficient separation and recovery of unconverted ammonia and carbon dioxide are critical for both economic viability and environmental compliance. The commercial synthesis of urea is governed by a chemical equilibrium, resulting in a reactor effluent that contains a mixture of urea, water, and unconverted ammonium carbamate. While the bulk of the unreacted components is removed in the high-pressure stripper and the medium-pressure recirculation stages, the aqueous urea solution routed to the evaporation section inherently contains residual ammonia and carbon dioxide. Furthermore, the stoichiometric water produced as a byproduct of urea dehydration, along with any external wash water, forms a process condensate stream that must be rigorously treated before it can be reused as boiler feed water or discharged to the environment.  
The Desorption and Hydrolysis section (System 328\) is specifically engineered to treat this urea-bearing wastewater. In this section, urea is thermally hydrolyzed back into ammonia and carbon dioxide in the Hydrolyzer (328C003), while a series of desorption columns strip these gases from the liquid phase using low-pressure steam. The overhead vapors from the first desorption column (328C002), which are rich in ammonia, carbon dioxide, and water vapor, must be condensed and returned to the urea synthesis loop to maximize overall feedstock efficiency.  
This comprehensive report provides an exhaustive engineering analysis of three highly integrated components within this overhead recovery system: the Reflux Condenser (328E004), the Reflux Level Tank (328D001), and the Reflux Pump (328P002 A/B). By analyzing process flow diagrams, piping and instrumentation diagrams, distributed control system architecture, and original equipment manufacturer datasheets, this report maps the thermodynamic streams, details the mass and heat transfer phenomena, and evaluates the instrumentation and mechanical design of this critical recovery subsystem.

## **Equipment Analysis: Reflux Condenser (328E004)**

### **Process Role and Fluid Dynamics**

The Reflux Condenser (328E004) serves as the primary thermal sink for the overhead vapors emanating from the first desorption column. Its fundamental operational mandate is to condense the gaseous mixture of ammonia, carbon dioxide, and water vapor into a liquid state, thereby forming a weak, or lean, ammonium carbamate solution. This unit operation is highly complex because it involves multicomponent reactive condensation. As ammonia and carbon dioxide dissolve into the aqueous phase at the gas-liquid interface, they immediately undergo a rapid exothermic chemical reaction to form ammonium carbamate.  
The heat of formation for ammonium carbamate must be continuously and efficiently removed to drive the chemical equilibrium toward the liquid phase, thereby preventing an excessive build-up of non-condensable gases that would inevitably over-pressurize the desorption system. The mass transfer rate in this environment is heavily dependent on the diffusion of carbon dioxide through the vapor film at the gas-liquid interface. By maintaining a low tube-side temperature, the vapor pressure of the solvent at the interface is minimized, creating a steeper concentration gradient that significantly enhances the absorption of the reactant gases.  
To facilitate this reactive condensation, a lean carbamate solution from the flash tank condenser (323E011) is introduced into the shell side of the Reflux Condenser via the lean carbamate pump. Additionally, process condensate can be injected to adjust the water balance and control the concentration of the carbamate formed, ensuring the mixture remains well below its crystallization temperature during transient operations. The fluid dynamics within the shell side are characterized by a turbulent two-phase flow regime, where the continuous condensation of the vapor phase progressively increases the liquid holdup as the mixture descends toward the outlet nozzle.

### **Mechanical Design and Heat Transfer Characteristics**

According to the engineering datasheets, the Reflux Condenser 328E004 is configured as a vertically mounted TEMA Type BEU heat exchanger, featuring a bell head, a single-pass shell, and a U-tube bundle. This specific vertical orientation is highly advantageous for falling-film or submerged condensation regimes, allowing condensed liquids and non-condensable gases to flow co-currently downward into the subsequent Level Tank under the influence of gravity.  
The exchanger is engineered to manage a massive heat duty of 4,357 kW. The mechanical specifications are robust, designed specifically to withstand the highly corrosive nature of ammonium carbamate at elevated temperatures. The shell, which contains the process fluid, has an inside diameter of 1,016 mm and is constructed from Stamicarbon material code BC.09, a specialized 25-22-2 austenitic stainless steel designed to resist severe carbamate corrosion. The design pressure for the shell side is 8.0 bar g with a corresponding design temperature of 165 °C, providing a wide safety margin above the nominal operating conditions.  
The tube side, which circulates the cooling water, consists of 300 U-tubes with an outer diameter of 25.0 mm and a wall thickness of 1.6 mm. This geometry yields a total effective heat exchange surface area of 212.00 square meters, based on an effective straight length of 4,500 mm. The tubes are arranged in a 60-degree triangular pitch of 32 mm, utilizing four tube passes to maximize the cooling water velocity and enhance the internal convective heat transfer coefficient. Six baffles, also constructed of BC.09 stainless steel, are installed on the shell side to direct the multiphase process flow radially across the tube bundle, thereby mitigating flow channeling and maximizing the shell-side heat transfer coefficient.  
The overall heat transfer calculations account for a stringent fouling factor of 0.00034 square meters Kelvin per Watt on the cooling water side. This conservative parameter ensures that the required thermal duty can be met even after prolonged operation in challenging climates, where cooling tower water is frequently subjected to high concentrations of suspended solids, silica dust, and elevated ambient wet bulb temperatures.

### **Instrumentation and Control Loops**

The stable operation of the Reflux Condenser is fundamentally tied to the thermal balance of the entire desorption section. The control architecture relies on modulating the heat removal rate to maintain the correct pressure and temperature profile within the first desorption column.  
The primary control mechanism is the temperature control loop governed by TIC328002, which continuously measures the temperature in the desorption system. Based on the calculated setpoint deviation, this controller modulates the cooling water outlet control valve, designated as TV328002, which is located on the cooling water return line (Nozzle N6, DN 250\) of the condenser. As observed in the distributed control system architecture, when the system requires maximum condensation during high load or operational upset conditions, the controller drives TV328002 to a fully open state. This action maximizes the cooling water flow, drastically increasing the log mean temperature difference across the exchanger and suppressing the vapor pressure of the unreacted ammonia and carbon dioxide.

## **Equipment Analysis: Reflux Level Tank (328D001)**

### **Process Role and Fluid Dynamics**

The Level Tank (Item Tag: 328D001) is a vertical pressure vessel installed directly downstream of the Reflux Condenser. Its primary operational mandate is phase separation. While the condenser operates at a highly efficient condensation rate, a small fraction of the inlet vapor remains in the gas phase. This residual vapor comprises inert gases introduced via passivation air, excess non-condensable ammonia, and trace carbon dioxide. The Level Tank provides the necessary physical volume and fluid residence time to allow these gas bubbles to disengage from the liquid lean carbamate solution via gravity settling.  
The fluid dynamics within the vessel are carefully managed to prevent the re-entrainment of gas into the liquid outlet. The downward superficial velocity of the liquid phase must be strictly maintained below the terminal rise velocity of the smallest gas bubbles intended for separation, a relationship governed by Stokes' Law for multiphase fluid mechanics. Once successfully separated, the off-gas collects in the top dome of the vessel and is continuously evacuated to the flash tank condenser, while the degassed liquid carbamate accumulates at the bottom, establishing a stable and continuous suction head for the downstream Reflux Pumps.

### **Mechanical and Design Specifications**

Designed under the stringent AD-2000 Merkblätter code and fully compliant with Stamicarbon engineering specification 18005, the Level Tank is an extraordinarily robust piece of equipment tailored for corrosive high-pressure service. The vertical cylindrical shell features an inside diameter of 1,684 mm and an outside diameter of 1,700 mm. The tangent-to-tangent height of the cylinder is 1,950 mm, which yields a nominal internal liquid capacity of 19 cubic meters.  
To combat the aggressive corrosive mechanisms of ammonium carbamate, all components in contact with the process fluid, including the shell, heads, nozzle pipes, and internal structures, are constructed from material 1.4306. This specific grade corresponds to Stamicarbon code BB.01, an ultra-low carbon 304L stainless steel that provides exceptional resistance to localized intergranular corrosion and chloride stress corrosion cracking. The vessel is engineered for a structural design pressure of 5.0 bar g and a design temperature of 160 °C, easily accommodating the nominal operating conditions of 2.6 bar a and 57 °C.  
To optimize internal fluid behavior and protect downstream equipment, the vessel is equipped with specialized internals. An impingement plate is positioned near the gas outlet or primary inlet to prevent high-velocity liquid droplets from shattering upon entry. This baffle prevents the direct short-circuiting of the incoming liquid phase into the gas discharge nozzle, which would otherwise result in severe liquid carryover. Furthermore, a vortex breaker is installed directly over the bottom liquid outlet nozzle. As the liquid accelerates toward the 200 mm discharge line, the conservation of angular momentum can cause a free-surface vortex to form. The vortex breaker, typically a rigid cross-baffle assembly, disrupts these tangential velocity fields, preventing a vapor core from extending downward into the suction piping, a phenomenon that would severely cavitate the centrifugal pumps.  
The physical orientation and sizing of the vessel nozzles are critical for managing external piping stress limits and optimizing internal flow dynamics. The primary gas and liquid inlet from the Reflux Condenser connects via a 250 mm Welding End nozzle (N1) oriented at 120 degrees. The non-condensed gases exit through a 150 mm nozzle (N2) at 45 degrees, while the degassed liquid discharges through a 200 mm bottom-center nozzle (N4). External forces and moments generated by thermal expansion, internal pressure, and piping weight are strictly constrained to prevent shell deformation; for instance, nozzle N1 is designed to withstand a maximum vertical force of 11.0 kN and a maximum bending moment of 10.0 kNm. Maintenance access is provided by a 600 mm manhole (N5) equipped with a mechanical davit, located at the 90-degree position.

### **Instrumentation and Control Loops**

The Level Tank serves as an essential hydraulic buffer, isolating the performance of the downstream pumps from the transient vapor-liquid dynamics of the upstream condenser. The instrumentation on this vessel is designed to maintain precise mass balance and pressure stability.  
The liquid level within the tank is indicated and controlled by loop LIC328501. The physical measurement is achieved using a differential pressure Level Transmitter, tagged as LT 328501\. This transmitter connects to the vessel via two 80 mm pad-type flanges, designated N6A and N6B, which are located at the 180-degree orientation on the vertical shell. The upper nozzle (N6B) connects to the low-pressure side of the transmitter to measure the static pressure of the vapor space. The lower nozzle (N6A), positioned near the bottom tangent line of the vessel, connects to the high-pressure side to measure the combined static vapor pressure and the hydrostatic head of the liquid column. The transmitter calculates the differential pressure, which is directly proportional to the height of the liquid carbamate.  
The transmitter sends a continuous analog signal to the Level Indicating Controller (LIC328501) located within the distributed control system. To maintain the level setpoint, the controller modulates the control valve LV328501, which is situated on the discharge line directing a portion of the liquid to the low-pressure carbamate condenser. The control system indicates a highly stable operating liquid level of 50.5 percent under normal parameters. High and low-level alarms are rigidly configured within the logic solver to alert operators to potential flooding incidents or severe pump cavitation risks. Additionally, a local physical level gauge, tagged LI328501, is provided on the vessel to allow field operators to manually verify the electronic readings during startup or troubleshooting.  
The operating pressure of the vapor space must be tightly controlled to prevent undesirable back-pressure from propagating into the desorption column. Pressure controller PIC328202 continuously monitors the tank pressure and actuates the pressure control valve PV328202, which is located on the gas discharge line routing the off-gas to the flash tank condenser. The control interface indicates an active pressure reading of 2.69 bar g, which is approximately 3.7 bar a. This indicates a slight variance from the nominal 2.6 bar a design point, demonstrating the dynamic pressure optimization utilized during actual plant load balancing. A local temperature indicator, reading 63.3 °C in the control interface, monitors the bulk fluid temperature, providing immediate feedback regarding the thermal performance of the upstream Reflux Condenser.

## **Equipment Analysis: Reflux Pump (328P002 A/B)**

### **Process Role and Fluid Dynamics**

The Reflux Pumps, designated as 328P002 A and B and configured as a one-operating, one-standby redundancy pair, serve as the hydraulic heart of the low-pressure recovery loop. These centrifugal pumps extract the boiling lean carbamate solution from the Level Tank and bifurcate the pressurized flow into two critical process paths. A calculated portion of the flow is pumped back to the top tray of the first desorption column at approximately 61 °C. This liquid acts as a cold reflux wash, driving the mass transfer rectification process within the column and strictly limiting the water vapor fraction escaping into the overheads to roughly 46 volume percent. The remainder of the fluid is pumped forward to the low-pressure carbamate condenser (323E003) for subsequent pressurization and eventual reinjection into the high-pressure urea synthesis reactor.  
Pumping hot ammonium carbamate near its boiling point presents a severe hydraulic challenge. If the static pressure at the eye of the pump impeller drops below the vapor pressure of the fluid, the liquid will flash into vapor, causing severe cavitation. The collapse of these vapor bubbles generates micro-jets that lead to catastrophic impeller erosion, mechanical seal failure, and an immediate loss of process flow. Consequently, the elevation of the Level Tank and the internal geometry of the pump are engineered to ensure that the Net Positive Suction Head available (NPSHa) significantly exceeds the pump's required Net Positive Suction Head (NPSHr) with a wide margin of safety.

### **Mechanical and Design Specifications**

According to the API 610 and ISO 5199 pump datasheets, the Reflux Pump is a single-stage, horizontally mounted, overhung centrifugal pump manufactured by KSB, model CPK-CX 40-200. Due to the severe corrosivity of ammonium carbamate, Stamicarbon strictly prohibits the use of any copper or copper alloy materials in the wetted parts of the pump. The pump casing, impeller, and case wear rings are constructed from 1.4581 austenitic stainless steel. The pump shaft is machined from 1.4462 Duplex stainless steel, providing exceptional tensile strength and superior resistance to chloride-induced stress corrosion cracking, which is vital for rotating components transmitting high torque.  
The pump is driven by a 400-volt, 50-hertz, 3-phase IEC-160M electric motor with a rated continuous power output of 11.0 kW. The mechanical power is transmitted from the motor to the pump shaft via an ARPEX NAN 115 flexible coupling, designed to accommodate minor thermal growth and angular misalignment during operation.  
The shaft sealing arrangement is uniquely adapted for carbamate service. Pumping hot carbamate precludes the use of standard mechanical seals because the fluid tends to crystallize immediately upon experiencing minor cooling or pressure drops across the microscopic seal faces. Therefore, the pump is equipped with a traditional stuffing box packing arrangement featuring an integrated lantern ring. To prevent crystallization and subsequent abrasive degradation of the sealing elements, the lantern ring is continuously flushed with high-pressure steam condensate at pressures up to 15 bar and temperatures near 46 °C. This flush creates a clean, pressurized fluid barrier that keeps the packing cool and prevents the abrasive carbamate crystals from scoring the shaft sleeve. The flushing line is extensively instrumented in accordance with API plans, incorporating a Y-type strainer, a flow indicator, and a precision flow regulating valve.

### **Process Flow Streams Mapping**

The pump suction draws directly from the bottom nozzle of the Level Tank, processing the combined fluid of Streams 774 and 775\. The fluid is a lean carbamate solution with a density of 1,098 kg per cubic meter and a dynamic viscosity of 0.8 centipoise.  
The pressurized discharge fluid is represented by Stream 776\. The mass flow rate of this stream is 8,275 kg/h. Due to the kinetic energy imparted by the rotating impeller and converted to pressure in the volute casing, the fluid pressure is elevated from the suction conditions to 4.1 bar a. The thermodynamic state of the fluid is assumed incompressible, so the temperature remains effectively constant at 61 °C. The chemical composition mirrors the suction fluid exactly, containing 47.1 percent water, 29.21 percent ammonia, 23.63 percent carbon dioxide, and 0.06 percent urea by weight.

The pump discharge header is outfitted with multiple control branches to manage the precise split routing of the pressurized fluid. The reflux flow directed back to the desorption column is governed by a flow indicating controller, designated as FIC328404. The control interface shows the automatic control valve on this specific branch currently operating at 30.2 percent open to maintain the required mass transfer reflux ratio inside the column.  
The remainder of the fluid routed forward to the low-pressure carbamate condenser is modulated by the Level Tank’s primary level loop, LIC328501, which actuates the inline control valve LV328501. The pump suction line is also equipped with a local temperature indicator, TI328002, which normally reads between 57 and 61 °C. This instrument allows field operators to monitor for flashing risks in the suction piping by verifying that the fluid temperature remains adequately below the saturation temperature at the local static pressure.

## **Broader System Implications and Operational Synergies**

The interaction between the Reflux Condenser, the Level Tank, and the Reflux Pump represents a tightly coupled thermodynamic and hydraulic system where a minor deviation in one component propagates immediately and forcefully to the others.  
If the cooling water flow to the Reflux Condenser degrades due to biological fouling, scaling, or a utility pump failure, the overall heat removal rate drops precipitously. This thermal deficit directly suppresses the exothermic condensation of ammonia and carbon dioxide. Consequently, the vapor mass fraction entering the Level Tank increases, leading to a rapid and dangerous spike in the vessel's internal pressure. The pressure controller, PIC328202, will forcefully open the gas vent valve, PV328202, to compensate for the pressure build-up. This action sends a massive surge of uncondensed, highly concentrated ammonia and carbon dioxide vapor into the downstream recirculation section. This sudden gaseous load can temporarily overwhelm the low-pressure carbamate condenser, leading to severe process instability that reverberates all the way back to the high-pressure synthesis reactor.  
Conversely, the hydraulic efficacy of the Level Tank is heavily dependent on the proper functioning of the upstream desorption column. If the rectifying column experiences internal flooding or foaming—often caused by excessive vapor velocities or damaged sieve trays—aqueous urea solution can be physically entrained in the overhead vapors. Because urea does not vaporize under these operating conditions, its presence in the Reflux Condenser and the Level Tank is a direct and undeniable indicator of mechanical liquid carryover.  
The accumulation of urea in the lean carbamate loop artificially increases the fluid viscosity and density. This alters the Reflux Pump's hydraulic performance, moving its operating point on the curve, and demands significantly more energy to eventually hydrolyze the recycled urea back in the synthesis section. Industry best practices recommend rigorous analytical monitoring of the urea weight percentage in the Level Tank fluid; values above 0.2 weight percent typically signal the onset of column flooding. In such events, operators are instructed to temporarily raise the system pressure to suppress the physical carryover and stabilize the column hydrodynamics before the pumps begin to cavitate.

## **Conclusions**

The desorption recovery loop within a Stamicarbon urea production facility relies absolutely on the synchronized performance of the Reflux Condenser, the Level Tank, and the Reflux Pump. The Reflux Condenser successfully manages a highly exothermic and reactive phase change, demanding precise temperature control via cooling water modulation to stabilize system pressures and maximize reactant recovery. The Level Tank acts as the crucial mechanical buffer, utilizing specific internal geometries such as vortex breakers and impingement plates, combined with differential pressure level instrumentation, to separate off-gases and protect downstream rotating equipment from catastrophic cavitation. Finally, the Reflux Pump navigates extreme hydraulic and corrosive challenges by employing external condensate flushing on its stuffing box and operating with an over-engineered NPSH margin to continuously route the recovered 29 percent ammonia carbamate solution back into the synthesis and desorption processes. Together, these three highly engineered components ensure the environmental integrity of the plant by capturing volatile compounds from the process wastewater, thereby closing the mass balance loop and optimizing the overall feedstock efficiency of the urea production cycle.
